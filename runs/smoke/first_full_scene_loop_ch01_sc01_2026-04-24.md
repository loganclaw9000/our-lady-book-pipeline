The priest was late. Andrés had knelt nine minutes on the stone — long enough to count the palm-ribs in the roof twice, long enough for the beam of window-light to cross from his left knee to his right. The chapel held eleven men. Sailors, two pilots, a notary from the Governor's house, and one boy who had not yet stopped crying since morning mass. Outside, the harbor of Havana sounded like a workshop being torn down: hawsers, iron, men shouting in three languages.

Father Bartolomé came in from the side door with the pyx against his chest and apologized to no one in particular. His cassock was salt-stained at the hem. He set the host and the small chalice on the rough altar and did not bother to straighten the cloth.

"Kneel," he said, though they were already kneeling.

Andrés had received communion in this chapel eight times since the feast of the Epiphany. He knew the floor. The stone under his right knee was lower than the stone under his left by the width of a thumb — a dip worn by forty years of sailors kneeling before crossings they did not return from. He had made a habit of putting his weaker knee in the dip.

The priest lifted the host. "Corpus Christi."

"Amen."

The wafer was dry and tasted of flour and nothing else. Andrés held it on his tongue until it softened. He tried to feel what he was supposed to feel. He felt the dip under his knee and the harbor noise through the wall and the weight of the rosary the Governor's wife had pressed into his hand at the door — a gift from a woman whose name he could not remember, for a voyage she would not take.

The chalice came. He drank. The wine was watered and warm.

"Ite," Father Bartolomé said. "The tide does not wait for you, and neither do I."

They rose. The boy who had been crying was not crying now; he had the face of someone who had decided something. Andrés envied him that. The notary crossed himself four times — once for each quarter of the compass — and went out first.

Andrés was the last to leave. He touched the altar with two fingers, not in reverence but in accounting, the way a man touches a ledger he has closed. Then he went out into the harbor light.

La Niña de Córdoba lay against the pier with her bow pointing at the channel mouth. She was smaller than Andrés remembered — eighty tons, three masts, a hull patched twice between Seville and here. Diego, the bosun, was already shouting at a man on the foredeck about a fouled line. The tide was running out.

"De Mora." Captain Ordaz came down the gangway with a sheaf of papers held under one arm against the wind. "You took your time."

"The priest was late."

"The priest is always late. You knew that." Ordaz looked at him the way he looked at a sail — not unkindly, but measuring for weakness. "We have the tide for another forty minutes. Stow your chest and find the pilot. He wants to go over the charts for the Yucatán coast before we clear the harbor."

"Yes, Captain."

"De Mora."

Andrés turned.

"Did you eat?"

"I received."

"That is not what I asked."

Andrés had not eaten since the previous evening — the fast before communion — and he realized, standing on the pier, that he was lightheaded. Ordaz read this in his face and, without changing his expression, produced half a ship's biscuit from inside his doublet and handed it over.

"Eat on the way up. The tide does not care about your soul."

Andrés took the biscuit. He glanced once at the chapel — low, stone, palm-thatched, indistinguishable from a storehouse — and climbed the gangway.

The deck of La Niña de Córdoba smelled of tar and fresh bread and the vinegar they used on the water casks. Eleven ships of the fleet rode at anchor in the outer harbor, their pennants pointed west. Somewhere among them, on the flagship, Cortés was doing whatever Cortés did before a tide. Andrés did not know the man. He knew only that by nightfall he would be on open water, chewing a biscuit that tasted of ash, with the body of Christ dissolving slowly into his blood and no way back.

He bit the biscuit. He went to find the pilot.
