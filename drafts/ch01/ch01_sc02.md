Malintzin woke before the sun cleared the stockade. The river was loud, too loud, and she knew already that today was not an ordinary day. Three of the guards had been drunk past moonset. A fourth had stood at the edge of the women's hut until he forgot why he had come. She had lain still in her hammock then, counting his breaths until they slurred and moved away. She had not slept again.

She swung her legs down and stood. The compound smelled of wet ash and yesterday's corn. A hungry dog watched her from the gap beside the kitchen.

The waterhole was two hundred yards off. An older slave had already walked it twice. Malintzin took her gourd and went, because not to go was to be noticed, and to be noticed was the worst thing a slave girl could be in a compound of drunk men. She filled the gourd. She watched her own face break apart on the water and did not wait for it to settle.

Back at the kitchen hut she poured the water into the clay pot. The embers were hot; the smoke stung. Four women worked inside. Three of them she had known for a year and could not have named under torture, slave names were a kindness nobody here offered. The fourth was new. Older. Heavier. A Mayan face, but a Mexica carriage in the shoulders. She gestured, and Malintzin sat.

They husked corn together in silence. The older woman was quick with her knife. Malintzin watched the cut, base, tip, one stroke each, no waste, and matched her pace.

"You are a beautiful girl," the woman said in Mayan.

Malintzin's hands did not stop. A year in this compound had taught her that beautiful, in any language, was a sentence handed down. "Thank you," she said.

"I was once pretty. Not beautiful. Pretty was enough." The woman smiled without looking up. "I am Citlal."

"Star," Malintzin said.

"Your mother taught you Nahuatl, then."

"My father. Before."

Citlal let the word *before* pass without picking at it. Malintzin was grateful. She had not decided yet whether this woman was safe to be grateful to.

They worked through the pile. Citlal's knife slipped once, only once, on a cob whose husk had gone to rot, and she swore softly in a dialect Malintzin did not know. Then she looked up.

"The boy in the field. The tall one, the one the overseer calls Rabbit because he runs."

Malintzin kept her eyes on the corn. "He is well. He works with the men. He has not been beaten this week."

Citlal exhaled, a whole breath, held since who-knew-when. "He is my sister's son."

"You came with him. Last year. The raid."

"Yes."

That was all she said. Malintzin waited for the rest, the shape of the rest was obvious, it always was, a woman like this did not arrive in a compound like this without a story, but Citlal only went back to her knife. The silence was not empty. It was chosen.

It was Malintzin who finally spoke.

"He said, once, that his aunt was a princess."

Citlal's mouth moved, not a smile. "Boys say things."

"Was he lying?"

"He was boasting. It is not the same."

"But."

"But." Citlal set the knife down. She looked at Malintzin for the first time, full, and Malintzin understood that she was being measured. "A girl who asks *but* is a girl who has already decided to know. I will tell you as much as it is useful for you to carry. No more. Carrying is heavy in this place."

Malintzin nodded. The older woman picked the knife back up.

"I was going to be married," Citlal said. "The marriage did not happen. The city did not survive the season in which it was to have happened. That is most of it."

"And the boy."

"Was going to be married also. To my cousin. She is somewhere. Or she is not." Citlal shrugged, a Mexica shrug, compact, finished. "I do not ask after her. Asking wears a thing down."

Malintzin thought about this. Outside, a man shouted at a dog and the dog did not answer. The embers under the pot had gone white at the edges.

"If anything happens to me," Citlal said, quietly, as if it were a further observation about the corn, "the boy will need a friend in this compound. Someone who speaks Nahuatl to him without making it a favor. I am asking."

Malintzin did not answer for a long moment. She finished her cob. She reached for the next.

"I speak Nahuatl and Mayan and a little of the coast tongue," she said. "I have not been asked for anything in a year that was not a taking. I will be his friend."

Citlal nodded once and did not thank her. Thanking, Malintzin was beginning to understand, was also heavy.
