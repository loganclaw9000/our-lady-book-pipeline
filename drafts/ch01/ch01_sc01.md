The chapel smelled as it had when he was a boy — incense, damp stone, beeswax, the old sweat of the pews. Fifteen years since he had last knelt in Havana, and the smell had not changed. He had not expected to come back. He had not expected to come back to anything.

Andrés de Mora stopped in the doorway. The sun was setting over the bay. Light struck the stained glass at a shallow angle and laid the colors flat across the flagstones — red at his left boot, blue where the aisle began. A breeze off the water moved the candle at the altar, and the shadow of the saint above it trembled on the wall.

Outside, eleven ships rode at anchor at San Cristóbal de la Habana. They had been gathering for weeks, not all in one place — the captain-general had moved between Santiago, Trinidad, and here, recruiting where he could, buying cassava, arguing with Velázquez's man. The last ship had come in from Trinidad three days ago. Antón de Alaminos, the pilot, kept to his own vessel and would not drink with the soldiers. Departure was fixed for tomorrow — the fourth time it had been fixed.

Andrés crossed himself and walked up the aisle.

He had come for quiet. He knelt at the prie-dieu, put his hands on the wood, and tried to pray. Nothing came.

A man stood at the altar with his back turned. A priest, by the robe — though the cut was old and the hem was wet, as if he had walked through the tide. Andrés had not heard him enter.

When the priest turned, his face stayed in shadow — the candle was behind him.

"You are leaving on the fleet tomorrow," the priest said. His Spanish carried an accent Andrés could not place — not Castilian, not Andalusian, not the clipped speech of the islands.

"Yes, Father."

"Then make your confession."

"I have not come to confess."

"No," the priest said. "But you will."

Andrés said nothing. His mother's rosary — fifteen years in a leather pouch on his belt — pressed against his hip.

"You were in Cuba with Velázquez," the priest said. It was not a question.

"In 1513. Yes."

"At Caonao."

Andrés looked up. He had not said the name aloud in six years. Not to the priest at Santiago, not to the woman he almost married in Baracoa, not to himself in the dark of any ship. The name had stayed inside his teeth.

"Yes, Father."

"Tell me what you did there."

He had rehearsed this in his head a hundred times and never once out loud. He found he could not begin with the village. He began with the boy.

"There was a boy," Andrés said. "Ten, maybe eleven. He ran when we came down into the square. I ran after him. I did not have to — he had no weapon, he was a boy — but I ran after him and put my sword through his back under the left shoulder blade. He fell into a doorway. I stepped over him to get to the next man. I did not go back to see if he lived. I think he did not."

The priest said nothing.

"His mother was in the doorway," Andrés said. "I did not see her until I had stepped over her son. She did not make a sound. I have thought about that many times — that she did not make a sound. I do not know her name. I did not learn his."

"What else."

"Afterwards we took the women. I did not take one. I stood in the square and watched the others take them and I did nothing, and I have told myself for six years that this is the same as not doing it. It is not the same."

"What else."

"My brother Diego wrote to me from Sevilla two years after. He asked if the stories were true. I wrote back that they were exaggerations. He died believing me. Fever, in '14."

The priest let the silence settle.

"And now," he said, "you sail with Cortés, who was the governor's secretary at Caonao and wrote the report that called it a pacification."

"Yes, Father."

"Why do you go."

Andrés had not been asked this. Not by Cortés, who had not cared. Not by the recruiter, who had cared only that he could ride. Not by himself.

"Because I do not know what else I am for."

The priest stepped forward. His face came into the candlelight for one moment and Andrés saw that he was younger than he should have been, and that his eyes were the gray of the bay at dawn, and that there was no cross on his chest, only a plain cord.

"Hear your penance," the priest said. "On the mainland you will meet the boy's mother again. You will not know her. When the hour comes you will have a choice. Make it as if Diego were watching."

"Father — "

"Amen."

The priest placed a hand on Andrés's shoulder. The weight was ordinary. He went back to the altar and knelt, and did not look up again.

Andrés stood. His knees hurt. He walked to the door and stopped in the frame.

The bay was dark now. The ships were lanterns on black water. Tomorrow, the Yucatán.

He put the rosary back into its pouch and went down to the boats.
