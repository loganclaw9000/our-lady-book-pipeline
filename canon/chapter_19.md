The workshop smelled of pine sap and sweat and iron. Adzed timbers lay in neat piles, grain still glistening with oil from the axe blades. Tlaxcalan carpenters and Spanish woodsmen worked side by side, each language a separate current in the same river, neither yielding. Andrés watched them a moment, then turned away. He disliked the smell of cut pine. It reminded him of the forests around Granada, and Granada was a place he tried not to visit in his thoughts.

*San Esteban del Río* stood twenty yards from the workshop, a refitted second-class brought up from Veracruz on Cortés's orders. Taller than *La Niña de Córdoba* had been, broader in the shoulders, the armor plates etched with the Calatrava cross in a way that seemed deliberate, almost aggressive. The engine was a man, an adult, a soldier, *La Niña* had been a child. The arm bone that powered it belonged to a Castilian martyr of the Reconquista, a knight killed at Granada in 1491. The adorator who had brought the Reliquary up the road had told him so, with the reverence one gives a thing that has been waiting for a purpose.

The adorator was a Franciscan named Fray Diego, a man whose face Andrés had not yet learned. He stood before the cockpit, holding a silver crucifix in his left hand and a small vial of oil in his right. He looked at Andrés and nodded once, and the movement was a permission.

Andrés walked to the engine. He kept his eyes from the Tlaxcalan watching from across the yard. He had marked her the moment he stepped from the compound, a woman with her arm in a sling, standing with the kind of stillness only pilots learned. She was watching him. She was watching the Reliquary. Not the carpenters, nor the brigantine timbers, nor the priests blessing the work. The engine.

Her name was unknown to him. No introduction had been offered, in Tlaxcala or anywhere since the retreat, except for the few Tlaxcalan nobles who had come to negotiate the alliance terms. He had not asked. She had not asked. Her presence in the yard was a fact, like the smell of pine sap, or the way the light fell across the engine's armor at this hour.

He climbed into the cockpit. The interior sat smaller than *La Niña*'s, tighter around the shoulders, the seating position canted a little forward. The monstrance was bolted into the center of the chest plate, and the arm bone inside it was larger than *La Niña*'s, a man's bone, a soldier's bone. Fray Diego knelt beside the cockpit and began the introduction-rite, naming Andrés to the saint and the saint to Andrés. The Latin was unfamiliar. Not the Latin of the Spanish expeditions. The Latin of the Reconquista, older and harder, a liturgy for a different war.

When the rite ended, the hum began. It sat wrong against Andrés's spine. *San Esteban*'s pitch was a third lower than *La Niña*'s, the cadence more military, the presence in the cockpit abrasive in a way that was not malice but was also not welcome. The saint was not his. The engine resented him; or the saint resented him; or he resented the substitution and was reading the hum through that resentment. He could not tell.

He walked *San Esteban* through three calibration patterns under the adorator's eye. The gait went stable by the second pattern, movements precise and uncomplaining, the armor plates singing against each other in a way that was not the singing of *La Niña*. He dismounted. He said nothing of the wrong hum. The adorator could hear it too. He had heard it. He had said nothing either.

The Tlaxcalan woman had not moved from her place in the yard. She had watched him climb into the cockpit. She had watched the rite. She had watched the calibration patterns. She had watched him dismount. She did not speak to him. He did not speak to her. Two pilots without an introduction, standing in a workshop yard where carpenters were building brigantines that would be carried in pieces to the lakeshore for the siege of Tenochtitlan. The siege was being machined into existence in the background of this calm. Andrés knew it. She knew it. Neither said anything.

He walked away from the Reliquary, eyes forward. The hum followed him, a third lower than *La Niña*'s, a cadence that was not his, a presence that was not his. He would have to learn to walk with it. How, he could not yet say.

He walked past the timbers. Past the carpenters. Past the woman who was still watching. The pine smell came up again, Granada, the knight at the wall, 1491, and he refused it the way one refuses a drink one cannot afford to take. The hum stayed with him a step behind, a beat off his own gait, the wrong saint pacing the wrong man.

He would. That was the thing that wanted saying, and it was the only thing. The siege was being machined into existence in the background; he was being machined into existence in the foreground. The brigantine pieces would meet at the lake. The wrong hum would meet his marrow. By spring there would be no daylight between him and the saint, or there would be no him at all to argue the point. Either outcome was a calibration.

At the edge of the yard he stopped. He turned, once, against his own intention. The Tlaxcalan was no longer looking at the engine. She was looking at him. Their eyes met across forty feet of pine shavings and unblessed ground. Neither moved. Then she touched the sling at her elbow, a small adjustment, nothing said, and turned back toward the workshop, and the moment closed.

Andrés walked on. The hum walked with him. The smell of pine sap and sweat and iron stayed in the cloth of his coat, and that night, undressing, he would not know whether to wash it out or keep it.

The hum of *San Esteban del Río* sat a third lower than *La Niña*'s had, a tone like the bottom of a cracked bell, registered in the sternum before the ear. Four months Andrés had piloted it. Four months and the engine still felt borrowed.

It was borrowed. Refitted second-class, arm bone of a Castilian knight from the Reconquista, liturgy in Mozarabic Latin he had to relearn line by line. The saint was not his. The hum was not his. The cradle the Tlaxcalan carpenters had built around it smelled of pine resin and cedar smoke and a third thing he could not name, something the local workshops did to the wood that the Spanish workshops had not done. *La Niña* had chosen him in a clearing on the road to Cempoala, when he was twenty-three and stupid with prayer. *San Esteban* had been chosen for him by a quartermaster's list.

The brigantines were being shaped in pieces, in a long workshop that ran the length of three Tlaxcalan houses with their walls knocked out. Carpenters argued in two languages that did not agree on the meaning of a joint. One man would say *mortise* and another would point at something that was clearly a tenon, and a third would say a Nahuatl word for *the way rain enters a roof badly*, and the argument would be settled by a Spaniard with a gauge and a Tlaxcalan with a knife, and the piece would come out true. They were building thirteen. The pieces would be dragged over the Paso de Cortés in spring. Andrés had stopped trying to count.

He walked from the workshops to the headquarters compound with his eyes on the ground, the way a man walks when he does not want to be recognized. The captains had recognized him at the council. The nobles negotiating the alliance had recognized him. The priests blessing the brigantine pieces had recognized him. Pilots are recognized by their engines, and his engine was being recognized by everyone. He had not yet learned how to make this one his.

The council had been in the main hall, around a table dragged in from a Tlaxcalan house and too small for the men sitting at it. Cortés at one end, Malintzin at his right hand, captains and priests and nobles ranged down the sides, and the man who was not supposed to be there.

Don Fernando Ixtlilxochitl.

He had defected during the regroup in October, brought three engines of his own, Texcocan-pattern Tezcatlipoca-class variants, second-class, all named, all with their *teōmachtiani* still aboard, and a retinue of nobles, and a standing offer of fifty thousand warriors over the course of the coming campaign. He had been baptized. He went by Don Fernando now. His Spanish was better than Andrés's Latin. He was charming. He was easy to underestimate. He was not easy to underestimate. The two facts sat in Andrés's chest without resolving.

Ixtlilxochitl sat at the table with the ease of a man who had been sitting at tables all his life. He spoke with the cadence of a man who had been speaking at tables all his life. He calibrated each Spaniard to within half a degree, Cortés first, Sandoval second, Olid third, Alvarado last and most carefully, and he held the room without raising his voice. He had been preparing for this campaign in his head for years. He did not know that Andrés knew it. Andrés knew it, could not say how, and filed the un-articulated distrust under a heading he did not yet have a name for.

The council was about everything. About the brigantines. About the calendar. About the men. About the engines and the gods and the saints and the arithmetic of an army moving against a city on a lake. Ixtlilxochitl was the arithmetic. He was the only man at the table who could do it cleanly, who knew which causeway took weight in the rainy season, which altepetl had how many warriors and which ones owed which other ones what, which Mexica garrison had which engine cradled where. He laid it out without raising his voice and the room listened. He was not supposed to be there. He was there.

When the council ended Andrés walked out without looking up. Ixtlilxochitl did not look at him either. They did not speak. They did not need to. They knew each other in the way enemies know each other before they have a word for it, and by spring one of them would be dead, and Andrés did not know which.

He walked to Bernardo's lodging.

It was a borrowed Tlaxcalan house at the end of a side lane, a single room, a small writing table, a tallow candle, a sleeping mat rolled against the wall. Bernardo sat at the table with a Nahuatl primer Malintzin had drawn out for him and a sheet of bark paper covered in his attempts. He wrote slowly. He wrote wrong, then crossed it out, then wrote it right. He had been at this since the regroup. He was rebuilding his vocation in a new language, and was the only man Andrés knew who was, and the only man Andrés had decided he could test a heresy on.

Bernardo looked up. He saw the look on Andrés's face. He set the pen down without capping it. He did not ask. He did not need to ask.

He said the sentence in Nahuatl first, accented, effortful, every consonant placed by hand. Then in Spanish, plain. Then in the Mozarabic Latin he had learned in Sevilla as a novice. Three languages. One sentence. He did not whisper. He did not raise his voice. He tested it the way a man tests a beam by leaning on it.

The sentence was the heresy. The Spanish were not winning because God was with them. They were winning because they cheated. Powder and steel, yes. Reliquaries that outweighed and outranged what they met, yes. Tlaxcalan allies bought by old enmities a hundred years deep, yes. And rumors he had heard from a muleteer down from the coast about a sickness moving inland, faces and bodies covered in something the muleteer had no word for, whole villages emptied. He did not name the sickness yet. He named the rest.

He finished the sentence in Latin and sat with his hands flat on the table.

Andrés said nothing for a long time.

He did not refute it. He could not. He thought of *La Niña* sinking under the gilding the conquistadors had hammered into her face plates against her saint's express will, drowned by her own ornament. He thought of Cholula, the dormant Quetzalcoatl shrine, the smell of that day. He thought of Alvarado in the Templo Mayor courtyard. He thought of the Tlaxcalan engines outside the workshop right now, *Matlalcueitl* among them, fueled the way they had always been fueled, serving the strangers who had baptized them last week. He thought of the *ixiptla* pipeline and what it took to keep it running and what it took to break it.

He did not affirm the heresy either. He could not. To affirm it was to say that the splinter of Medellín wood inside *La Niña*'s monstrance had been a piece of carpentry and nothing else, and he had felt her tilt her head when he prayed at the cradle on the third day out of Cempoala, and the cedar timbers had groaned, and that was not nothing. He could not call it nothing. He would not.

So he registered the sentence. He registered that he could not refute it. He registered that he could not affirm it. He registered that the registering was itself a kind of betrayal, of the saint, or of the man across the table, or of both, and sat with the betrayal because there was nowhere to set it down.

After a long while he said, in Spanish: *Say it again.*

Bernardo said it again.

Andrés said: *In Latin.*

Bernardo said it in Latin.

Andrés took the pen from where Bernardo had set it down. He drew the cap back over the nib. He laid the pen across the page so that it pinned the sentence Bernardo had been writing, a Nahuatl conjugation, third-person plural, the verb for *to carry*, and he said: *Once more. The Nahuatl.*

Bernardo said it in Nahuatl. The candle guttered, recovered, held.

Andrés said: *I will not tell anyone.*

Bernardo said: *I know.*

They sat like that until the candle ran low. Outside, the hammers had stopped, the carpenters had gone to their suppers, and in the silence the hum of *San Esteban del Río* in his cradle two streets over reached them, that lower third, the cracked-bell tone, lodged behind the breastbone. Andrés noticed it the way a man notices his own breath when someone has asked him to hold it. He noticed that he had stopped resenting the engine for the length of one sentence in three languages, and that he would resent it again by morning, and that this was the shape of the next four months, small reprieves bought at the cost of something he did not yet have a name for.

He stood. Bernardo stood.

At the door Andrés said: *Don Fernando was at the council today.*

Bernardo said: *I heard.*

Andrés said: *He frightens me, and I do not know why.*

Bernardo did not answer at once. He blew the candle out and they stood together in the dark of the small room. Then he said, in the careful effortful Nahuatl he had been building for four months: *Because he is doing the same arithmetic you are. From the other side of the table.*

Andrés walked back to his quarters with his eyes on the ground. The hum followed him the whole way.

The hammers had stopped an hour ago. Cortés's council chamber sat in the gap they left, a repurposed Tlaxcalan house, cedar smoke gone cold in the rafters, the table too small for the men sitting at it. Cortés at the far end. Aguilar to his right. Malintzin at his left, as she had been since autumn, six or seven months along now, the pregnancy visible and not hidden, the Spanish having absorbed it into the camp's ambient knowledge without comment.

Andrés stood by the door. *San Esteban del Río*'s wrong hum was still resident under his collarbone from the morning's walk-rite, a third lower than *La Niña*'s had been, a cracked-bell tone four months old now and not yet his. He was on duty as a junior pilot in attendance. He had been watching Malintzin translate for two years; he could read her now better than he could read his own confessor.

The nobleman they brought in was alone, in civilian clothes, a Mexica. The pickets had sent word up the chain. The room had closed around him in the way a room closes around something that is not supposed to be there.

He sat across from Cortés. He gave his name: Itzcoatl, of Texcoco, pilot of *Obsidian Mirror*, the Tezcatlipoca-class engine the Spanish knew by reputation from the Templo Mayor and from Otumba, where it had harassed them on the right flank. Andrés had heard the name *Obsidian Mirror* in operational briefings. He attached the man across the table to the engine in his head and found the sum unstable.

The man was calmer than Andrés would be in his place.

Andrés watched Malintzin translate. Something in her cadence stuttered, not in the rate, not in the volume, but in the small held breath she took before rendering one of Itzcoatl's sentences into Spanish, a half-second longer than her habit. Andrés noted it. He did not know what triggered it.

Itzcoatl's sentence, as Malintzin delivered it into Castilian, was a lineage statement: he named a sister, Xochitl Tlapala, widowed at the massacre, of a Mexica house in Tenochtitlan. The name passed through the room and registered, with Cortés, only as one more datum of standing. It registered with Andrés as a name he had never heard. With Malintzin it registered as something else. Andrés could see only the evidences. Her hand went briefly still on the table edge. She finished the sentence in Castilian exactly as a translator should.

The negotiation proceeded. Preliminaries only. Nothing decided. Itzcoatl would return in some weeks with *Mirror* or he would not. The meeting ended.

As the chamber emptied Andrés saw Malintzin walk, not toward Cortés for debrief, but down a short corridor toward the household staff rooms. She did not return that hour.

Late that night Andrés went to Bernardo's lodging, they had meant to continue last week's conversation about the Reconquista martyrs, and Malintzin was already there, sitting at Bernardo's table.

Andrés had not seen them in private together before.

He withdrew without speaking. The door pulled to behind him. Bernardo's hand raised in a half-acknowledgment that meant *another night.*

Andrés walked back to his quarters under the cold sky and registered, the way he had been registering things for months without articulating them, that there were conversations now happening among the people he loved that he was not party to, and that whatever had just been spoken in that room was going to load-bear something in the months ahead.

*San Esteban*'s wrong hum was the only company he had on the walk back. A third lower than *La Niña*'s. Cracked-bell. The saint who was not his, pacing the man who was not hers. By spring there would be no daylight between him and the saint, or there would be no him at all to argue the point. Either outcome was a calibration. He had told himself this for four months and the telling had stopped helping somewhere around January.

He went inside. He sat. He did not pray. The hum was the prayer, and it was wrong.

Outside, the hammers stayed stopped. The carpenters had gone to their suppers hours ago. The brigantines were being shaped in pieces, in a long workshop that ran the length of three Tlaxcalan houses with their walls knocked out. The pieces would be dragged over the Paso de Cortés in spring. The siege was being machined into existence in the background of this calm. Andrés was being machined into existence in the foreground.

He noticed, sitting there, that he had stopped resenting the engine for the length of one sentence in three languages, for the length of Itzcoatl's lineage statement, when the room had been holding still around something he could not name, and that he would resent it again by morning. This was the shape of the next four months. Small reprieves bought at the cost of something he did not yet have a name for.

He lay down. He did not sleep. The hum stayed with him a step behind, a beat off his own gait, lodged behind the breastbone the way a man notices his own breath when someone has asked him to hold it.

Whatever had just been spoken in that room, whatever name Malintzin had carried out down the corridor and into Bernardo's lodging, was already loading the spring. He did not know its shape. He would learn its shape by being inside it.

He would. That was the thing that wanted saying, and it was the only thing. He would. By spring he would be either the man who could walk with the wrong hum, or the man who could not. He did not know which. He would see.

The hum kept its step behind him, a beat off, and he let it.
