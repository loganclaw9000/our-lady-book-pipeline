Dawn at Centla. The tide had gone out in the night and left the sand wet and dark for forty yards before the dry line, and *La Niña de Córdoba* stood in the dry line with her halberd grounded and her monstrance breathing.

She was fifty feet of gilded plate at the shoulder. The hum had been with Andrés since Havana, a low constant note under the bone of his sternum, the saint's pulse through the cradle of the cockpit, but it had grown on the crossing, and now, with the cordage of his prayer fitted again to her, it was no longer one note. It was the room of the chapel at Havana, the bishop's hand on his head, the viaticum wafer dissolving on a tongue that had not held the host in five years. It was Flora's name unspoken. Finger, tooth, small rib, behind crystal at the center of his chest. He could feel them as he could feel his own ribs.

He had written his mother a second letter the afternoon before sailing. He had not told her about the lift. He had told her about the weather.

Now there was the beach, and the smoke of cookfires further inland where the Maya town stood, and at the edge of the palm-line the shapes that had been mistaken for shrines until they moved. Six of them. Squat. Wide at the haunch. Carved in the round with jaguar-rosettes. The plate was older than anything he had fought in Iberia, older than the Mamluk calligraphy he had cut through at Granada when he was twenty-three, and the proportions were wrong to his eye, the legs too thick, the chest too low, no monstrance visible anywhere on their bodies. Where a Reliquary kept her saint at the heart, these kept something he could not name. The category he had was *not Moorish.* It did not help.

Cortés's voice came over the speaking-horn from *San Cristóbal del Viento*, two hundred yards down the line. *Adorators. In the name of God and the Emperor. Forward.*

The halberd came up. The hum took the shape of a girl's voice. Not words, Flora had never been given words; in eight years she had only ever been a pressure and a cleanness. He had been good at this since he was fifteen. He was twenty-six now, and the Order had told him, gently and without superstition, that pilots did not often see thirty.

*La Niña de Córdoba* went forward at a walk and the wet sand took the print of her feet eight inches deep.

The first Maya engine met him at the high-tide line. It came faster than its proportions promised, a low charge, head down, the jaguar-shoulder lifted to take his halberd-stroke on the bone of its plate. He stepped inside the line of the charge. He had been trained at ten. He had killed his first Moor at twenty-three outside Granada in a vineyard where the vines had not yet been pruned for the year, and the man inside that engine had been screaming the *shahada* through a viewport, and Andrés had heard it, and had still driven the spike home, because the Order forbade thoughts of glory and reward but had never forbidden hearing.

Through this viewport, now, he saw a woman.

She was perhaps thirty. Her face was painted in two colors he could not name. She was looking at him as he was looking at her, and she did not scream, and she did not pray that he could hear, and her engine was already half-turned to bring its short forearm-blade across his greave. He had a fraction of a heartbeat. The hum at his sternum did not change. Flora made no sound. He brought the halberd over his head in the two-handed stroke the Order taught for armored apex work, *the heart-chamber, brothers; only the heart-chamber; brute force on the plate is futile*, and he drove the spike down through the jaguar-rosette at the center of her engine's chest.

The plate gave. He felt it give the way a cooper's barrel gives at the last hoop. Something inside the chest broke that was not metal and not wood, something that had been alive in the way a banked coal is alive, and the engine stopped mid-step with one foot still raised, and the woman in the viewport was no longer looking at him. She was looking at the ceiling of her own cockpit. Her mouth was open. Then it closed.

The engine fell sideways and the impact went up through *La Niña de Córdoba's* arms and into Andrés's shoulders, and he felt the halberd-haft in his palms and he felt nothing else.

The hum at his sternum had gone quiet.

Not absent. Quiet. The way a room is quiet after a child has stopped crying and you do not know yet whether she has fallen asleep or whether she has simply decided not to call for you anymore. He had prayed Flora's short life into the machine that morning, the way he had prayed it before every engagement for eight years, *born in 837, refused to recant, killed at thirteen by men who asked once and then asked again*, and the saint had taken the prayer the way she always did, and now, with a Maya woman's chest opened on the sand, the saint was not answering.

He bent forward inside the cockpit and he was sick into the brass basin under the seat. He was sick a second time. The basin was cold against his forehead.

Down the line, *San Cristóbal del Viento* was still moving. The other Maya engines were still standing. The battle had not, in any sense that a captain would recognize, begun.

Andrés straightened. He wiped his mouth on the inside of his vambrace. He looked through the viewport at the fallen jaguar-engine and at the woman inside it whose name he would not learn, and he thought, without permission, against the Order's most explicit instruction, *she was somebody's.*

Then he thought: *so was I.*

The hum came back. Not a girl's voice. A pressure. A cleanness. He raised the halberd. *La Niña de Córdoba* went forward into the second.

The salt-flat at Centla took the weight of *La Niña de Córdoba* and gave back nothing, no echo, no purchase, only the slow sink of sixty tons of gilded saint into mud the color of old blood.

Mid-morning. March 25. The tide was low and the river had pulled its skirts back from the flat by perhaps two hundred paces, leaving a glaze of brine and rotted weed that the engines had to walk through to reach each other.

Andrés stood inside the cockpit-shrine and breathed through his teeth. The monstrance at his sternum was warm, not hot, and the hum was there, the way it was always there, a tone below hearing that lived in the bones of his jaw. The child was quiet. Holding. He had killed once already this morning, in the first hour, and the child had not asked him about it.

He did not have words yet for what that silence meant.

To his right, *La Niña*'s halberd dragged a long furrow through the mud. To his left, the second-class *Nuestra Señora del Campeón* moved in formation at his elbow, two paces back and two paces wide, carrying a war-pick rather than a halberd because its pilot, Alonso, had small hands and preferred a shorter haft. Behind them came the third-class infantry, eleven, twelve engines of perhaps thirty feet each, *San Millán* and *San Isidoro* and others Andrés could not pick out by their plating from this angle. They moved in a loose wedge.

Across the flat, the Maya engines came on.

There were six. They were squat, that was the first thing, before anything else: squat, no more than thirty-five feet at the shoulder, and they walked low, with their weight forward over their knees in a way that no Spanish engine could manage without falling. Their plating was bone-pale and carved in panels that suggested jaguar, the spotted shoulder, the heavy haunch, the wide flat skull, and where the Reliquaries carried a monstrance at the heart, these carried something Andrés could not yet name, a sealed stone vessel set in a cradle of cord and copper, low in the belly.

The hum in his jaw did not change at the sight of them. It should have. Against Moors, the hum had always sharpened, *not Moorish* was the only theological category the Order had given him for these, and the category did not work. The child stayed quiet. He could not tell whether that was good.

The first impact was not his. *Santiago del Paso* on the far right took a Maya engine in the shoulder with its lance and the lance bound, went in clean and would not come out, and the Maya engine and Santiago staggered together for three steps like two drunks holding each other up before the Maya pilot drove a stone-bladed mace into Santiago's hip-joint and the lance came free with a wet crack.

Andrés saw all this from a hundred paces away in the corner of his left vision-slit and filed it without thinking. The shape of the battle was already inscribing itself in him, the Spanish line was longer than the Maya line, but the Maya engines were faster than they looked, and the mud was working against the Spanish more than the Maya, because the Maya engines were lower and broader and their feet were broader still.

The second jaguar came at him.

He brought *La Niña*'s halberd up, across the body, blade-up, the way an *adorador* was taught from the first year, and the Maya engine drove its shoulder into the haft. The shock went through his harness and into his spine and the cockpit-shrine's lamp swung once on its chain.

*Combat-bind.* The Maya engine's left hand came up and locked on *La Niña*'s gauntlet, and *La Niña*'s left came up and locked on the Maya engine's wrist, and they were bound, two saints' worth of weight against whatever the stone vessel held, neither giving ground, both sinking inch by inch into the salt-mud. He could hear the suction of it under their feet. He could hear, somewhere very far away, the child humming, still quiet, still holding.

He did not kill it.

That was the decision he made in the cockpit-shrine in the half-second before he made it, not a thought, a movement: he was not going to drive the halberd into the heart-chamber of this one, because he had done that already this morning to another and the child had not asked him about it and he did not want to find out yet what would happen if the child asked.

So he killed the knee instead.

He let the bind go a half-inch, gave the Maya engine the inch of give it was pulling for, and as the weight came forward, he brought the halberd's fluke down and across and into the back of the Maya engine's right knee. Not the heart-chamber. The knee. The fluke went through plating that turned out to be thinner there than at the shoulder, and the joint folded, and the Maya engine went down on one greave with a sound like a barrel of nails dropped from a wall.

It was not killed. It was disabled. It would not walk again on this field, but its pilot was alive, and the stone vessel in its belly was unbreached, and that mattered to Andrés in a way that he did not examine yet.

He stepped back. *La Niña*'s left foot came out of the mud with a sucking noise that he felt in his molars. He pivoted to find the next.

The battle ran for ninety minutes by the timepiece *Campeón*'s pilot kept on his belt, Andrés learned this later. Inside the cockpit-shrine he had no time, only the count of binds. He took two more. The third was harder than the second; he caught a stone mace on his halberd's haft and the haft bent, bent, not broke, which was its own small miracle, and he drove the bent halberd butt-first into the Maya engine's face-plate hard enough to crack a panel, and the Maya engine stepped back and another Spanish engine, *San Cristóbal del Viento*, came in over Andrés's shoulder and finished it. He was glad it was not him. He was tired. The hum in his jaw had not sharpened, not once.

By the time the sun was high, fifth hour of the day, he learned later, this also from *Campeón*'s pilot, three of the Maya engines were withdrawing in good order toward the palm-line at the flat's far edge, walking backward in a staggered line so that any one of them was always covering the other two. They did not run. They did not break formation. The smallest of them carried the disabled engine's pilot on its back, lifted out of the wreck before the Spanish could reach the cockpit. Two Maya engines lay broken on the flat. One, the one Andrés had taken in the knee, sat on its single greave with its hands open at its sides, and its pilot stepped down out of its belly into Spanish hands without resistance and without expression.

Captured. The sixth, Andrés did not see, it had gone somewhere on the left flank.

He let the halberd drop. The bend in the haft was a hand's-breadth deflection above the head; the Order's armorer would straighten it tonight or replace it tomorrow. *La Niña* stood in mud to her shins and did not lean. The monstrance at his sternum was warm, not hot. The child had not spoken once the entire morning.

He had been told, at his investiture, that the child would speak when the killing was wrong. He had also been told that the child would speak when the killing was right. He had been a boy then. He had not asked which.

He stood in the cockpit-shrine of *La Niña de Córdoba* in the salt-flat at Centla and listened to a quiet that the Order had not taught him to read. The work was done. The work had been the work. He was not the man who had knelt at Havana. He was a man who had now done it twice in one morning, and would do it again, and the child would or would not speak, and either way *La Niña* would walk forward.

He turned her toward the camp. The mud held, and then released, and then held.
