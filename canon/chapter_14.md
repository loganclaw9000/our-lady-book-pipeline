The rain stopped before the fighting did. From the doorway of the Totonac chieftain's compound, Malintzin counted four engine hums in the dark, La Niña's first, low and even at the bottom of her throat, then three others bracing around her in the sharper Spanish key. A fifth voice, foreign to her ear, climbed and broke. A Narváez engine taking a pike to the knee joint. The hum dropped a full tone, then thinned to the wet metal sound of a Reliquary going down on one knee in the rain. After that the fight was bookkeeping.

The compound stood at the edge of the temple precinct, where the wounded would be brought. That had been arranged. That had been part of what she had arranged. She held a clay bowl of water she had not yet given to anyone. The torches burned in the rain at half their height, and the smoke smelled of wet copal and burnt cotton wick. The first stretchers came up the path under torchlight, Spanish wounded carried by Spanish, then Spanish wounded with Narváez's badge already cut from their shoulders by the men who carried them. The badges were coming off in the dark, on the path, before the men reached the compound. By the time the wounded were inside, they were Cortés's.

Under an hour. She had told herself it would take three. Most of Narváez's captains had been bought weeks before, she had translated the buying, carried the figures, named the towns where the silver waited. Eight hundred pesos to one captain. Twelve hundred to another. A house in Veracruz to a third, who had a wife there and an older one in Spain. The rank-and-file, hearing in the dark that their officers had gone over, set down their pikes the way men set down a tool at the end of a long day. Narváez himself had lost an eye to a pike. He was alive. He would be useful as a humiliated thing.

By full light the courtyard had become a column. Cortés moved through it without armor, in a stained shirt that had been white the day before, speaking in the clean low voice he used when he had already won and wanted no one to forget it. The bought captains were brought to him one at a time. He took each by the elbow rather than the hand, a small adjustment, a man's gesture for another man, easier than pretending to embrace. The new pilots stood by their cradled engines on the far side of the compound, awaiting introduction. La Niña de Córdoba sat among them at her full fifty-five feet, gilding dulled by the rain, the small glass box dark against her chest. Andrés was not in her. He was on the ground somewhere, sleeping or praying. Malintzin did not look for him.

She walked. There was no errand. The bowl of water went to a Totonac boy with a cut along his jaw, and then her hands were empty and she kept walking. Men spoke as she passed and she heard the new orders already in their mouths, the names of the towns they would garrison, the marching order for the road back, the share each man would draw from the silver shipment. The orders were three weeks old. They had been written into the bribes. She had been the pen. She had not, until this morning, stopped to look at the pen and notice it was warm in the hand.

The man passed her between two cooking fires. A black freedman, Francisco de Eguía, listed on Narváez's roll as a servant and now on Cortés's roll as the same. He was sweating in the cool dawn, the kind of sweat that beads at the temple and runs without being wiped, and he walked carefully, as a man walks when his joints have begun to ache and he does not yet know why. She glanced at him once. The glance did not catch. He carried something in his blood that would take a city, and in a year would take more than one, but on this morning he was only a tired man going to find water. She did not look at him twice. No one did. The path he took crossed the path of three bought captains and one of Cortés's pages, and the morning recorded each crossing the same way it recorded the rain still dripping from the eaves, as nothing, as weather.

By midday the column was on the road south.

She rode in the second wagon, behind the supply train and ahead of the wounded. The road had been cut by their own boots three weeks earlier and was already healing under new grass. She took the obsidian mirror from the pouch at her hip. Xochitl had pressed it into her hand in Tenochtitlan two months ago, polished black stone the size of a flattened plum, edged in worked silver, the back carved with a small Tezcatlipoca glyph the width of a thumbnail. *Look at yourself sometimes,* Xochitl had said. *Otherwise you forget that you exist.* Malintzin had not used it. She had carried it because Xochitl had given it.

She held it up now in the rocking shade of the wagon's canopy. Her own face came back at her dim and steady, the obsidian giving her skin the color of a bruise that had not yet decided what color it would be. She looked at the line of her jaw, at her mouth, at the small tightness under her eyes that had not been there in Centla a year ago. She looked at the place where the stone caught a thread of dawn light and turned it into a thin line across her cheekbone. Then she lowered the mirror two hand-spans and held it angled against the slight rise of her belly and watched the light fall and not fall.

She had known for eleven days. She had not said the word to herself in any of her languages. She said it now, in Nahuatl, under her breath, where it belonged. Then in Maya, because Maya was the language she had been a slave in for a year, and the word needed to hear itself in that one too. Then she did not say it in Spanish. Spanish was Andrés's language and Aguilar's language and Cortés's language. She did not yet wish to give them this. The child, when it came, would inherit whichever language she chose to raise it in, and that choice was hers, and she had not yet made it.

The wagon went over a root and the mirror jumped in her hand. She caught it before it struck the plank. She set it flat against her thigh and held it there.

Three weeks of translation had bought nine hundred men. Nine hundred men, plus the four Reliquaries already on the road, plus the Tlaxcalan auxiliaries waiting at the pass, would walk her back into Tenochtitlan, and the city she walked back into would be a city she had decided. Not chosen. *Chosen* was a word for women with options. *Decided.* The word in Nahuatl was the same word used for a judge passing sentence and for a midwife cutting a cord, and it did not flinch from either use. She tested it in her mouth. It fit. It had been waiting.

She had translated, until tonight, in order to live. From this morning forward she would translate in order to move things. She did not yet know what she would move. She knew the hand was hers, and she knew she would not be giving it back.

She put the mirror in the pouch and kept her hand on the pouch. The wagon kept its pace. Somewhere four wagons back, Francisco de Eguía was lying down in clean straw because his head hurt and he could not say why, and the column carried him south the way a river carries a seed.
