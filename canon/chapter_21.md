The chant came in two voices, neither of them his mother's.

He heard them from the outer courtyard before he saw the masks, the cotton, the obsidian, two physicians the calpulli had sent because the calmecac no longer had men to send. Counterpoint, slow, on a Tezcatlipoca formula she had taught him as a child. He was thirty paces from her and they were already singing her into the dark.

He had ridden from Tenochtitlan in two days. *Obsidian Mirror* sat in its cradle by the lake shore at the edge of the compound, hum low, an apprentice he did not know polishing the disc on its chest plate. The engine was the last thing in the household that was working. He had not stopped to look at it.

Inside the inner courtyard the smell hit him before the sight, sweat, vomit, the sour rot of pustules opening on skin that had been clean a week before. His mother lay on a mat under the colonnade. Her face was a face he had known his whole life, pitted now in a pattern he had learned to read on others first: three days of fever, then the rash, then the black collapse. She had reached the collapse. The pustules on her throat wept a thin dark fluid into the cotton at her neck.

He knelt. His knees met the mat and the weight of the ride came down on him at once, calf to hip to spine.

Her eyes opened. Glass, clouded, but they saw him. She said his name in the household register, the easy one, the one she had used to call him in for the evening meal. Then she shifted into the high priestly idiom she had not spoken to him since he was small, the archaic Tezcatlipoca cadence dense with formulae from codices he had never been taught. He understood maybe two sentences in three.

The third sentence, the last one, he lost entirely. A verb he could not place. A case ending that hung in the air. She did not finish it. She breathed once more and then the breath stopped.

He held her until the physicians touched his shoulder. The colder of the two said the bones would be ready in nine days. He nodded. He did not stand.

A runner reached the compound at dusk. Cuitláhuac was dead. The man who had taken the throne after Moctezuma had taken it for eighty days and was gone. Same disease. Same afternoon. The runner's hands shook on the cup of water Itzcoatl handed him, not from cold. Itzcoatl told him to eat and to sleep in the outer room and did not warn him about the apprentice's hands.

He walked out to the lake shore alone.

The water was the color it always was in winter, brackish, gray, the long line of the Albarradón cutting the horizon to the west. Beyond the causeways the Spanish camp was a smudge of smoke he could see if he knew where to look. The Spanish were immune. They did not know why. He knew why. Something had arrived in their blood, generations ago, in a country he would never see, and they carried it the way a man carries a name. The disease was not an arrow. It was not a spear. It was a thing they had brought and not aimed, and the moral gradient of that was worse than anything he had seen in war. War you answered. You took your dead and sharpened the obsidian and you went out again. This he could not answer. He could not even see it coming.

*Obsidian Mirror* hummed in its cradle. The apprentice, sixteen at most, younger than Itzcoatl had been at his first deployment, looked up and made the small bow proper to a pilot returning to his engine. His knuckles were red. The first pustules had risen on the back of his right hand. Itzcoatl saw them and did not say what he saw. The boy would be dead inside the week. Telling him would change nothing except the boy's last days, and Itzcoatl had nothing to give him in exchange for that knowledge.

He thought of the corps. He thought of the pipeline. The Templo Mayor ground was already months behind on its quotas after Alvarado; now the priests who tended the bones were dying on the same mats as the *ixiptla* they had been preparing. Mexica engines across the valley were going dormant for lack of fuel. His mother's bones, when the priests finished with them, would go into a Tezcatlipoca engine. He did not know which one. He might be told. He might not.

His brother in the calmecac had died eight days ago. A cousin in the household had died the day before. The servants had buried both with the household elders and then the household elders themselves had taken sick. Inside the space of a fortnight the house was gone. Everything he had refused to defect for, the lineage, the obligation, the ground his grandfather had cleared, was already in the ground or on its way to it. Ixtlilxochitl had said *you will come when you come*. Itzcoatl had hated the certainty in that sentence. He understood now that the certainty was not arrogance. It was arithmetic.

Xochitl was in Tenochtitlan. The city was sealed. Runners were not crossing. He did not know if she was alive. He did not know if Tepin and Citlalli were alive. He could write a letter and the letter would not move.

He stood at the shore until the cold worked into his shoulders. Then he walked back through the colonnade to the inner courtyard and did not enter the room where his mother lay. The priests would prepare her. He sat against the wall opposite the door and watched the braziers smoke and tried, for the third time that day, to reconstruct her last sentence, the verb, the case ending, the formula it belonged to. He could not. He had been a Tezcatlipoca pilot for eleven years and he could not finish his mother's sentence.

In the morning he would ride for Tenochtitlan. Cuauhtémoc, young and defiant, would be raised. The Mexica would fight. He understood, sitting there, that they would lose, not because the Spanish were stronger but because the fuel was dead, and the last thing his house might have been defending was already gone.

He would go anyway. There was nothing else to do with a body that still moved.

Copal smoke pushed under the door of the inner room and pooled along the courtyard tiles. The priests had been at it since dawn. Itzcoatl knelt on his mother's mat and listened.

He had not slept. The chant was a Tezcatlipoca funeral cadence, he had heard it at his grandfather's, his grandmother's, his father's. He knew the cadence. He did not know the words. His mother had known the words. She had sung them at the funerals of her own parents in this same courtyard, and Itzcoatl, eight years old then, had listened as he listened now and understood nothing of the language but the shape of the grief carried in it.

The priests had come on the first day with cotton and obsidian blades. They sang the formulae over her body. They told him the bones would be ready by the ninth day. They left without looking at the boy who was her son, meaning him, kneeling in a courtyard, a grown man taken back to a child by the cadence of a chant he could not parse.

On the second day he had sent a runner to Tenochtitlan. To Xochitl. To his sister, who would want to know.

The runner had come back at dawn of the third day with his face the color of wet ash. He stood at the gate and would not come into the compound. Itzcoatl had to walk out to him. The runner's hands shook. His message was brief because the cousin who had sent it had been brief: Xochitl Tlapala died three days after you left. Her children died within the same week. The cousin did not give the names of the children. The cousin did not know whether they were buried or whether the burial party had simply piled them with the others by the Tlatelolco wall. The cousin's own house had lost six in the past week. The cousin himself was not yet sick but was waiting.

The gates of Tenochtitlan were shut. The city had sealed itself against the contagion. Itzcoatl could not go in to claim her. He could not stand the rites. He could not confirm by his own eyes that his mother was dead and that his sister was dead and that her children, Tepin who had carried the scar across his temple, Citlalli who was not yet four, were dead.

He thanked the runner. He sent him to the kitchen to be fed. He walked out alone through the eastern gate of the compound and down the path to the lake shore.

It was midday and the lake was the color of lead and very still. Across the basin Tenochtitlan sat low on the water, a pale geometry of temples and rooftops with smoke rising from it in a dozen places, funeral fires, slow and continuous, the smoke not in columns but in a flat haze that did not rise, that hung over the city like a second water. The smoke marked a city that had lost a quarter of its people in six weeks. The smoke marked a city that had lost its *ixiptla* pipeline, its priests, its engine corps, its fuel. The smoke marked a city that would, by the time the siege came, be a shell.

He stood on the shore and made himself look at it for a long time. He had grown up looking at this city from this shore. As a boy he had stood here with his sister and named the temples to her, Huitzilopochtli's pyramid there, Tlaloc's beside it, Quetzalcoatl's small round shrine off to one side. She had been four years younger than him and slow to learn the names and quick to learn everything else. The girl on the shore who had memorized the temples by their colors was now a body somewhere inside a sealed city, and her two small bodies beside hers, and there was no one left in his line to name temples to.

His mother had taught him an exercise, when he was small, that her own father had taught her. The Texcocan philosopher's exercise: name the thing you do not want to name. She had used it, she said, to keep the unnameable from becoming a tyrant inside her. He had used it himself on the plain at Otumba, five months back, with a Spanish lance going by his face and his hands not yet steady on the controls. Then he had named *I am beginning to choose*. Standing now on the shore he tried the exercise again and the word came up and he let it through.

*I have no house.*

He stood with it. He let it sit in the air between him and the lake. It did not become smaller for being named but it stopped being able to ambush him from behind.

He turned and walked back through the compound to where *Obsidian Mirror* stood in her cradle.

She had been moved out into the open courtyard for the funeral, which was an old custom, the engine of the dead pilot's house attended the rites by being present, by being looked at, by being acknowledged. Tezcatlipoca engines passed through houses, not individuals; she had come to Itzcoatl when the previous pilot of his line had died and he was old enough to take the cradle. She had been working for thirty years. She would work for thirty more if the bones in her chest were kept fresh. Forty-eight feet of obsidian skin and matte ceramic plate, and the polished disc of the smoking-mirror motif at her chest, and she was humming, as she always hummed at rest, half a step below the note that meant alert.

Itzcoatl walked to her feet and put his ungloved hand on the obsidian of her left greave. The afternoon sun had warmed the surface. The warmth went through his palm into his wrist into his blood. The hum did not change. It had not changed when his mother died and would not change when he died and would not change when the city across the water finished dying. The hum was the hum of an engine, not a mourner. He had needed to know that. He stood there for a long time and let the hum be what it was.

Then he computed.

The arithmetic was simple and the answer had been waiting for him on the shore. His mother was dead. His sister was dead. Her two small ones were dead. The lineage he had been refusing to defect for, the obligation, the ground his Texcocan grandfather had cleared, the house he had been told was his to keep, was dead of a disease the Spanish had brought and had not meant to bring. The moral gradient he had clung to in Tlaxcala when he sat across from Cortés and gave only preliminaries was dead with it. He had been defending a shell. He had thought it was a wall.

He stood at *Mirror*'s feet and let that arithmetic finish. He did not weep. He did not pray. He did not curse the Spanish, who would not have understood the curse and did not deserve the dignity of being its target. The disease was not a Spanish weapon. The disease was a thing that had arrived in the bodies of men who themselves did not know they carried it. There was no enemy at the right scale for the grief. There was only the lake and the smoke and the engine humming and the body still standing.

The decision the arithmetic produced was not a decision to go to Tenochtitlan. He could not go. The gates were shut. The decision was smaller and harder. It was to sit on his mother's mat and wait for the ninth day and the bones and the song he did not know the words to. It was to pilot *Mirror* into Tenochtitlan when the gates opened, which they would, which they would have to, when Cuauhtémoc, newly raised, young, defiant, called the engines to the defense. It was to fight a war he now knew the Mexica would lose, because their fuel was dead and the Spanish were immune and there was no arithmetic in which the city survived. It was to defend a shell because the shell was the last thing his hand could be laid on.

It was, in the end, what remained to do with a body that still moved.

The sun moved a hand's width across the lake. He took his hand off *Mirror*'s greave. The obsidian was already cooling where his palm had been. He turned and walked back across the courtyard, past the door of the inner room where the priests were still singing in two voices, and sat down on his mother's mat to wait for the ninth day.

The first thing was the silence in his boots. He had walked the engine cradles a dozen times in his life and his soles had always come back loud, the low constant hum of consecrated obsidian, the faint shiver a pilot read through his heels before he read it through his ears. Today the obsidian gave back nothing. Cold stone. Cold mud. A breath of wind off the lake.

Itzcoatl rode the shoreline on a horse borrowed from the compound stable, not Obsidian Mirror. The engine sat in its cradle in Texcoco, hum low, an apprentice he did not know polishing the disc on its chest plate with a rag that had once been a shirt. The boy's knuckles were red. The first pustules were rising on the back of his hand. Itzcoatl had seen the same pattern on the boy's cousin, who was dead by the time the funeral pyre burned itself out. He had not said anything to the apprentice. The boy would be dead inside the week. Telling him would change nothing except the boy's last days, and Itzcoatl had nothing to give him in exchange for that knowledge.

He rode as far as the sealed causeway permitted, then turned inland to loop around the forward cradles. The staging had been set up the previous summer, when the Templo Mayor's hum first began to stutter after Alvarado's massacre. The *ixiptla* pipeline had been wounded then, priests and *ixiptla* alike killed in the plaza, and the priesthood had sent engines out to the valley margins to wait for fuel that was not coming fast enough. Through the autumn the fuel had come slowly. Through the winter it had stopped.

The first cradle was empty.

Itzcoatl dismounted and walked to the edge of the pit. The engine that had sat here, a Huitzilopochtli-caste field engine named *Sun-That-Answers*, was gone. It had been moved, he knew, not abandoned. The priests had taken it back to the city when the staging was no longer defensible. The cradle itself was an open pit with a ramp, lined with obsidian chips to keep the engine's feet from sinking into the mud. The obsidian was stained with the residue of years of consecration, a thin black film that no amount of scrubbing would remove. He set his palm to it. The film was cold.

The second cradle held an engine that was still in its pit, but it was not humming.

Itzcoatl walked closer. The engine was a Tezcatlipoca, thirty feet tall, obsidian skin dull with the film of inactivity. The smoking-mirror disc on its chest was clouded, not polished, and the mirror-mask face was slack, the way a face goes slack when the pilot inside it is asleep. Two attendants stood at the ramp with nothing to do. One of them was a boy, maybe sixteen, whose face was already pitted in the early pattern of the rash. The other was older, maybe thirty, chewing a stalk of something, a habit he had picked up in the last few months because there was nothing else to do with his mouth.

Itzcoatl stopped five paces from the ramp. The older attendant looked up.

"The pilot died," the attendant said. "Four days ago. The boy was preparing to take over. He took sick yesterday."

Itzcoatl nodded. "Where did you move the Huitzilopochtli?"

"Back to the city. The Great Engine needs bones."

"And the boy."

"Will be dead inside the week. He knows it."

Itzcoatl looked at the boy. The boy was looking at the engine, and his face had the flat look of a man who has already accepted something he cannot change.

"Take him to the kitchens," Itzcoatl said. "Feed him. Let him eat until he cannot eat anymore."

The older attendant bowed. "Thank you, *teōmachtiani*."

Itzcoatl did not correct him. He was not a *teōmachtiani* of this engine. He was a pilot of another, and he was here because the engine corps was dying and he wanted to see it die with his own eyes.

He walked the rest of the line. The third cradle was empty. The fourth held another Tezcatlipoca, silent, attended by a boy with pustules on his hands. The fifth was half-built and abandoned, the ramp interrupted mid-course, the obsidian chips piled where the carriers had stopped, no one working on the lay. A pair of crows had begun to use the unset stones for shelter. The wind moved their feathers, not them.

The pipeline was dead.

He stood at the lip of the fifth pit and made himself name what he had come to see. Five cradles. One engine functional and absent. One engine present and dormant. One pit empty by design. One Tezcatlipoca asleep with no pilot ready to wake it. One construction abandoned in place. The corps Cuauhtémoc was about to be raised over was not a corps. It was a series of holes in the mud with crows in them.

He thought, for the first time without flinching from the thought, of what Ixtlilxochitl had brought to Cortés the previous October. Three engines. Texcocan nobles. Fifty thousand warriors offered. A new name, baptismal water, a church his cousin would fund out of stone yet unquarried. Itzcoatl had heard the report in Tenochtitlan and felt, then, only the heat of the betrayal. He felt now what was underneath the heat. Ixtlilxochitl had not chosen the winning side because it was winning. He had chosen which kind of surviving Texcoco would exist on the other side of the ruin, and he had made the choice early enough that the choosing was still his to make.

The choosing was no longer Itzcoatl's the way it had been Ixtlilxochitl's. The Mexica corps would fight. He would fight beside it. Cuauhtémoc would be raised today, and the body of a young man with no fuel to draw on would carry the weight of an empire that had already been hollowed from the inside. Itzcoatl would stand at the installation. He would bow. He would ride out beside *Sun-That-Answers* when the priests woke it for the next causeway sortie, and the sortie would happen, and Mexica men would die in it, and he would die with them or he would not.

But there would be an after. The after was not a question he had been allowed to ask while his sister and her children were still inside the city walls. He could ask it now. The answer would not arrive today. It would not arrive in a month. It would arrive on a road, or at a table in a borrowed room with paper walls, or across a brazier from a cousin who would not gloat and would not press.

A bell rang from the staging ground, the small bronze the priests used to call the corps to witness an installation. Cuauhtémoc was being lifted to the mat. The sound carried thin across the marshes, a clean shape that the cradles around him refused to answer. He listened until it stopped. He took his hand off the cradle stone.

He turned the horse toward the staging ground. He did not look at the lake.
