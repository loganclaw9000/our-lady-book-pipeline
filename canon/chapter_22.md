The room had two dimensions and a window. The window was shuttered but light came through the slats anyway, the way it does with all windows in this country. She sat at the table with her notebook open. The pages were cheap paper, the kind the Spanish brought in reams, and they drank the ink like they drank everything else.

Names. Place-words. Two phrases of ecclesiastical Latin that refused to sit in her mouth right: *verbum caro factum est*, *in principio erat verbum*. She wrote them down and then wrote them again, because the shapes of the letters were different from the sounds, and the sounds were different from the meanings, and she had to hold all three of them in her head at once.

Her belly was under her hand as she wrote. Not big. Maybe four months. A shape inside a shape. She did not touch it. She had not touched it in days, because the gesture felt like prayer and she had stopped praying somewhere between Cholula and the Noche Triste.

The Tlaxcalan women in the yard were weaving. The sound of the loom came through the shutters, rhythmic, the same sound she had heard in every village from Potonchán to Texcoco. She could have walked out into the yard and spoken to them. They would have spoken back. They would not have asked her about the Spanish. They would not have asked her about the war. She would have gone with them.

She did not.

The notebook lay open in front of her. On the left page: *Alvarado. Pedro de. Captain. Handsome. Killed in battle? No. Lives. Will kill again.* On the right page: *Tecpatl. Obsidian knife. Also: date. Also: sacrifice. Also: time. All four meanings are the same word. The Spanish have four words for this. They do not understand.*

That was the thing, the thing she had carried away from Bernardo's table, the night she had set his Latin confession beside the Mexica priest's Nahuatl account and understood, finally, that the two machines were the same machine.

She had wanted to walk away after that night. She had wanted to go to Tlaxcala proper, to find her people, to let the pregnancy be the reason no one questioned her. She was pregnant. That was a fact. She could have said: I am pregnant. I am going home. And someone would have sheltered her. Someone would have fed her. Someone would have told her stories about the child who would grow up to be something other than what she was.

She did not.

The loom clicked. Then clicked again. The sound was a rhythm she could have joined. She could have stood up and walked out. The door was not locked. No one was watching.

She stayed.

The choice was not heroic. It was arithmetic. If she stayed, she would be in the room when the sentences were written. She would be in the room when the place names were decided, when the verbs were chosen, when the history was made. And in the room, an awake translator could soften some of the sentences. Could preserve some of the names. Could make a Spanish verb stand in for a Nahuatl idiom in a way that kept the meaning alive in the record, in the paper, in the transcription that would outlive the burning.

None of it saved the city. The city was going to burn. She knew that now. The engines, the fuel, the pipeline, the smallpox. It was a finished thing. The siege ahead was the period at the end of the sentence. She was not hoping. Hope was a luxury for people who did not know the arithmetic.

She was choosing.

The hand on her belly moved. Not a kick. A shift. A settling. The child inside was the ground-state fact of her life now, the thing she carried that made every choice a choice for someone else. She did not think of it as a blessing. She did not think of it as a curse. It was a fact, the way the pregnancy was a fact, the way the war was a fact, the way the machine was a fact.

The women in the yard stopped weaving for a moment. Someone laughed. The sound came through the shutters and went on.

She wrote the Latin phrase again. *In principio erat verbum*. In the beginning was the word. She wrote it and then she wrote it again, in Nahuatl, because the Nahuatl version was longer and the longer version was the true one. *In nahuac in iuhqui in tlamati*. In the speech was the speech.

She turned to a fresh sheet and began the morning's actual work, the brief Cortés had asked for before the column moved, names of the towns the army would pass on the way to the lake. Iztapalapa. Mexicaltzingo. Coyoacan. She wrote each one twice, Castilian on the left, Nahuatl on the right, because she had learned that a place name written in only one language was a place name half-erased. The scribes who came after her would copy the Castilian and forget the rest. She made it harder for them to forget.

Footsteps in the corridor. She knew the cadence before the knock. Andrés. He came in without being asked, the way he had come in for a month now, and stood at the doorway with his hands at his sides. He had given up trying to be invited.

He looked at the notebook. He did not look at her belly. He had stopped looking at her belly two weeks ago, when it had become a thing they would not discuss, Do you believe you are doing the right thing, he said.

The Castilian came out careful, the way it did when he had rehearsed the sentence. He had not rehearsed the question. He had rehearsed the way he would ask it, so that it did not sound like an accusation.

She set the quill down. She thought about it. Not for show, she actually thought about it, because the question deserved an answer and she did not want to give him a phrase she had used before, I believe I am doing a thing, she said.

He did not answer. His face did the thing it did when a sentence landed in him and he did not know yet what shape it would take. He had been a priest for twelve years. He could sit with a sentence.

After a moment he nodded. He did not say anything else. He left.

The door clicked shut. The loom in the yard kept its rhythm. The child inside her settled lower against the bone of her hip, the way it had begun to do in the last week, and she let the hand stay where it was for a count of three before she moved it back to the page.

She wrote *Iztapalapa* in Nahuatl and underlined the *t-l* because the Spanish scribes always wrote it as a cluster and it was not a cluster, it was one consonant, lateralized, a sound Castilian did not have a letter for. She wrote a small note in the margin explaining this. The note would be ignored. She wrote it anyway.

Outside, the loom changed pattern, a different weft, a different beat. One of the women began singing. The song was in a Tlaxcalan dialect she could mostly follow, and the chorus was about a girl who walked to the river and did not come back. The girl in the song was not unhappy. She had simply chosen the river.

Malintzin listened to one verse. Then she went back to the brief.

The light through the shutter had moved. It was no longer striping the table; it had climbed the wall and was striping the plaster behind her. By the time the brief was finished it would be on the ceiling. By dusk the room would be dark and one of the women would bring her a clay lamp, and she would work by it for another two hours, and then she would sleep.

She was twenty years old. She would be twenty-one before the city fell. The child would be born after.

The choice was made. The arithmetic was done. The brief had nineteen place names left to translate and the column moved at first light.

She picked up the quill.

The child turned. Once, twice, then settled at an angle that pressed up under her ribs on the left side. She let it. She had stopped trying to coax the position by shifting her own weight; the child made its decisions inside her without consulting hers, the way the Spanish camp did, the way the war did.

She stood at the open end of the long workshop. Three houses had been knocked through to make it, the lime walls broken at shoulder height and the rubble cleared, so that the building ran sixty paces from end to end and held the ribs of thirteen brigantines in numbered piles. The numbers were charcoaled onto the timbers in Spanish script. The Tlaxcalan carpenters had begun adding their own marks beneath, in a glyph script that named the joint, not the hull. No one had told them to stop. No one had told them to continue. They simply did it, and the Spanish gauge passed over both marks and certified the cut true.

She had been at the yard since the hour after sunrise. Two hours, perhaps. The child shifted again, slow this time, then settled. Seven months, the captain's wife had said when she had last looked at her. Seven, maybe a little less. The Spanish camp had absorbed the pregnancy the way it absorbed weather. No one mentioned it. Cortés had not spoken of the child since the night he had laid a hand on her belly, briefly, in the dark, and then withdrawn it as though he had touched a thing that was not his to claim.

The porters came past in their line. Each man carried a beam on his right shoulder, lashed at two points with rawhide. The lashings were Tlaxcalan; the rawhide was Spanish, shaved from cattle that had survived the crossing. The beams were pine, and the pitch on them stuck to her hand when she touched the cut end of the nearest pile, sticky and bright, smelling of the lower slopes of La Malinche, the mountain that shared her baptismal name and would not let her forget it. The porters went past, twenty paces apart. At the far end of the building the hammers struck in pairs, one Spanish, one Tlaxcalan, the second always a half-beat late. She did not count the lag. She had counted it on prior mornings and the number had not changed.

Everything in the yard kept time with everything else. This was the part she had not expected. A war was being machined into existence around her, and the work of it sounded like a household.

She had decided in February. Not the way a woman decides to take a path through a market, by weighing two routes and picking the shorter one, but the way an animal decides to lie down in a particular patch of grass: by being the kind of animal that lies there. She could leave. She could stay. If she left, the war would happen without her, and the men in the room when the city fell would be men who did not speak Nahuatl and did not know what a *teōmachtiani* was and would mistranslate every surrender into a slaughter. If she stayed, the war would happen with her, and some fraction of what was salvageable would be salvaged, and she would be in the room. The number of people whose lives turned on her being in the room was not large. It was not zero.

She had stayed.

She heard him before she saw him. Andrés walked with a slight drag of his right boot now, ever since Otumba, a cut in the leather where a Tlaxcalan lance had grazed him without breaking skin, but the boot remembered. He came to a stop a pace away and did not greet her. He had stopped greeting her in February, the same week she had stopped weeping at night, and neither of them had remarked on either change.

He looked at the porters. Not at her. Not at the belly.

*Do you believe,* he said, in Spanish, the words placed carefully, the way he placed them when he had walked through the sentence in his head before speaking it aloud, *that you are doing the right thing?*

The hammers struck. The lag held.

She considered the question the way she considered a Nahuatl word for which Castilian had no equivalent. There was a verb her father had used, she had not heard it spoken since she was nine, that meant the act of standing where you had to stand when the thing you could not stop arrived. Not resisting. Not collaborating. Standing. Knowing what the standing was. Choosing it again at every breath.

She did not try to render the verb. He would not hear it correctly, and the rendering would do the verb harm.

*I believe,* she said, *I am doing a thing.*

He did not answer. His face did the thing it did when a sentence settled into him and he had not yet found its dimensions. Twelve years of priesthood. He could sit with a sentence the way a *teōmachtiani* could sit inside a god.

They watched the porters together. The cradle for the foremast of the third brigantine was nearly finished. Two Spaniards and four Tlaxcalans were tying the final lash, four hands per knot, two voices arguing in alternating syllables about whether the angle wanted another quarter-turn. The master carpenter held his thumb against the string and listened.

She knew what Andrés had not said. He had not said: *the child you are carrying is the child of the man whose orders fed La Niña de Córdoba.* He had not said: *that man's orders will, in some month I cannot yet name, fall on me, and on the engine I am pretending to pilot, and on someone I have not yet failed to save.* He had not said: *I am asking you the question because I am rehearsing it for myself.*

He did not have to say any of it. She heard the shape of the unsaid sentences in the breath he took before the asked one, and in the half-step his right foot made when he shifted his weight. She had been reading men's silences for eight years. His was not a hard one to read.

She did not say what she had not said either. *The child will be born in August. The siege will begin in May. I have counted the weeks. The child will be born inside the falling city, or inside a city that has just fallen, and in either case the name on the baptismal record will be a Spanish name and the language of the room will be Castilian, and I have made my peace with each of these in a different week.* She did not say it because saying it would have required him to take a position, and his position was already taken, and the saying would have made him pay twice.

The cradle was tied off. The master carpenter nodded once. The porters dispersed without ceremony to fetch the next pair of beams. Somewhere behind the workshop, a loom started up, a single shuttle, then two, then four, picking up the rhythm of the hammers and adding a higher register to it.

She let her hand rest on the curve under her tunic for a count of three. Then she moved it back to her side. He saw the count. He did not name it.

*The brigantines go over the pass in eight days,* he said.

*I know.*

*I am not going with the carry party. They have asked me to stay with the engine until the lakeshore camp is set.*

*I know.*

He nodded. The drag in his right boot eased as he settled his weight. They stood for another length of time she did not measure. When he left, he did not look back, and she did not watch him go. The porters returned with the next beam. The hammers found their lag again. The loom multiplied behind the wall.

She stayed at the open end of the workshop until the sun moved a hand's breadth across the floorboards. Then she went back to the residency through the narrow street, and the women at the doorways looked at her belly with the distant recognition she had stopped flinching from, and she did not flinch.

In her room she opened the Nahuatl primer she was drawing for Bernardo, dipped the reed, and wrote the next entry. The verb her father had used. She wrote it in the glyph script first, then in the Latin alphabet beneath, then a Castilian gloss, and then, because Bernardo would ask, and because the gloss was insufficient, a sentence of her own in Castilian explaining what the verb did not mean.

She did not write what it did mean. He would have to find that himself.

The loom went on past dusk.

The chant of the porters carried up from the rear of the column in the half-cadence they used on a grade, two beats of breath, one of step, the rawhide creaking against pine on the offbeat. The road climbed. The dust climbed with it.

It was not a road the way Spanish roads back in Spain were roads, not a thing built but a thing worn, the top of a ridge worn by the feet of porters for a thousand years, then widened by the backs of mules, then crushed to dust by the iron-shod hooves of Spanish cavalry that had been moving over it for six months. The dust was fine. It rose in a haze that hung a foot above the ground and made every figure in the column look like it was floating.

Malintzin rode forward near the front, on a mule that had been given to her because of the child and that she had accepted, not because she could not have walked. She had walked from Potonchán to Tlaxcala in the year before. This was a different kind of walk. The child inside her was the weight that mattered, not the miles.

The column stretched back behind her like a snake that had forgotten how to hide itself. The brigantine pieces came first, on the backs of perhaps eight hundred Tlaxcalan porters, each man carrying a beam or a plank or a coil of rope on his right shoulder, lashed at two points, the lashings rawhide, the beams pine. The porters moved in pairs, a Spanish overseer with a whip in his right hand walking between them, the whip used more as a baton than as a weapon, a thing to point with, to signal, to threaten, not to strike. The Tlaxcalans did not look at the overseers. They looked at the ground.

Behind the brigantine train came the Spanish cavalry, the pikemen, the arquebusiers, the cannon pieces on the wooden carriages, the mules that pulled the cannon pieces. Behind the Spanish came the Tlaxcalan warriors, flanking on both sides of the road, not in the column, in the brush and the scrub, watching the column, watching the porters, watching the overseers, watching everything.

The Reliquaries were spaced along the line, three of them visible to her: *San Esteban del Río* a quarter-mile ahead, the newest of the Spanish engines, its cockpit designated for Andrés and empty, the gilded monstrance at the top of the head catching the sun and flaring; *Saint James of the Pass* two miles back with Sandoval at its heart, eleven stories of stately iron walking at a pace it did not need to break, the apostle's finger in the chest-monstrance bending the air around the column even at that distance; and a third-class engine she had not been told the name of, perhaps a saint's tooth, perhaps a knuckle, moving with the cavalry. The engines were spaced so that no section of the column was more than a mile from a standing iron giant, because the road was a kill-zone.

The road crested.

For the first time in six months, the horizon fell away. The valley of the lakes was there. Not the whole basin, just the north edge, the gray water of Lake Texcoco visible through the haze, a rectangle of color the size of a province, and beyond it, in the haze, the shape of the city, a smudge of stone and thatch and the smoke from the cooking fires, the smoke rising in straight lines because the wind was not yet moving.

The smoke was the wrong kind. It was not the smoke of a city that was cooking its morning meal. It was the smoke of a city that had already been burned.

She had known this would be the view. She had known it for months. She had known it since Cholula, since the massacre, since the night she had understood the two machines and chosen to stay. Knowing it did not make the sight any smaller.

The Spanish soldiers on the mules ahead of her had turned in their saddles. They were looking back at her. Not with hostility. Not with curiosity. With the wide berth that a column gives a woman who is carrying the child of the man who commands the column. They gave her the space because it was the arithmetic of the situation. They did not give it to her because they were kind.

She let the mule walk.

A Tlaxcalan captain came out of the brush on her left. He was not in armor. He wore a tunic and a loincloth and a necklace of greenstone beads that had been his father's. He had a bow in his right hand and a quiver on his left shoulder. He walked to the mule and stopped, and the mule stopped with him, not because it was trained, because it sensed the weight of the stop.

He spoke in Nahuatl. The voice was flat. He was not angry. He was asking about water. The porters were dying of thirst on this stage. The captain was too proud to put it that way. He phrased it as a request for the rationing of the water barrels that the column carried, and he made the request in a tone that suggested he was offering a suggestion rather than a necessity.

Malintzin listened. She listened to the verbs. The captain had used a verb that meant *ask politely.* He had used a second verb that meant *hope you will consider.* He had not used the verb that meant *they are dying.* He could not have used it. He was a captain. He did not speak of death in the present tense.

She looked at Cortés, who was a quarter-mile ahead on his horse, the horse walking at the same pace as her mule. He had turned. He was waiting for her. She did not have to ride up to him. He would wait.

She looked back at the captain. She spoke.

She rendered his request into Spanish. She rendered it accurately. She chose her Spanish verbs with care. She used the verb *solicitar* rather than the verb *preguntar.* She used the construction *es necesario* rather than the construction *sería bueno.* She weighted the request slightly more strongly than the Tlaxcalan phrasing strictly warranted, because the porters were dying and the captain was too proud to put it that way, and because the weight of the sentence in the translation would make the difference between the request being heard and the request being filed.

She did not add anything. She did not subtract anything. She made the sentence stand straight in the second language, the way a Nahuatl sentence stands straight in the first, and she rode the sentence forward to the man who would decide.

Cortés heard her. He did not ask for clarification. He turned to the quartermaster at his right and gave the order. The quartermaster turned to the porters at the rear of the brigantine train and the water ration was doubled for the next league.

The captain at her side heard the decision in the Spanish. He did not speak. He nodded. Once. He thanked her in Nahuatl. The thank-you was in the flat voice again. He did not smile.

She nodded back.

The captain turned and walked back into the brush. The mule moved. The column moved. The smoke on the horizon held its shape.

This was the power within. Not the power of a captain, not the power of a pilot, not the power of a man who could command a column. The power of a translator who stood between two sentences and chose which verbs the second sentence would carry. One ration. One weighted verb. One set of porters who would reach the lake alive instead of being buried at the pass.

She had been doing it for eight months. She would keep doing it. The city was going to burn. She was going to be in the room when it burned. She would soften what she could. She would preserve what she could. She would save sentences.

The mule stepped up the crest of the ridge. The valley opened. The smoke from the valley villages showed on the horizon, a dozen plumes, maybe more, the smoke from the cooking fires, the smoke from the burned thatch, the smoke from the things the Spanish had burned on the way in.

She looked at the smoke. She did not turn her head. She let the mule walk.

The child inside her shifted, a small movement, a settling. She let it. She had stopped trying to coax the position. The child made its decisions inside her. The child would be born in August. The siege would begin in May. The child would be born inside the falling city, or inside a city that had just fallen, and in either case the name on the baptismal record would be a Spanish name and the language of the room would be Castilian.

She had made her peace with each of these in a different week.

The road began its descent. The dust rose. The column moved.

Behind her, the Reliquaries were spaced along the line. Ahead of her, the lake. To her left and right, the Tlaxcalan warriors, watching. In the center, the Spanish, moving.

She was the hinge. She would hold.
