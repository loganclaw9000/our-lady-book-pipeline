Itzcoatl pilots *Obsidian Mirror* on the right flank, Tezcatlipoca-caste, fifty feet, Texcocan-made.

The plain at Otumba was forty kilometers from the city, dust to the horizon. Late morning. The sun sat high enough to flatten shadows. Bad ground for a Tezcatlipoca, and he had said so to the field commander an hour earlier; the commander had not answered, because the order had come from above him.

Through the viewport he counted what remained of the Spanish line. Two Reliquaries upright, second-class, both, neither moving fast. The rest of the foreign engines were down, scattered back along the causeway like dropped tools, abandoned in the lake mud during the flight from the city seven nights earlier. Around the Reliquaries, a Spanish square of perhaps six hundred men, unhorsed, unarmored where their armor had been ditched to lighten the swim, faces that were more sunburn than face. Behind them, two thousand Tlaxcalans on foot.

Should be over by midday.

*Sun-at-Noon* stood at the center of the Mexica line. A hundred feet. Actual gold on the chest disc, talons rather than bootplates, hummingbird-helm crested with quetzal feather banks ten meters tall. The pilot was Cuauhtemoctzin's cousin, Itzcoatl had eaten with him three months earlier in the precinct refectory, both of them complaining about the rations. The engine drank only the bones of warriors taken in the Flower Wars who had ascended the pyramid willingly. Three generations of those bones rode in its teōcalli-chest. It was the largest field engine the Triple Alliance kept outside the city itself.

It was already moving.

Itzcoatl's cockpit was lit only by the smoking-mirror disc on the chest of his own engine, which fed reflected light back through the divination port. The disc showed him what *Obsidian Mirror* saw, the Spanish square, the Tlaxcalan rabble, the dust. He reached forward, not with his hand but with the deity, who had been holding his hands for two hours and would hold them until evening, and *Obsidian Mirror* reached forward with him.

The deity was already considering routes through the Tlaxcalan rear when the woman broke from the line.

She was small. He marked her as small first because his vision had been counting engines, not people, and people on this scale were cursor-marks. She came forward at a run, alone, dust kicking, no formation behind her. She carried a banner.

The banner was Mexica.

He registered that next. Not Tlaxcalan colors. Mexica, a captured standard, the cotinga-blue cloth, and fixed at the head of the staff, the solar disc of a Huitzilopochtli company. The disc was hammered gold. It would have weighed thirty kilos. She was running with it angled forward like a lance.

The deity inside Itzcoatl said: *teōmachtiani.*

He saw it then, the way she ran. Not a soldier's run. The slow-motion blur of someone whose body was not currently theirs. She was a pilot on foot. He could see, at this range, the rain-glyphs tattooed up her forearms. *Matlalcueitl.* A storm-pilot, on foot, at noon, on a dry plain, where her deity was at its weakest. She had picked the worst possible terrain for herself and walked onto it carrying a Huitzilopochtli relic.

That was the trick. He saw the trick. The sun could not strike its own banner.

*Sun-at-Noon* saw her. *Sun-at-Noon* was direct, aggressive, did not retreat unless its pilot died. It saw a single armed figure crossing toward it and it did what it was theologically required to do, it accepted the challenge. It strode out from the line to meet her.

The macuahuitl came down, a horizontal sweep at standing-human height, obsidian edges trailing flecks of gold leaf, and Matlalcueitl went under it, not around. The blade passed over her shoulders by less than a meter. She did not slow. The second strike was vertical, and she stepped through where the engine's foot had just been and the foot came back down on the dust where her foot had just been, and neither of them touched the other.

Itzcoatl heard a sound that was not transmitted through the air but through the deity in his skull: the deity laughing. Not at her. Recognizing her.

*Sun-at-Noon* took a step backward. Then another. A first-class Huitzilopochtli engine does not retreat, but it does take ground to set its strike, and the deity inside it had decided, in the hummingbird mind that was its mind, to bring the macuahuitl down with the full descending arc the third blow required. Two steps. Not enough.

The right knee went first.

Itzcoatl did not understand at first what he was watching. The hundred-foot engine bent at the knee, a slow ceremonial drop, the way a warrior kneels to receive a captive's submission. Then the left foot took the weight. The chest disc, the gilded sun-plate, came down out of the sky and settled at the height of the cotinga-blue banner Matlalcueitl was carrying.

She did not break her stride.

The seam on a Huitzilopochtli engine ran low on the chest, a hand's width above where the navel would be on a man, where the priests bound the teōcalli-chest shut every panquetzaliztli with the names of the year's fed warriors. On a standing engine the seam sat fifty feet in the air. On a kneeling one it sat at the height of an upraised banner.

She drove the captured solar standard into the seal.

The disc on the head of her staff matched the disc on the chest of the engine. Same god. Same metal. The names on the seal recognized one of their own, and opened.

*Sun-at-Noon* stopped moving. Not as a person stops moving. As a temple stops being a temple. The kneeling pose held. The right arm, sword still raised, did not finish the swing. The feather banks went still in a wind that was still blowing.

Cuauhtemoctzin's cousin was somewhere behind that gold, and he was either dead now or about to be. Itzcoatl did not know which.

The deity inside him said nothing.

He stood *Obsidian Mirror* in place on the right flank and watched Matlalcueitl walk back from the kneeling engine. She was not running anymore. Her arms hung. The Tlaloc inside her had spent itself on the approach. She would be a woman again by the time her feet carried her past her own line, an exhausted woman on a dry plain with no shelter, and the Mexica line would close on her in seconds.

Except the Mexica line was not closing.

The line was looking at *Sun-at-Noon.* Two thousand warriors and forty engines, looking at the hundred-foot solar giant kneeling motionless at the center of their formation with a Tlaxcalan stick through its heart, and not one of them was moving forward.

Itzcoatl, in his cockpit, framed by the matte light of his own divinatory disc, allowed a thought to form that he had not allowed for two years of war.

*We could lose this.*

The thought arrived complete, in his own voice, undiluted by the deity, who had withdrawn a half-step to let it through. It sat in the cockpit like a fourth person. He looked at it. He did not unthink it.

That was the betrayal. Not the doubt. The not-unthinking.

Behind the Tlaxcalan line, the Spanish square, six hundred sunburned men with no engines and no horses, began, raggedly, to advance.

Mirror walked the right flank at half-pace and the mirror-stack showed him the field at four removes, direct optic, low ground-glass, high obsidian, and the corner-mirror set behind the seat that gave him the line of his own back.

The Mexica field had held around *Sun-at-Noon* as around an axis. The axis was now a hundred-foot standing dead. The hummingbird helm canted forward by six degrees and held there. The right arm with its bronze blade had not finished the stroke it had begun fifty seconds ago. The solar heraldry was still vivid in the noon light. The bone-fuel had not yet drained out of the lines. A Mexica engine in this configuration would, by the chronicles, stand for a season before any motif consented to fade. The Spanish would have to walk past it for the rest of the afternoon and the rest of their lives.

He read the line breaking before he heard it. A standard-bearer somewhere in the Tlaxcalan rear was already shouting. The cohort of foot teōmachtiani that had been pulling back toward the broken creek bed in good order ten minutes ago had stopped pulling back. The Spanish cavalry, on the ground, were standing up. Sandoval's flag was moving. Cortés's banner, ill-positioned to recover at the third hour, was now positioned exactly correctly to receive a collapsing enemy.

Matlalcueitl was walking. She had come down off the chest seam and off the ankle with the slow care of a woman descending a tree she did not own, had stood one pace from the standing engine, and then had set off across the dust without raising her head. Her hands were empty. She did not look at the Mexica line, or at the Spanish, or up at the dead thing she had killed. She looked at the dust in front of her own feet. He recognized her by the cloth on her head, a stained wrap from a wound she had taken on the causeway seven nights ago. He had seen her come off in the long line of Tlaxcalans crawling out of the lake at dawn. He had not known her name then. He knew it now. Pilli house, five generations of teōmachtiani. Brother killed at Tlaxcala by the Reliquary called *La Niña de Córdoba*, sixty yards away and watching. Roughly thirty years old. *Hummingbird at Dawn* left behind in Tenochtitlan with a broken left shoulder-joint, which meant she had crossed the causeway on foot, and the plain on foot, and had killed the largest engine on the field on foot, and was leaving the field on foot.

Mirror's standing orders were to hold the flank and harass any Spanish second-class that broke for open country. Mirror had not engaged. Mirror would not engage now. The kill-geometry that had said the day was closed twenty minutes out had been rewritten in the half-second after the standard went, and the geometry now said something he had not let himself consider since he was nine years old.

He let himself consider it.

The consideration arrived whole. He did not push it away. He examined it.

He examined the engines lost on the causeway. He examined the pilots lost behind them. He examined the *ixiptla* killed at the Templo Mayor in the month of Toxcatl, and the priests who would have bled them, and the calendar specialists who had timed their bleeding, and the bone-fuel pipeline that ran out of all of them and was now not running, and would not be running by the next harvest, and would not be running, possibly, by the harvest after. He examined a Tlaxcala that had once fielded engines for the Triple Alliance and now fielded them against it. He examined Cholula, where the dormant Quetzalcoatl shrine had turned its head and had not, despite everything, turned it back. He examined the Maya at Centla turning. He examined Moctezuma on the rooftop in the last days before the lake, the stones or the swords, the chronicles already disagreeing, and his own eyes registering only that the Huey Tlatoani had died in the noise. He examined a Spanish caudillo who had walked his column out of the lake seven nights ago and onto this plain this morning and would, by the end of the afternoon, have walked his column into a victory the kill-geometry of dawn had said he could not have.

The examination covered all of this in the time *Sun-at-Noon*'s solar heraldry took to declare itself once more in the noon light, undimmed.

The examination itself was a betrayal of the loyalty he had carried since he was nine years old.

He knew it as he was doing it. He named it to himself, in the Texcocan dialect his grandfather had taught him, in the verb form the calmecac used for the betrayal of a sworn lord. He used the verb. He did not soften it. There was a softer verb for the same act, the verb a poet would use, the one Nezahualcoyotl used in the song about the rabbit and the snare, and he did not use that one either. The calmecac verb said he was already inside the act. The poet's verb would have said he was approaching it. He was not approaching it.

Mirror's hum stayed steady against his back.

He thought about that. The engine, whatever it knew of him, did not register the betrayal as a wound, or did, and was the kind of god that watched a betrayal without comment, the smoking mirror that showed a man what was already there. Tezcatlipoca did not depart for a stranger. Tezcatlipoca did not depart for a man examining his own loyalty in the cockpit of a Tezcatlipoca-caste engine, either, which meant one of two things: either the god did not consider the examination a departure, or the god had been waiting nine years for the examination to begin.

He could not, in the cockpit, tell which. He thought it might not matter.

On the corner-mirror, the line of his own back. He looked at it for a moment. The leather of the seat. The lacquer on the chest panel. His own shoulders, set the way his father had taught him to set them on the day he was given his first calmecac robe. He looked younger in the corner-mirror than he expected to. Younger than he had any right to look on the afternoon his side lost a war.

He kept walking Mirror on the flank.

The Tlaxcalan square had begun to advance. The Spanish square, what was left of it, had reformed around Cortés's banner and was advancing also. The two advances were converging toward the standing body of *Sun-at-Noon* and the small walking figure of Matlalcueitl, who had not stopped or sped up and who was, by the geometry, going to walk straight through the seam where the two armies were about to meet, and who was not going to be stopped by either of them, because what she had done at the heart-chamber had purchased her something the calmecac did not have a verb for. Itzcoatl watched her for the length of one of Mirror's strides. He did not have the verb. He thought about asking the engine for it, and did not.

Sandoval's cavalry were forming. Cortés's banner was steady. Somewhere out beyond the broken creek bed, a Mexica drum that had been beating since dawn stopped, and the field heard it stop.

Mirror walked.

The hum that comes off Mirror as he walks her back from the line is half a tone lower than at dawn, and the rain on the engine's gilt is doing something to the sound, turning it from a chord into a single ragged note. He hears it before he sees anything else. He does not look up.

Behind him the Mexica plain is unwinding in the way an army unwinds when its showpiece has been killed by a runner with a stolen standard. Three engines pass him at the camp's edge, already shutting down. Two Huitzilopochtli second-class. One Tlaloc. Their pilots sit in the cockpits with helmets off, faces wet with rain or sweat or both, hands flat on their knees in the posture *teōmachtiani* take when they have nothing to say. Itzcoatl does not greet them. They do not greet him.

The rain is lighter than the storm at the causeway seven nights past. It runs gray and thin down Mirror's heraldry, the gold leaf shedding water in narrow streams along the shoulder plates and the long ribs of the flank.

He gives the order at the camp's edge. He wants Mirror put into rest-rite here, in the field, not at the home cradle in Texcoco. He wants her shut down before he walks back among his own.

The two adorator-priests who have been with him since dawn are *telpochcalli* boys, sixteen and seventeen. They do not argue. They do not ask. They have seen what happened at the center of the line, and they understand what the rite means in this hour, and they begin the closing sequence with their faces bent so he cannot read them.

He helps. He takes off his helmet and sets it on the wet ground. He does the rite with them, the closing rite of the Tezcatlipoca caste, shorter than the Huitzilopochtli's, quieter, no fire. Only the recitation of the deity's names. Only the smearing of his own blood on the chest-disc, which is not a painted motif but a real piece of polished obsidian, four hands across, the working face of the engine's divinatory mirror.

The disc drinks his blood. It shows him his face. The face is distorted by the curve of the obsidian, and gray, and not a face he recognizes.

He finishes. He steps back. The engine goes still.

---

He leaves the cockpit and walks to the edge of the camp and stands there and looks west.

Cortés's column is making for Tlaxcala, three miles out, ragged at the rear. The men are tired. The Reliquaries that are left are walking last, on foot, blessed crystal cracked in two of them, he can see the dull patches where the heraldry has begun, already, to fade to the gray that follows a saint's departure. The Spanish, who lost half their corps on the Tlacopan causeway, who should have died here today under *Sun-at-Noon*'s sword, are walking to Tlaxcala alive.

He thinks, then, in sequence, the things a pilot of his caste is trained to think when something has come apart.

His cousin Ixtlilxochitl, in December, in a borrowed room at Texcoco, paper walls, brazier between them, no servants. The proposition had been simple: join the Spanish, take Texcoco back from the Mexica, with the Spanish engines as leverage. He had refused. He had been loyal to Tenochtitlan, to the *cuauhtin* alliance his caste was sworn into, to the seal that bound Mirror's relic to the Templo Mayor. The refusal had been clean. His cousin had not pressed. They had drunk *octli* together afterward and not spoken of it again.

His sister Xochitl, widowed in May, her son scarred from temple to jaw. The letter she sent after the Templo Mayor massacre had no words. Inside the folded paper: a feather, a piece of cloth, a child's sandal. All three stained. She had sent them without writing his name on the outside. He had understood. He had understood that she had lost everything, that he had lost nothing, that she was sending him the difference between those two facts in three small objects.

His mother in Texcoco, philosopher in the Nezahualcoyotl tradition, who at dinner months ago had told him, without saying *Quetzalcoatl*, without saying *wake*, that the seals at Cholula and at Tula had cracked further. She had said *cracked further*, and it had been enough. He had understood the dormant engines would wake. He had understood that when they woke they would wake against the Mexica. Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl are brothers in the cosmogony, and his mother was telling him whose turn was ending.

Matlalcueitl, on the plain, hands empty, head down, the standard already driven home where she had climbed the engine's leg to put it.

*Sun-at-Noon* tilted forward at six degrees, a hundred feet of solar gilt arrested, sword arm raised and never finishing its swing, the standard pole through the heart-chamber where she had reached it.

He does not pray.

He does the exercise his mother taught him as a boy. The Texcocan philosopher's exercise. Name the thing you do not want to name.

He names it.

*I am beginning to choose.*

What he has called *dedication* for thirteen years, what his teachers in the *calmecac* called dedication, what every priest who certified him called dedication, what the deity inside Mirror has accepted as dedication, has, in the past hour, become a different word. His teachers would not recognize it under the new name. He is not sure he does either.

The night closes over the camp.

---

He sleeps in Mirror's cradle in the field. The engine is at rest, away from the Templo Mayor's accumulated hum, for the first time since the campaign began. He notes the fact as he lies down. He has not eaten since dawn. The two boys have laid hammocks in the lee of Mirror's left foot, fifteen paces off, and are already asleep. The fact of the distance from the temple is small and physical and it matters.

He dreams. The dream is not battle. The dream is his mother's dinner table at Texcoco, copal smoke, two cups, the lacquered edge worn pale by his grandfather's elbows, and his mother is sitting across from him, and she is saying nothing, and he is saying nothing, and the silence between them is the thing he has just named on the plain, and the silence is full. The candle on the table burns the way it burned the night she told him about the seals.

He wakes in the dark with rain on his face and the named word still in his mouth. He knows, lying there, that he has already made the decision he will walk through in a week, in civilian clothes, through Tlaxcalan pickets, into a room where Cortés will be sitting and Malintzin will be standing at his shoulder. He does not need to make it again. The decision was made on the plain at the moment of naming. The week between is logistics.

He lies awake.

The engine hums, softer now, fifty miles from any temple of her god, but with a catch in the sound that was not there at dawn. A hum like a person coughing in sleep. Once. Then again. A longer pause. Then again. The rite has put her down properly. She should not be making the sound. Older pilots describe this catch, it appears in an engine whose pilot has begun to drift from the deity, and is, in some plain mechanical sense no priest will explain, the deity noticing.

He does not get up. He does not check the chest-disc. He does not recite.

He goes back to sleep.

The rain falls. The engine coughs, at intervals, through the night. Mirror is at rest in a field she has never rested in before, and her pilot is asleep beside her, and the small physical fact of that distance from the Templo Mayor matters more than anything that happened on the plain.

By morning the dedication-through-trial is a defection that has not yet been walked.

The war continues.
