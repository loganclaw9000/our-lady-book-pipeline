The galleons sit half-submerged on the sand. The surf has already begun to lap at the lower ribs of the *Santa María de la Consolación*. The tide is low. The wind is steady. The ships will not sail again.

They will burn.

Cortés says it is about God, and about glory, and about God and glory together. He says they will build a city here, and the city will be called **Veracruz**, and the city will belong to him. He says they are founding a cabildo, that they will elect regidores and an *alcalde*, that there will be a town hall and a charter and a seal, and that he will stand as their *alcalde mayor* and their *justicia mayor* and, by their own free vote, their *capitán general*, in a place that does not yet have a name.

He says they are founding a city in a single morning, and that this is the way of Spain, to turn the world into paper first, to make the world belong to them by law, and then to make it belong to them by blood.

Andrés watches him. He has watched him do this since the *Santa María de la Consolación* first cast off from Havana. He watches him sign the founding charter of Veracruz in a tent that has not yet been raised properly, with the canvas flapping and the pegs half-driven into the sand. He watches him appoint men to offices they do not yet hold, in a place that does not yet have a name. He watches him invent a bureaucracy, in the moment, for the sole purpose of making himself the head of it.

He understands, with a clarity that will not leave him for the rest of the campaign, that Cortés is a lawyer before he is a soldier. The men around him are Reconquista veterans or sons of veterans, and they think war is the thing being done here. It is not. The thing being done here is paperwork. The war will follow.

Cortés signs, then passes the pen to the other captains, then passes it to him. Andrés watches Cortés's signature, then his own, then the signatures of the other captains and the other men, the *alférez*, the chaplains, the engineers, the pilots. He watches the paper fill with names.

He watches, and he understands, slowly, that he has signed onto a mutiny against his governor. That he has signed onto a war that is not against the Mexica alone, but against Velázquez and the Audiencia and, if it comes to that, the Crown. That he has signed onto Cortés, not the Crown. That he has no way to take this signature back, because Cortés will burn the ships that would carry him home.

Outside the tent, through the canvas, he can hear the hum of his Reliquary. *La Niña de Córdoba* stands in her cradle on the sand, gilded breastplate polished, the small glass box at her sternum catching the morning light. The bones inside the box are Flora's, a Mozárabe girl-martyr of Córdoba, dead seven hundred years, who is somehow now in this place that did not exist for her, that did not exist for anyone she ever knew. He can hear her hum through the canvas. He can hear her hum through his own body.

He does not know what to make of the fact that signing this paper changes nothing about how she feels.

---

The cabildo is elected. The city is founded. The charter is signed. The paper is sealed with wax, Cortés's own seal, which he has carried from Cuba for exactly this purpose, the wax warmed in a cup of seawater over a brazier because they have no proper hearth. The seal is pressed. The paper is folded. A clerk is dispatched to copy it three times.

Cortés stands. He walks out of the tent. The men follow.

The *Santa María de la Consolación* is set on fire first. Andrés had thought she would be saved for last, she is the flagship, she is the largest, she is the one with Cortés's name carved into her sternpost. But Cortés wants her gone first. He wants the men to see that the flagship goes the way of the rest. He wants no man to think that there is a vessel on this beach reserved for officers, or for officers' kin, or for the wounded, or for retreat.

The *Trinidad* burns. The *San Lázaro* burns. The *Santa María de la Victoria* burns. The *San Pablo* burns. The *San Jerónimo* burns. The *San Juan de Letrán* burns. The *San Bartolomé* burns. The *San Nicolás* burns. The *Santa Cruz* burns.

Nine ships. Nine hulls, one by one, to the waterline. The flames climb the masts. The tar hisses. The canvas curls and falls in black sheets onto the wet sand. The smoke does not rise straight; the steady wind off the dunes bends it inland, over the men, over the half-raised tent, over the cradles where the Reliquaries stand. Andrés breathes it. Everyone breathes it. The smell is pine resin and pitch and salt and the particular sweetness of the gilding on the figureheads as the gold leaf melts and runs.

The *Santa Cruz* breaks her keel and goes sideways into the surf. A cheer goes up from somewhere along the dune line, Andrés cannot tell who, cannot tell why. The *San Pablo* takes the longest, because her hull is the newest and her timbers the wettest, and she sits there glowing red through her gunports for an hour after the others are blackened ribs.

He counts them. He counts them again. Nine. The *Santiago del Paso* does not burn, and the second-class engines do not burn, and the third-class engines do not burn. The Reliquaries stand in their cradles on the sand, watching, the way men watch.

*La Niña* watches. He can feel her watch, through the cord that runs from the relic-box at her sternum to the small medal he wears under his breastplate, because that is how the binding works, not magic, not metaphor, but a real conducted thing, a thrum like a struck bell that travels through bone. She watches the ships burn. She does not turn her head. He does not turn his.

He understands that he is weeping. He has been weeping for some time. Not the weeping of the faceplates, that is the engine's weeping, which the chroniclers will record and which Cortés will not discuss. This is the pilot's weeping, which is different, which is his. He does not know whether he is weeping for the ships, or for Cuba, or for the village outside Córdoba where his mother is still alive and is, at this moment, perhaps cooking something, perhaps hanging linen, perhaps walking to the church of his childhood and not knowing that her son has just signed his name to a thing that cannot be unsigned.

He watches *La Niña* watch the ships, and he wonders whether Flora, whose bones do not bend when the gilding bends in prayer, whose bones are seven hundred years older than this beach and have been lifted out of her shrine in Córdoba and carried across an ocean for this, wonders whether Flora has ever wept inside her box. He does not know. He suspects he is the wrong person to ask.

---

The smoke is one column now. The nine columns have braided themselves into a single black thing leaning inland, and the inland wind keeps it leaning, and the leaning column is the tallest landmark for thirty miles in any direction. The Totonac scouts on the ridge above the beach will see it. The Mexica couriers on the road to Tenochtitlan will see it. Moctezuma, eight days from here on foot, will hear about it before the week is out.

Cortés stands on the wet sand with his hands behind his back. He does not speak. He has done his speaking. The paper is signed and the ships are burning and the men are watching, and there is nothing further he needs to say, because the thing is done, and saying it would only diminish it.

Andrés stands beside *La Niña*'s cradle. He puts his hand on the timber. The timber is warm, not from the fire, which is too far, but from her, from the running of her, from the slow heat of a Reliquary at idle.

They are not coming back.

He says it aloud, quietly, to the cradle, to her, to himself.

*No volveremos.*

She hums. The hum does not change.

Behind him, the *Santa María de la Consolación*, last to catch and first lit, gives up her mainmast. It comes down across her own deck with a sound like a door slamming in an empty house. The men do not turn to look. They have already seen.

The march arrived at Cempoala on the morning of July 15. They had been four days inland from the coast, climbing through scrub that gave way to maize, and the road that had been sand and crushed shell was now packed earth, swept clean by people Andrés did not see but who he knew were watching from the cane.

He walked beside *La Niña de Córdoba*. She was not running. None of the four engines were running, Cortés had given the order at the last river crossing, and the cradles had been struck and the pilots had walked the last six miles on foot beside their machines, which walked at the pace of an unloaded ox. The hum was still there. It always was. But it was the low hum, the resting hum, the hum of a saint asleep in her box and not the higher resonance she gave off when Andrés was inside her and the prayer was running.

Cempoala's outer wall was lime-washed white and the lime was fresh. Andrés had been told to expect this and had not believed it. The wall was so white it hurt to look at, and the gates were open, and beyond the gates was a plaza paved in flat stones and lined with low pyramids that were also white, not stone-color but a deliberate human act repeated, he supposed, every season.

The fat cacique was waiting in the plaza. Andrés had been told to expect this too. He had not been told that the cacique would be carried on a litter by four men of the household, or that the litter would be set down in the dust without ceremony, or that the cacique, once standing, would be a head shorter than Cortés and would have to crane his neck to look up at him.

Bernardo stood three paces behind Cortés, hands folded inside the sleeves of his robe, mouth moving silently. He was rehearsing. The translator chain was longer than Andrés had ever seen one, Malintzin to a Totonac woman whose name Andrés did not know to a Totonac man in a feathered cape and back again, Bernardo at the Spanish end with the Latin and the Castilian both. Andrés watched the friar's mouth and understood that he was not rehearsing words. He was rehearsing the act of waiting through four mouths for one sentence.

The cacique spoke first. The Totonac woman spoke. Malintzin spoke. Bernardo turned to Cortés and said, in a voice that carried only as far as it needed to: *He welcomes you to the city he says is no longer the Mexica's city. He says he has been waiting for you. He says he has not been waiting long, but it has felt long.*

Cortés smiled. He had a smile he used for caciques. Andrés had seen it three times now. It did not move his eyes.

Cortés spoke. Bernardo translated. Malintzin spoke. The Totonac woman spoke. The cacique listened. The cacique answered, and the chain ran in reverse, and what came out at Bernardo's end was: *Twenty years of tribute. The Mexica come every year and they take the boys for the pyramids and the girls for the houses of the priests and the cotton for the warriors' quilted armor. He says he will give you eight hundred warriors. He says he will give you twenty engine pilots. He says they are not as many as he would like to give. He says he has more, but the Mexica took the others.*

Andrés watched the cacique's face while Bernardo's voice ran. The cacique was looking at him. Not at Cortés. At him.

He understood, after a moment, why. The cacique had thought a Spanish pilot would be smaller. He had probably been told by the Totonac woman, who had been told by Malintzin, who had been told by Aguilar who had been told by some sailor who had seen Reliquaries unloaded at Veracruz, that the men who piloted the iron giants were boys. Andrés was twenty-three. He was not a boy. He stood five-foot-eleven and had broadened through the shoulders during the crossing, and the cacique was trying to fit him and the kneeling weeping-woman behind him into the same picture and could not.

The picture got worse for the cacique when the Totonac engines came in.

There were four of them. They came in from the east side of the plaza, walking, and they were smaller than *La Niña*, perhaps thirty feet to her forty-five, and they were not iron. Andrés could not tell at first what they were. Wood, mostly. Wood and lashed cane, with plates of beaten copper at the joints and at the chest. Each one wore a feathered headdress that ran the length of its spine, green-and-red feathers as long as a man's arm, and each had jade set into the eye-sockets of the mask. The masks were animal-faced. A jaguar. A long-beaked bird. A serpent. A frog.

They were not running, either. But their hum was wrong. Not louder than *La Niña*'s, not lower, just *off*, at a different interval, a different harmonic, like four singers in a different mode. Andrés felt it in his sternum and understood that whatever powered them was not bone. Or was bone, but not the bone of any martyr he had ever heard named.

The pilots walked beside their engines, the way the Spanish pilots had walked beside theirs. Andrés had time to look at them. They were older than he was. Two were his father's age. They wore quilted cotton and their faces were painted in narrow black bands across the eyes, and they were anxious. He could see it in the way they kept glancing back at their machines and then forward at his. They had heard the iron-giants were coming. They had not known what iron-giants meant. Now they knew, and they were afraid that what they had was not enough.

Cortés, through the chain, said something gracious. The cacique, through the chain, said something gracious back. Bernardo translated only the first half of the cacique's reply and then stopped and looked at Cortés and said, very quietly, *He says they will salute you in their fashion. He says you will salute them in yours.*

The Totonac engines stopped at twelve paces. Their pilots, in unison, lifted their right arms. Each engine lifted its right arm, the weapon arm, Andrés understood, though none of them were holding weapons today, and held it out and up, palm forward, fingers spread. The headdress feathers hung straight. The copper plates caught the sun.

Andrés had been instructed, on the road, what to do.

He stepped forward. He turned to face *La Niña de Córdoba*. He raised one hand to her and made the sign of the cross in the air between them, and she, without him in her cockpit, without anyone in her cockpit, with only the weight of her own bones in their box and the morning light on her gilded breastplate, sank slowly onto her right knee. The cradle-timbers were not under her this time. Just the white stone of the plaza. The stone took her weight with a sound like a held breath let out. Her head bent. The weeping mask faced the ground.

The plaza was silent.

The cacique had not been told this would happen. The Totonac pilots had not been told. They were standing with their arms still raised and their engines' arms still raised, and across the twelve paces Andrés could see them adjusting, slowly, to a thing that did not fit any of the categories they had brought with them into the plaza.

He understood, distantly, that they were doing the same thing he was. Two traditions standing across from each other in good faith and finding that good faith was not a translation.

Bernardo, behind him, said in Latin, very softly, *oratio.* Then in Castilian, almost too soft to hear: *not the same word.*

*La Niña* rose. Andrés walked back to his cockpit and climbed in, which took a minute exactly, because it always took a minute exactly, and when he was inside her and the prayer was running her hum changed up to the higher register, the working register, and the four Totonac engines across the plaza did not change. Could not change. Their pilots heard the difference and looked at each other and looked at the cacique, and the cacique, from the dust where his litter had been set down, looked at Cortés.

The cacique said, through the chain, something that took a long time to reach Bernardo. Bernardo translated last:

*He says the alliance is offered. He says he hopes you will not regret accepting it as much as he hopes he will not regret offering it.*

Cortés accepted.

Andrés, inside *La Niña de Córdoba*, with Flora's bones humming against his sternum at the working register and the four Totonac engines watching him from twelve paces with jade in their eyes, understood for the second time in eight weeks that he had signed onto something he could not unsign.
