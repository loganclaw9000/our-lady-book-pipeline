Malinalli walked in the wake of giants. She had walked in their wake since she was a child traded across three lords, and before that in the wake of her mother's people, who had served the wake of Tenochtitlan from a distance. The difference now, the difference that mattered, the only one, was that the giants ahead of her were giants she was helping to make.

The Spanish camp at the edge of Lake Texcoco was a city inverted. Where Tenochtitlan had been stone and ceremony floating on water, this was canvas and ash anchored in mud. Tents in rows of twelve. Cooking fires at fixed intervals of roughly thirty paces. Engine cradles inspected at dawn the way priests had once inspected the temple before sunrise, same gestures, different gods, the same horrified care. The conquest had become a routine, and the routine was its own horror, because routines were what survived the men who began them.

She moved through the rows in her seventh month. The command tent had a small walled annex that was her quarters, six paces by four, a brazier she did not need in May and a low cot she used more than she wanted to. Two Nahua women she had chosen herself walked at her elbows: Tozi, who had been with her since Cempoala, and a younger Tlaxcalan named Citlal who had come to her in Tlaxcala without being asked. They were her guard, her companions, and on bad nights her interpreters of her own face back to her. She translated each day for Cortés and the Tlaxcalan and Texcocan captains, as she had translated since the day before Tabasco, as she had translated since before she had a name anyone recorded. What had changed was that now she knew what the words were doing on both sides of her tongue.

Three brigantines came in from patrol with smoke in their rigging, *Magdalena*, *Espíritu*, and the smaller one whose name she always forgot. Lake-mist hung in pale strips at knee height. *San Esteban del Río* sat in cradle being refitted with a new monstrance bracket; the old one had cracked at Otumba and never been properly fixed, only welded and prayed over and welded again. A line of Tlaxcalan porters passed with lake-fish strung on reeds for the camp's supper, the silver of the fish very bright against the gray mud. Across the water, the half-awake Great Engine of the Templo Mayor sounded, a hum that was not a hum, a pressure under the breastbone, rising and falling like a slow lung. The lake carried it the way water carries thunder. From the camp she could not see the engine; she only knew where to face. She had been carrying that sound in her chest since the siege began, the way a woman carries a second heartbeat in her seventh month, and she did not always know which beat was hers.

Fray Bernardo sat with her many evenings. He was the Franciscan who had joined the expedition at the last moment, in Cuba, at his own insistence, a moral anchor for the Spanish pilot, for the commanders, for anyone who needed one and admitted it. He had seen the truth that ran the engines on both sides of the lake. He understood what the Inquisition was actually for. He refused to pilot. He also refused to let the siege be won by a machine fed on a child's bones, and he had not yet worked out, in any way he could say aloud in daylight, what the third option was.

She taught him Nahuatl. He taught her ecclesiastical Latin. They sat on a folding bench outside her annex with a clay lamp between them, and the lamp's flame stood very still in the lake-air because the air at evening did not move. They traded words for water, *atl*, *aqua*, and words for sacrifice, where the Latin had three words and the Nahuatl had eleven, and the gap between three and eleven was a thing they both began, after several evenings, to avoid looking at directly.

They both knew, by now, what the engines ran on. They both knew what the siege would cost in the currency the engines used. Bernardo began saying things he would not remember saying, about what a priest might owe to a city he could not save, about whether a willing death could be a sacrament if it was also fuel, about the saint of radical poverty and what poverty meant when one had nothing left but a body. Malinalli heard each sentence as it left him, and she did not stop him, and she did not write anything down, because the writing of it would have been its own betrayal. The next morning he would come to her with reddened eyes and ask what they had spoken of, and she would say *the conjugation of the verb to give* and he would nod and not press her, and by the third or fourth such morning he had stopped asking, which was worse.

Outside the annex the siege moved on its own logic. The aqueducts at Chapultepec had been cut in the first week, Sandoval's work, swift and unsentimental. The causeways were blockaded at all three ends. The brigantines patrolled the lake in pairs, thirteen hulls assembled at Texcoco from sections dragged over the Paso de Cortés, and they were the reason the Mexica canoes no longer ran at night. Tenochtitlan was burning block by block as the ring tightened, not all at once, not theatrically, but house by house in a method Cortés had ordered and Ixtlilxochitl had refined. Inside the city, starvation had begun. Outside, the Spanish and allied camps had settled into the rhythm of destruction: meals at fixed hours, duty rosters posted on a board nailed to a post, laundry on lines between the tents, the cradles inspected at first light by men who crossed themselves before they touched the joints.

A Tlaxcalan boy ran past with a message tube. A horse coughed somewhere down the line. The lamp between her and Bernardo guttered once and steadied. He was looking at the lake, at the place across the water where the sound came from. She watched his hands. They were a priest's hands, thin, the knuckles dry, and they were not shaking, which she had learned to find more disturbing than shaking would have been.

"*Quid debet sacerdos civitati*," he said, half to her, half to the lamp. *What does a priest owe a city.*

She gave him the Nahuatl: *tlein quitlacuilia teopixqui in altepetl*. She gave it back to him slowly, so he would hear how the verb sat at the end and waited.

He repeated it. He got the stress wrong on the second syllable. She corrected him. He repeated it again, correctly, and looked at her, and she saw, in the held second before he looked away, that he had heard the verb and understood where the verb sat and what it was waiting for.

She did not say anything. She did not need to. The half-awake hum across the water rose, paused, fell. A brigantine bell rang the watch. Citlal, from inside the annex, cleared her throat to remind Malinalli that she had not eaten since noon.

Malinalli walked in the wake of giants. She was helping to make giants now, and she would, in the morning, walk in the wake of those too. The conquest had become a routine, and the routine was its own horror, and the horror was that one could live inside it, eat fish, mend a bracket, conjugate a verb, without the routine ever pausing to acknowledge what it was. She set her hand briefly on the curve of her belly, where the second heartbeat lived, and listened, across the water, to the first.

It began the first week of May, the evenings outside her annex when the siege settled into its rhythm. Bernardo would come after the evening meal, carrying his breviary and a small folding stool, and Malinalli would bring two bowls of tepid chocolate and a plate of the dried fish the Tlaxcalans salted in their own way, which tasted of lake and smoke in a combination she had grown to find acceptable. The lamp was hers, a clay thing with a wick that burned a steady inch-high flame, and they sat facing each other with the small folding table between them, a scrap of parchment and a reed pen for the Nahuatl, and his breviary for the Latin.

She corrected his mouth on the *tl* cluster the third evening. *Cuauhtli*, eagle. He said it with the Castilian tongue, flat and hard. She made a sound in her throat and showed him how the tongue touched the teeth and the air slipped through the side of the mouth. He tried again. Again. By the fourth attempt he had it and he smiled at her in a way that was new, a smile that had nothing to do with command or rank or anything he had learned in Spain. He had been a scholar before he had been a friar, and the scholar part of him was waking up in the grammar.

He corrected her on the ablative of *vita* the sixth evening. She had said *cum vita* when she should have said *cum vitâ*, the long final *a*, and he showed her how the accent mark changed the vowel's weight, the way the Latin made weight out of nothing but a mark. She repeated it three times until the accent felt natural in her mouth, and he smiled back, the same scholar-smile, and she realized with a small sharp jolt that they had found something, the two of them, that neither of them had expected.

They had done this in Ch 11, the wonder and the curiosity, the two people in the palace gardens who had been told they were on opposite sides and had discovered they were both people who read books. They had done this in Ch 20, when it had broken, when she had said the thing she should not have said about what the engines ran on and he had said the thing he should not have said about what the Inquisition was actually for, and they had both walked away into the dark that night without speaking. They were doing it now, months later, and it was the same activity and it was not the same activity, because they both knew now what they knew, and they were doing it anyway, in full knowledge, which made the grammar something else again, something that had its own shape.

He came on the evening of the twelfth of June with the breviary and the stool, and the lake-mist was at knee-height and the hum from the Templo Mayor was a pressure under the breastbone that she had been carrying for three months. She had the table out, the lamp lit, the chocolate cooling in its bowls. He sat and unrolled a fresh scrap of parchment. She corrected him on *tlacatecolotl*, the war-drums, because he kept making the *tl* flat and the *c* soft, and he laughed at himself and tried again. He corrected her on the genitive of *sacerdos*, the priest, which she kept saying wrong, and she repeated it four times until the *e* and the *i* and the *s* fell into their places.

It was the small pleasures of grammar that kept them doing it, she saw now, the way a verb sat at the end of a Nahuatl clause and waited, the way an accent mark made a vowel heavy with meaning. They were both people who had spent their lives translating, both of them, she from Nahuatl to Maya to Spanish and he from Latin to Castilian to the languages of the missions, and the grammar was the one place where neither of them had an advantage, where the rules were the rules and the rules applied to both of them. It was a place of equality, which was a thing she had never known in her life, not when she had been traded at eight, not when she had been given at sixteen, not when she had been carried in a litter through a Spanish camp that was not hers.

He began to say the other things the twelfth evening, or perhaps it was the thirteenth, she did not count, because the days were all the same and the evenings were all the same and the only thing that marked time was the hum from across the water, rising and falling like a slow lung. He was thinking, she could see it in the way his hands moved when he thought, the way they flexed on the table edge, the way the thumb rubbed the forefinger. He was thinking about the Castilian arm-bone in *San Esteban del Río*, the one in the new monstrance bracket that had been welded and prayed over and welded again. He was thinking about the Franciscan cord at his belt, the coarse brown cord that was the emblem of the Order of Poverty, of the saint who had nothing but a body.

"*Quid debet sacerdos civitati*," he said, half to her, half to the lamp. *What does a priest owe a city.*

She gave him the Nahuatl, slowly, so he would hear the verb at the end. *Tlein quitlacuilia teopixqui in altepetl*. He repeated it twice, correctly, and looked at her, and she saw the thing that was waking up in him, the thing that had been waking up in him for months, and she did not stop him, because she knew, with the knowledge of a woman who had been traded and given and carried and chosen, what he was going to say.

He said the other things in Latin, partly because Latin was the language of the theology he was unpacking, partly because saying them in Spanish would have been unbearable. He talked about whether a willing death could be a sacrament if it was also fuel. He talked about the consent of a martyr, what that actually structurally permitted, what the difference was between a body that died and a body that chose to die. He talked about a Reliquary's monstrance charged not by a centuries-old relic but by a freshly informed willing death, and he said the words *voluntas*, *consensus*, *sacramentum*, in a voice that was very steady, and he was not looking at her, and he was not looking at the lake, and he was looking at the thing that was forming inside him.

She heard every word. She did not ask him to stop. She corrected his Nahuatl on a separate clause, the genitive of *ixiptla*, the god's body, which he had said wrong, and she made him repeat it three times until the *x* and the *tl* and the *a* fell into their places. She let the Latin run. She let him say the thing that was forming inside him, in front of her, in a language she now understood well enough to audit, and she did not write anything down, because the writing of it would have been its own betrayal.

The next morning he would come to her with reddened eyes and ask what they had spoken of, and she would say *the conjugation of the verb to give* and he would nod and not press her, and by the third or fourth such morning he had stopped asking, which was worse. She had been the only witness to the construction of the martyrdom, and she would be the only witness to the martyrdom itself, and she did not know, on the twelfth of June, whether the witness was a thing that made her whole or a thing that made her nothing.

Outside the annex the siege moved on its own logic. A Tlaxcalan boy ran past with a message tube. A horse coughed somewhere down the line. The lamp between them guttered once and steadied. He was looking at the lake, at the place across the water where the sound came from. She watched his hands. They were a priest's hands, thin, the knuckles dry, and they were not shaking, which she had learned to find more disturbing than shaking would have been.

She set her hand briefly on the curve of her belly, where the second heartbeat lived, and listened, across the water, to the first. The hum rose. The hum fell. The grammar waited.

Her right hand cramped. She set the pen down. The list had taken the entire afternoon to write and there were only eleven words on it.

Lamp lit. Stool placed. The folding table set with a parchment that was not the grammar parchment. From the canvas wall of her annex she could hear, distantly, the hum from across the water, thinned tonight, by the twenty-eighth of June, not gone, only stretched the way a held note stretches before it breaks.

Nahuatl words in her own hand. The words a priest would need to administer last rites to an indigenous Christian during and after the fall of the city. Death. Soul. Mercy. Forgiveness. Peace. Body. Blood. Pain. Fear. Hope. The words for God in all the forms the Nahuatl had, the old god, the new god, the god who was both.

She had written them deliberately. She knew what he was going to do. She knew what he would need. If she did not give him the words he would not have them when the moment arrived; if she did give him the words he would use them; if he used them he would be doing the thing he had been forming in Latin for six weeks; and the thing he had been forming would kill him. She would be the only witness. The witness would shape the rest of her life, make her whole in a way she had never been whole, or hollow her in a way she had never been hollow. The choice was hers. It was the hardest choice she had ever made, and she had made it before he arrived.

Bernardo came. He sat. He unrolled the parchment and saw the words. He looked at her, and she saw in his face that he understood what she had done. He did not ask. He did not thank her. He picked up the reed pen and began to read the words aloud, slowly, testing the weight of each one in his mouth, the way a man tests the weight of a sword before he draws it.

*Death.* *Miquiztli.*

*Soul.* *Teyolia.*

*Mercy.* *Tlaocoliliztli.*

*Forgiveness.* *Tlapohpolhuiliztli.*

*Peace.* *Tlamatcayeliztli.*

*Body.* *Nacayotl.*

*Blood.* *Eztli.*

*Pain.* *Cocoliztli.*

*Fear.* *Mauhcayotl.*

*Hope.* *Tlachixcayeliztli.*

*God.* *Teotl.*

He read them all. He read them aloud. He read them slowly, as if memorizing them for a ritual he would have to perform soon, and he was, and she was, and they both knew it, and they both did not say it. The grammar waited. The hum across the water rose and fell. The lamp's flame stood very still in the lake-air, because the air at evening did not move.

When he had finished the list he looked at her, and he thanked her in Nahuatl. He thanked her accurately. The verb landed in its proper place at the close of the phrase. The *tl* held as a single consonant rather than the cluster every Spaniard wanted to make of it. The *x* came out as the soft sibilant she had drilled into him for two weeks. The penultimate stress fell where it ought to fall on every word of the formula. He had been a clumsy student a month ago and a serviceable one a fortnight ago; tonight he sounded like a man who had decided that nothing he said again in this language would be wasted.

*Tlazohcamati,* he said, in Nahuatl, accurately. *Thank you for the words.*

She said nothing. She let him thank her. She let him continue the lesson.

Then she asked him, in Latin, to walk her through the *commendatio animae*, the prayer for a soul leaving the body, including the variant forms a confessor used for a martyr versus a layperson. She asked him slowly. She wanted him to hear the Latin in his own request, to hear the weight of it, the shape of it, the sound of it. She wanted him to hear the thing forming inside him spoken back to him by the only voice in the camp he had not yet stopped trusting.

He did. He walked her through it in full. The *Proficiscere, anima Christiana, de hoc mundo*. The intercessions, in order. The variant for a martyr, shorter, harder, no plea for mercy on sins, because the blood was its own absolution. The variant for a layperson, longer, gentler, the saints called by name. She listened. She wrote nothing down. She did not want this on parchment in any tent in the camp. She wanted the words in her head, in her mouth, in her chest, in her hands, in her bones, in her blood, anywhere they could not be confiscated.

He finished. He looked at her. He reached across the table and rested his hand briefly on her wrist, the only physical touch they had ever exchanged, and he said, in Nahuatl: *thank you for not asking me to stop.*

She said, in Latin: *I am not in the business of stopping anyone tonight.*

He smiled. He gathered his breviary. He walked back toward the Franciscan tent.

She sat with the lamp until it burned out. Outside the canvas the hum from across the water rose and fell, rose and fell, the city refusing to be quiet even in its starvation. Somewhere a brigantine creaked at its mooring. A horse shifted in the picket line and was still. The lamp guttered. She did not refill it. When the wick went black she stayed in the dark a while longer, palms flat on the table, learning by feel where the parchment ended and the wood began.

The convergence was complete. The *commendatio animae* was the prayer Andrés would say over Bernardo in the templo plaza six weeks hence. Her refusal to dissuade was the *power within* of Tlaxcala enacted in its hardest specific case, not the council-floor abstraction but a list of eleven words in her own hand for a man she had decided not to save. The touch on the wrist was the only intimacy their thread would ever hold, earned because they had stopped pretending to each other and could not now go back to pretending. The lamp burned out. The hum from across the water rose and fell. The grammar waited.
