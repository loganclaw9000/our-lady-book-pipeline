The morning air in the sacred precinct smells of copal smoke and lake brine. Thirty Teōmecahuītlī kneel at the base of the Templo Mayor. Their metal is warm from the sun, though the sun is not yet high enough to be hot. Itzcoatl's engine, *Obsidian Mirror*, kneels among them, its obsidian-gilt surface drinking the light rather than reflecting it. The scrying-mirror on its chest is real obsidian, polished until Itzcoatl can see his own face distorted in it when he looks down from his cockpit.

He is not the pilot in this moment. The engine is not yet awake. He has bathed in the cold water of the designated spring. He has fasted for the required twelve hours. He has memorized the teoxicalli over three years of lessons with a teōmachtiani priest, the classical register of Nahuatl that feels like a different language from the vernacular he uses to buy food in the market. Now he waits.

The calmecac priests at the pyramid's base are intoning the maintenance liturgy. The sound is a low hum, felt more than heard, that seems to emanate from the stone itself. The Great Engine is not awake. It never is, not fully. But Itzcoatl can feel its presence the way he feels the presence of a sleeping giant in the same room: the air is heavier here, charged with potential. When the Great Engine does wake, the lake will rise. The causeways will crack.

For now, the smaller engines kneel upward toward it. Their joints lock into place at the pitch shift that signals the beginning of the drill. The sound is like a giant insect clicking its legs. *Obsidian Mirror* rises. Itzcoatl rises with it, though his body is still seated in the cockpit behind the teōcalli-chest.

The pilot is not him. Not entirely. He has recited the teoxicalli, and in the recitation something has opened. He is not Itzcoatl anymore. He is the mirror. He is the smoke. He is the jaguar in the night.

The drill is formal, precise. Forward, backward, turn left, turn right, raise weapon, lower weapon, strike, parry, strike again. The movements are not Itzcoatl's. They are the deity's. Tezcatlipoca knows what a drill requires. Tezcatlipoca knows how to move the engine so the ocelot-motif pauldrons turn at the right angles, so the scrying-mirror chest catches the light at the right moments.

Itzcoatl watches his own engine from inside. He sees the other engines in the plaza: Huitzilopochtli-class giants, solar-gilt masks gleaming, their scale massive compared to *Obsidian Mirror*'s fifty feet. He sees the smaller specialists, Tlaloc-class with their turquoise masks, Mictlantecuhtli engines with their skull-painted surfaces. He sees, in his mind's eye, the Tlaxcalan engines that are not here. He should know their names. He should know their pilots. He does not.

The drill ends. The formation breaks.

Then the runner comes.

Itzcoatl sees him through the scrying-mirror: a man in the plaza below, running with the gait of someone who has run too far too fast, his breath visible even though it is not cold. The man is shouting. The calmecac priests stop their liturgy. The other engines go still.

By the time Itzcoatl has dismounted, a young scribe from the calmecac stands before him, pale-faced, sweating despite the morning chill. The scribe speaks in a voice that is not his own: the voice of the runner who is no longer with him.

*Tlaxcala has flipped.*

The other pilots are looking at one another. They are calculating. They are afraid.

*Not just allied,* the scribe continues. *Sent their teōmachtiani to march with the Spanish.*

The word *Spanish* is a word no one in Tenochtitlan has spoken aloud before. A word from the coast. A word from the north. A word that does not belong in the sacred precinct.

*They are coming.*

The scribe's eyes glaze over. He falls to his knees as the runner who was him is released. Itzcoatl catches him before he hits the ground.

Teōmecahuītlī fighting for foreigners. Against Nahua. Against the Triple Alliance.

Something has broken.

The council chamber is cold. The walls are stone. The floor is stone. The light is stone gray, the color of an overcast sky pressing down from above, even though the chamber has no windows, only a roof of stone beams and flagstones underfoot. Itzcoatl sits on the floor. He does not know if he is supposed to. He knows only that he is a provincial, and that his seat is at the back of the room.

He keeps his head bowed. He looks at his own hands. They are stained with obsidian flake, which is what teōmachtiani hands look like after a drill. His uncle is the engine's warrant-holder. The family seat is not in Tenochtitlan. He is here for the drill cycle. He has not expected to be in a council.

The men around him are not teōmachtiani. They are *teōcīhuānōtli*, lords of the priesthood. They are *tlahtoāni*, rulers. They are *pochteca* who have grown rich enough to be considered nobles. They know the protocol.

Moctezuma is at the center. Itzcoatl sees him only as a gap in the circle of men, one is not supposed to look directly at the tlatoani. He hears him only as the sound of the room itself, the way stone hums when the wind hits it at the right angle.

The debate is whether to attack the Spanish on the march, or to invite them into the city and assess.

Itzcoatl sits silent. He is not supposed to speak. He does not know what he would say if he were allowed to speak. He knows only what he knows, which is: teōmecahuītlī fighting for foreigners, against Nahua. Something has broken.

The debate moves in circles. Arguments about honor. Arguments about timing. Arguments about whether the Spanish are gods or men. Arguments about whether it matters. Arguments about whether Quetzalcoatl has returned, or whether the Spanish are only men who have learned to look like gods, or whether the distinction matters.

The hours pass. Itzcoatl is half-dozing when he hears the priest mention the seals.

*"...the seals at Cholula and Tula, they are not to be disturbed. The Quetzalcoatl engines, "*

His head snaps up. He has heard the word before, from his uncle, from the teōmachtiani who trained him, from the scribes who wrote the codices in the calmecac. *Quetzalcoatl.*

The priest is not speaking to him. He is speaking to Moctezuma. But Itzcoatl hears him anyway.

*", dormant for generations. The elders say they are not to be awakened. The elders say that if they wake, they wake for the wrong side, and the continent ends."*

The room goes silent.

Then Moctezuma speaks. The voice from the center of the circle is not loud. It does not need to be. It is the voice of a man who has decided.

*"We will not march. We will not attack on the road."*

A pause. The stone hum of the chamber.

*"Send envoys. Gold, feathers, the proper gifts. Tell the strangers we welcome them. Bring them across the pass between the volcanoes. Bring them down through Cholula. Bring them into the city, where we can see them, where we can measure them, where the Great Engine stands above them."*

Another pause.

*"If they are gods, we will know. If they are men, we will know. The seals will hold. They have held for generations. They will hold for one more season."*

The lords assent in the formal manner. The priest who spoke of the seals lowers his head and does not raise it again.

Itzcoatl looks at his stained hands. The decision has been made by men in rooms he was not meant to enter, on a calculation he was not meant to weigh. The Spanish will come across the pass. They will come down through Cholula, where the dormant engines sleep. They will come into Tenochtitlan.

The Triple Alliance has not decided to fight. It has decided to look.

He understands, sitting on the cold stone floor of a chamber whose protocol he does not know, that the looking is itself a kind of breaking. A war engine that does not move when the moment requires it does not stay still. It loosens. The metaphysical word for what has happened is one Itzcoatl does not yet have. The plain word is *coming apart*.

Outside, the morning's drill is already a ritual of a vanished world.

The drill is in its third hour when the runner comes.

*Obsidian Mirror* is mid-pivot, Itzcoatl half a beat behind the formation, when he sees the small figure cross the plaza at a pace no one crosses the sacred precinct at. Not running. The precinct does not permit running. Walking very fast, the way a priest walks when the news he carries permits no slower gait. The runner's path goes to the calmecac steward at the southwest corner of the formation. The steward leans down. The runner speaks. The steward does not move his face.

Itzcoatl, fifty feet up in the cockpit of his engine, sees this. He keeps his hands on the controls. Tezcatlipoca knows how to keep hands on the controls.

The pitch shift comes thirty seconds later.

He has heard the Great Engine's low hum every drill of his three years. He has heard it the way a man hears his own breath: present, unattended, a baseline. The hum has a register. The register is the register. It does not change.

It changes now.

A half-step up. Pitch tightening, the way a string tightens when the peg turns one notch past its set point. Not loud. Not visible to anyone outside the formation. But every priest-pilot in the thirty-engine corps feels it through the soles of their feet, through the seats of their cockpits, through the bones of their jaws. The Templo Mayor, which has been sleeping the same sleep for a generation, has just opened one eye.

Itzcoatl's body knows before his mind does. The hairs on his forearms rise. His hands stay on the controls.

He looks, very carefully, at the Huitzilopochtli engine to his left. The pilot is a senior teōmachtiani named Cuetzpalin, twenty years in the corps. Cuetzpalin's engine continues the drill movement on the count. Lower the macuahuitl. Step. Pivot. Lift. Cuetzpalin's helm does not turn toward the calmecac steward. Cuetzpalin's helm does not turn toward the Templo Mayor. Cuetzpalin's engine moves as though nothing has happened, except that Itzcoatl, watching for it, can see the joints have gone a quarter-degree tighter at the shoulder. The Huitzilopochtli is not at parade alertness now. It is at combat alertness, executed in the shape of parade.

Itzcoatl sees it because he has been told to see things like it. Tezcatlipoca pilots are taught to see what other pilots will not show.

The news passes mouth-to-mouth across the formation. Steward to first-rank pilot. First-rank to second. The order of the relay is the order of seniority. Itzcoatl is junior. He hears it sixth.

*Tlaxcala has flipped. Their teōmachtiani march with the Spanish.*

He keeps his hands on the controls. He executes the next movement on the count. He does not look at the Templo Mayor. He does not look at Cuetzpalin. The drill continues. Lower. Step. Pivot. Lift.

Tlaxcala. The blood enemy. The altepetl that has fed the Flower Wars for four generations, that has supplied the bones of half the Huitzilopochtli engines in this very plaza. Tlaxcala has put its teōmachtiani in formation alongside foreigners. Mexica engines, Mexica deity-housings, fueled by Nahua dead, fighting against Nahua.

The hum tightens another quarter-step.

Itzcoatl watches his own hands on the controls and notes that they are still steady. He notes this with a detachment that is itself a kind of fear. Tezcatlipoca is keeping the hands steady. He is not.

The drill does not end on a command. It ends on its own. The senior priest at the formation's head, a man named Acatl, fifty-some, the warrant-holder of the corps, raises his engine's right arm one count early. The other engines fall in behind the gesture as though it were the ordained sequence. Forward to cradles. Walk, do not march. Each engine to its assigned bay along the eastern wall of the staging ground. *Obsidian Mirror* takes thirty steps and kneels.

No one has issued an order. Every engine has obeyed the same unspoken one.

Itzcoatl recites the closing teoxicalli. The deity withdraws, the way smoke withdraws into a vent when the wind shifts. He is himself again. He becomes aware that he is sweating through his ritual cotton, which a man in deity-state does not do. He becomes aware that his jaw is locked.

He climbs down the cradle ladder. His hands are shaking by the third rung. He grips harder. They shake harder. He reaches the flagstones and waits with one hand on the ladder rail before he trusts himself to walk.

Cuetzpalin passes him on the way to the priests' antechamber. Cuetzpalin does not look at him. Cuetzpalin's hand brushes Itzcoatl's elbow once, the formal-corps gesture for *be still, junior, the older men will manage this.* Then he is past.

Itzcoatl follows the corps in silence.
