The candle on Bernardo's desk had burned down to a nub, the wax pooling around it like a small lake in miniature. Malintzin watched the flame gutter and die, then watched as Bernardo, with a sigh she could hear through the thin wall of his cell, relit it with a taper. He had been at the table with her for three hours. Four, if she counted the stretch when he had sent her for more parchment and she had taken the opportunity to sit in the courtyard and watch the Tlaxcalan women who worked the brigantine frames.

They worked in the light of torches, their shadows long and liquid on the adobe walls, and she had watched them for twenty minutes before she went back in. They did not see her. They were Tlaxcalan and she was Totonac and the Spanish had made them useful to each other in ways that neither side quite understood. She understood them.

Now Bernardo was at the table again. The candle had been relit. He was rubbing his eyes with both hands, the knuckles white, and when he looked up at her his face had a look she had never seen on it before. Not the look of the man who taught her Latin, nor the look of the man who had wept over Alvarado's orders at the Templo Mayor, nor the look of the man who had stood on the causeway during the retreat and watched a hundred of his brothers die.

This was the look of a man who had stopped guarding what he said.

"*Reddere*," he said, and the word was tired. "*To render*. To give back. To return." He wrote it in Latin on the parchment, then beneath it, in Nahuatl, the phrase she had given him an hour before. She watched his hand, the small tremor in it that had been there for weeks and which he hid from everyone but her.

"The bone," he said, and he did not look up. "When a heretic dies. When a martyr dies."

She waited. He had been teaching her the Latin word for bone for weeks. *Os*. She had learned it. She had not asked why he was so interested in the word.

"The Church keeps it." He wrote the word *os* on the parchment, then the word *sanctum*, holy, beside it. "It is not just a memory. It is not just a story. It *works*. It feeds the Reliquary. The saint in the monstrance needs fuel, Malintzin. You know this. You have seen it."

She had. She had watched Andrés pilot *La Niña de Córdoba* and she had watched the other Reliquaries on the campaign, and she had watched the Mexica engines, the *teōmecahuītlī*, and she had understood that they worked, however they worked, and she had never asked. She had never been in a position to ask.

"I have been at three auto-da-fés," Bernardo said, and his voice was flat, the way a man's voice is when he has decided to tell a thing he has never told anyone. "Three. The bones are kept. They are not buried. They are not burned. They are kept. The saint takes them. The saint *feeds*."

He looked up at her then, and his eyes were not the eyes of the man who had taught her Latin. They were the eyes of a man who had just confessed a murder he had not known he was committing.

She did not change her face. She did not change the angle of her hands on the parchment. She let him speak. She let him say it.

"The willing death," he said, and he was trying now, trying to walk it back, trying to put the theology on top of the thing he had just said to make it clean. "The consent. The martyr chooses. The bone is given willingly. That is why it works. The saint receives what is given. Not what is taken. Never what is taken."

He trailed off. He was tired. He had been tired for months. She had watched him watching the smallpox take the children in the camp and not say a word. She had watched him watching the Tlaxcalan priests and not asking the questions he wanted to ask. She had watched him, and she had been watching him for a long time.

"*Os sanctum*," she said, and the Latin was clean. She had learned it from him. "Holy bone."

He nodded once. He looked away. He stood up and he was shaking, just a little, in a way that she had never seen him shake before.

"I should go to bed," he said. "Good night, Malintzin."

"Good night, Fray Bernardo."

He went to his cell. She watched the candle in his room go out a minute later. She did not move. She sat at the table with the parchment in front of her, with the word *os* and the word *sanctum* and the word *reddere* written on it in his hand.

She had been carrying another account for weeks. A Mexica priest, old and dying of the slow weight of his own years, had been brought to the Tlaxcalan workshops under guard, and she had been asked to translate for him because he spoke a dialect close to hers and because no one else could understand him. He had told her, in a voice that was already half gone, what an *ixiptla* acceptance did. He had said that the god took the bone. He had said that the god *fed*. He had said it the way Bernardo had just said it, the way a man describes plumbing.

She had filed the account beside the others she carried. The accounts of the Reliquaries. The accounts of the engines. The accounts of the dead.

She had not known, until tonight, that they were the same.

The candle on the table guttered and died. She did not relight it. She sat in the dark and she did not move and she did not sleep. The accounts were side by side now. They were the same account. The conquest was not a clash of civilizations. It was one machine grinding against itself in two dialects.

She would keep translating. She did not know why she knew that. She just knew it.

Outside, the Tlaxcalan women were still bent to the ribs of the brigantine hulls. Their torches had thinned to coals. The night had thickened around them and they did not look up, and the work went on without witness, the way the other work, the work she had just understood, had gone on without witness for a thousand years on both sides of an ocean that nobody had thought to cross.

Her milk had come down twice on the walk. She felt the cotton dampen and tighten against her ribs, and she did not stop to adjust it. The granary stood at the lane's end where it had always stood, emptied of corn now and holding one dying man, and the guards nodded her through without asking what she carried or why she came alone.

The smell of dry husks lay under everything like an older language. Two women had been there in the morning to wash him; they were gone. The Tlaxcalans had stopped asking her things weeks ago. The Spanish, too. Most camps did, eventually.

She wore Nahua dress today. Cotton huipil, loose at the breast for the nursing. Skirt the color of dried husks. Not the dress she had worn before the Spanish, that one had been burned at Veracruz with a great many other things, but the cut of it, the weight of it, was the same. She had walked out of the Spanish quarter in it without comment. They had stopped commenting on most of what she did.

The priest opened his eyes when she knelt. The whites were yellow.

"*Malinalli*," he said.

It was the name her father had used. *Grass*. The Spanish had heard it as *Marina* and the Nahua as *Malintzin*, and somewhere between the two the original syllables had gone missing, except in the mouths of old men who no longer cared who was listening.

"I brought water."

He drank. A little. Most of it ran down his chin and into the cloth she had spread on the pallet, and she dabbed at it because that was what one did. He watched her hands while she dabbed. He had spent sixty years watching hands at altars and he watched hers the same way, neutrally, without preference.

"You came to ask," he said.

"Yes."

"About the great engines."

"Yes."

He was quiet a long while. She thought he had drifted. Then his lips moved.

"What you have heard is true," he said. "I will say it once. The lesser engines feed on bone. Warrior bone, given. They run because the giving was clean. The great engines, the ones in the mountains, the ones at Cholula and Tula, those do not feed on bone."

She waited.

"Those feed on consent. On the knower. An *ixiptla* who has learned the god into himself and walked up the pyramid in his own skin and said *yes* with his last breath. The bone is housing. The seal eats what was given. A man can be coerced to walk up the steps. He cannot be coerced to mean it. The seal knows."

He coughed. Pink at the corner of his mouth. She wiped it.

"I was at Cholula once," he said, as though this had been the next sentence all along. "Forty years ago. A young teōpixqui sent to inspect the seal. I stood on the seal-stone an afternoon and felt nothing. That was the proof. Nothing was meant to be felt. The seal was good. The seal is still good. It is the apparatus around the seal that has gone hollow, quietly, generationally, because for four generations there have been no knowers willing in the right way, and we have agreed among ourselves not to notice."

"Why are you telling me."

His eyes moved. Not far. Just to the dampened place at her huipil and back to her face.

"Because I am dying. Because the children of Tenochtitlan are dying with the *huey cocoliztli* and there will be no one left to tell. Because you came in your mother's dress and asked in your father's tongue. Pick whichever reason suits you. They are all true."

She sat with that.

"The *tlamatinime* at Texcoco," she said carefully. "The ones with Itzcoatl's people. Would they say what you have said?"

"They are saying it now. We agreed a year ago to stop guarding the seal. There is nothing left to guard."

Outside, down the lane, a child was being scolded for something in Tlaxcalan Nahuatl, and the cadence of the scolding reached the granary and then stopped. The priest closed his eyes. He did not open them again while she was there.

She stayed until the breathing changed. It did not halt, only shifted into the slower pattern she had heard twice before in her life, in women who had been her family. She laid the cloth on his forehead. She left the water within reach of his hand though the hand would not move. She walked out into the noon.

The market was the long way back to the Spanish camp. She took it.

She took it because she needed the time, and because the short way ran past the brigantine yards where Andrés would be, and she did not want Andrés yet, and because the market was loud and a person could think inside loud.

A woman selling chiles called out a price. A boy threading past a porter knocked her elbow and apologized in two languages without looking at her. Pulque came up from a stall and then copal from the next one over, and underneath all of it the iron smell of the brigantine forges drifting up from the south end where the Tlaxcalan crews were shaping ribs for the lake-boats Cortés meant to float on Texcoco.

She walked with one arm crossed lightly over her chest, against the ache the milk left when it came in.

What Bernardo had told her the week before, in the same language she used to translate his confessor's Latin into Castilian for his own confession, was this: that the Inquisition extracted *consent* from its subjects before burning them. Not confession alone. Consent to die. A penitent's *yes*. The auto-da-fé without that *yes* was a flawed engine; the *yes* was what fed the relic beneath the cathedral and kept the stone walking. He had said it shaking. He had been unable to look at her while he said it.

The priest in the granary had said it without shaking and without looking away. Different temple, different word for *yes*, same machine.

Two systems. Two oceans. The same fuel. Both pretending, through different theologies, with different vestments, in different scripts, that the fuel was something else. Bone. Bread. Water. Fire.

Consent.

She passed a stall where a Tlaxcalan woman was selling small clay figures of saints alongside small clay figures of older gods, on the same blanket, the same prices, no distinction made when she quoted them. The woman caught Malintzin's eye for half a second and looked away.

By the time she reached the edge of the market the shape of it had settled in her. It would not unsettle. It was the kind of understanding that arrives once and then stays, the way a name stays once spoken.

What she did with it was the question.

She could tell Cortés. He would refuse to believe her, and if he believed her he would weaponize it, and the weapon would hit Mexica and Spanish in the same stroke and she could not yet see whose side it would land harder on. She could tell Itzcoatl through Xochitl. He probably already knew. She could tell Bernardo, who would weep again and add it to whatever ledger he kept inside himself that the Franciscans had been unable to take from him.

She could stop translating. Walk into the sierra. Become a woman in a village no one would write down.

The thought had texture. She gave it a moment.

Then she set it aside, because she had had four words of Spanish in February of 1519 and she had something near a hundred now, and because the chain *Aguilar, her* had become *her, Cortés* in nine months and would become *her alone* in six more, and because at the hinge of two machines there was exactly one job, and it was hers, and she had not arrived at it by accident.

She would keep at it.

The certainty was not a feeling. It was a fact about the geometry of where she was standing.

She passed under the Spanish gate. The sentry, a Castilian boy from Medellín who had learned to nod at her without quite meeting her eye, nodded at her without quite meeting her eye. She put a hand on the gatepost and stepped over the threshold.

Inside the camp, somewhere, Andrés would be at *San Esteban*'s feet, going through the manual flat, on duty, the way she had heard him go through it, without the cadence pilots used once a saint had become theirs. Somewhere Bernardo would be writing. Somewhere Cortés would be dictating to the king; the letters always came out the same shape, a long preamble, a shorter middle, a request at the end she had to read twice to find.

She went to find Bernardo first, because his Nahuatl primer was still on the table where she had left it in the morning, and because the lesson she had planned for the afternoon was the conjugation of the verb *to give*.

The hammers had not stopped since vespers. Three slips over, the Tlaxcalan women were ribbing the seventh hull by lamp-light, and the iron rang in pairs, strike, strike, then a pause, then strike, strike, because two crews worked opposing keels on a shared rhythm. Malintzin walked into that rhythm and let it carry her.

She passed the long shed where the brigantine planks were soaking. Pine pitch and wet rope. Beyond it, the engine sheds: deeper smell, oil and cold metal and the faint copper that always sat under a Reliquary in cradle. *San Esteban del Río* stood at the back with one shoulder-plate dropped open, the new monstrance bracket bolted and unbolted three times in two days. The Castilian arm-bone in its housing did not glow. Cradled engines never did. The cockpit lamp was lit. Andrés had taken to sleeping in the cradle since the engine began to dream of him.

She climbed.

Thirty rungs. She had counted them three nights running without setting foot on the ladder. Tonight her hands did the counting for her. By twelve her back complained. By twenty her palms had begun to slip on the iron, sweat or fear, she did not stop to tell which. The small obstinate weight under her ribs, she had not announced it to anyone, made each rung a calculation she made and discarded. She did not pause. She did not give the climb to anyone watching.

Andrés saw her crown the lip of the cockpit and did not stand. He moved his blanket aside and made space on the gunner's seat. He had not shaved. His Latin breviary lay open on his knee at a page he had not turned in some time.

"Buenas noches," she said.

"Buenas noches."

She took the seat. She put her hands flat on her thighs. She did not soften her face.

"I have two things to tell you. I am going to tell you the first and then the second and then what they mean. Do not interrupt me until I am finished."

He closed the breviary. He set it aside as if setting down a knife. He nodded.

"Fray Bernardo told me, eight nights ago, what an auto-da-fé produces. He has been at three. The bones are kept. They are not buried, they are not burned. The Church takes them and the saint takes them and the saint *feeds*. He used the word *reddere*. He used the word *os sanctum*. He said the consent of the martyr is what makes the bone work. He said the saint receives what is given, never what is taken." She watched his face. He did not move. "He did not know what he was telling me until the next morning. By then it was too late to take back."

Andrés's breath went out slowly through his teeth.

"I have not finished."

He lifted his hand a little, go on.

"Six weeks before Bernardo, a Mexica priest was brought into the workshops under guard. Old. Dying of his own years. They asked me to translate because his dialect was close to mine. He told me what an *ixiptla* acceptance does. He said the god takes the bone. He said the god *feeds*. He said the willingness of the offering is what makes the engine wake. He said the deity receives what is given, never what is taken." She let the air settle. "He used the same plumbing, Andrés. Different language. Same plumbing."

The cockpit was very quiet. Two sheds away the hammers thinned, the night taking the women off one by one.

"They are the same account. The Reliquary is the *teōmecahuītlī*. The *teōmecahuītlī* is the Reliquary. The metaphysics on both sides of the ocean run on consent, on the willing death, on the bone of the one who chose. There is no clash of theologies. There is one machine, and we are speaking it in two dialects."

He asked her, and his voice was not the voice she knew, "Say it again. The Mexica word."

"*Ixiptla.*"

"The phrase, the exact phrase he used."

"*The deity receives what is given.* The Nahuatl was simpler than that. Nearer to *the god eats consent.*"

"And Bernardo's."

"*The consent is what the seal eats.*"

He closed his eyes. She watched the muscle in his jaw work twice.

"And the bone."

"The bone is the housing. He said that in Spanish. *El hueso es la vasija.* The Mexica priest said *the bone is the bowl the god drinks from.* I am giving you this exactly."

A long breath through his nose. "Go on."

"*The willingness of the martyr.*"

"Both," she said, before he could ask. "In different words. Both."

He opened his eyes. He was looking past her, through the cockpit slit at the dark.

"How long have you held this."

"Bernardo, eight days. The priest, six weeks before that."

"And you came to me."

"I came to you."

"Why me."

She had thought about that. She had thought about it for the eight days.

"Because you carried Flora. Because *La Niña de Córdoba* was your engine before this cradle, and her bones are at the bottom of Lake Texcoco, and you have not stopped praying to a child whose ossuary is mud. Because the Mexica priest told me what we are doing to children, and Bernardo told me what we are doing to children, and you are the only Spanish pilot in this camp who knew the name of the saint he flew before he knew his own. You will hear both halves of the sentence. The others will put their hands over their ears."

He did not answer. He bent forward and put his face in his hands. He stayed that way long enough that she counted his breaths. They did not slow.

When he lifted his face it was wet. He did not apologize.

"I need to be alone," he said.

"I know."

"Don't, please, "

"I will not tell anyone else. Not yet."

He inclined his head once. He gathered the breviary and held it against his chest like a wound he was packing. She watched him for one more breath. Then she went down the ladder. Thirty rungs again, slower, and at the bottom she stood holding the iron of the cradle until her hands stopped shaking.

She did not see him for three days.

She watched, because watching was what she did. On the first night his cockpit lamp burned through and the engine did not move. On the second morning Bernardo carried a wooden bowl across the workshop yard, knocked once on the cradle ladder, climbed up, came back with the bowl empty, and said nothing to anyone. On the second night the lamp burned again. On the third morning the lamp was out and the cradle door was open and the cockpit was empty.

She went to the brigantine slip at dawn because that was where she went when she had nowhere else to be. The Tlaxcalan women had the keel of the seventh hull half-ribbed. The channel was still dark. The smell was pine sap and pitch and the iron-and-blood smell of new caulk.

He found her there.

He had shaved. His eyes were the eyes of a man who had not slept and would not sleep soon. He stood beside her at the slip-edge and did not look at her. He looked at the hull.

"What do you intend to do," he said.

She had been ready for the question for three days.

"I intend to keep translating."

He turned his head. He looked at her properly.

"Malintzin, "

"I intend to keep translating."

"Why."

She thought about the answer she had prepared and did not give it. The prepared answer was for someone who needed to be persuaded. He did not need to be persuaded.

"Because I am the only one who hears both halves. Because if I stop, the machine grinds without anyone in the room who knows what it is. Because I do not yet know what to do with this and I will not stop moving while I decide. Because the day I stop, someone else will say the words, and they will say them wrong."

He took that in. He did not nod.

"You know what they will use you for."

"I do."

"You know what *I* may use you for."

"I have known that since Cempoala."

He looked back at the hull. The hammers had started up again on the next slip. A woman's voice called something in Tlaxcalan, and another woman answered.

"I cannot stop you," he said.

"I am not asking you to."

"No."

A silence. Not the cockpit silence. A working silence, with iron in it.

"Then we keep doing what we are doing. And you tell me when you have decided what to do with what you know."

"That is the agreement."

"And I will tell you when I have decided what to do about Flora. Whatever a man can decide about a saint at the bottom of a lake."

She did not answer that. There was no answer to it that would not be cruel.

He left the slip without saying goodbye. She stayed. The sun had not cleared the workshop roofs, and the channel water was still black, and the new ribs of the hull stood up from it like the bones of something the lake had not finished giving back. She put one hand on the small weight under her ribs. She kept it there until the hammers found their rhythm.

She would keep translating. She had said it twice now. The second saying had made it true.
