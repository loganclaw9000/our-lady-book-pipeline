The hall ran longer than any room she had stood in. Mexica nobles to her left, Spanish captains to her right, and herself between, translating, watching the small things no one else seemed to count.

Tribute arrived first. A noble from Texcoco brought jade in three weights, a pectoral the size of a man's palm, a string of beads, a single carved frog, and behind him came feathers, the long quetzal greens that had to be carried two-handed, and gold. The gold came last. It always did. Gold went on a low table beside Cortés, and Cortés smiled the smile he used when he wanted something to look like surprise.

She watched his hand. The right hand drifted to the back of his chair, and three fingers tapped, twice, against the wood. Pedro de Alvarado, four seats down, set his cup down and laid his palm flat on the table. The signal was for weight. They had practiced it on the road from Cempoala, she had seen them practicing, though she had never been told what the taps meant. Tonight she watched the taps reach Alvarado, and Alvarado look at the gold without seeming to look, and she counted: nine pieces on the low table, the smallest of them heavier than her wrist. She translated none of this. She translated the noble's polite phrasing about the friendship of Texcoco and the open road to the coast. She kept her face the face she wore when she translated.

Moctezuma, on his dais, did not eat. He drank water, twice. He watched Cortés receive the gold with an expression she had decided three days ago not to try to read, reading him cost more than knowing him was worth, and she was learning to spend her attention more carefully.

Through the high windows on the western wall she could see, between courses, the bulk of the palace courtyard. Sandoval's engine knelt there. *Santiago del Paso*, eleven stories of riveted iron and gilded plate, head lowered to keep below the roofline, the finger-bone of the apostle locked at its chest under three keys and a lit candle. Sandoval slept in its cockpit when he could not sleep elsewhere. The engine had walked into Tenochtitlan on November 8 and had not moved since. It sat in Axayacatl's courtyard the way a saint's reliquary sits in a side chapel, quiet, present, the room arranged around it. From where she stood she could not hear it; she could feel it, the small shift in the air a large blessed thing makes by being near. Two Tlaxcalan engines crouched at the outer gate, smaller, third-class, their pilots playing a dice game on the paving stones.

Across the sacred precinct, where the great pyramid took up half the night sky, the Mexica answer stood in its house. She had been told its name once and asked not to repeat it. A Huitzilopochtli engine of the first class. She did not need to see it to feel it the way a person feels weather. The whole palace stood inside the pressure of it. The Spanish, she had noticed, slept badly here, and pretended they slept well.

The food came in the order the Mexica gave food at feasts of state: turkey first, then a stew of fish from the lake, then tamales filled with a paste of squash and small dried fish, then chocolate. The chocolate came in lacquered gourds, foamed, bitter, with chile in it and a single petal of vanilla floating. She drank a swallow and her stomach turned the way it had turned every morning for eleven days, and she set the gourd down and did not pick it up again. A Mexica noblewoman two places away noticed the gourd set down. She filed the noticing without acting on it. Malintzin filed the filing.

Eleven days. She counted again because counting was the form thinking took when she could not afford to think in sentences. Her last bleeding had been before Cholula. Cholula had been three weeks ago. She had eaten almost nothing in Cholula and had assumed, then, that hunger was the cause, but here in the palace there was food at every hour and the mornings still came up against her like a wall. She kept her hands in her lap and translated a sentence about the canals of Texcoco being deeper than the canals of Xochimilco. She did not know yet what she would do. She knew she would not say. Not to Cortés. Not yet to Jerónimo. Not to the priest.

Between the second and third courses a woman approached her chair. A Mexica noblewoman of perhaps twenty-six, in a huipil dyed the green of a leaf two days off the branch, her hair braided in the Texcocan style. She inclined her head, the small inclination, not the deep one, and spoke in a Nahuatl so courtly Malintzin had to listen twice.

"Tecuichpotl sends me. The lady says you should not have to learn our court alone. I am Xochitl Tlapala. I will sit with you tomorrow, and the day after, and as long as is useful, and you will tell me what you do not know, and I will tell you. She thought you would prefer a person to a book."

There was a brightness behind it that did not match the formal phrasing. She was, Malintzin understood at once, being introduced to an instrument and to a friend, and the woman herself knew it and did not pretend otherwise.

"I will be glad of it," Malintzin said, in court Nahuatl, and then added, in the lower register, "I have been learning this room from the inside of my mouth. A teacher would be a kindness."

Xochitl's mouth moved at the corner. She had two children, she said, eight and five, and they would want to meet her eventually because they wanted to meet everyone Tecuichpotl thought worth meeting. Then she took her seat at the lower table, and Malintzin returned to translating.

The third course came. Cortés tapped the chair again, three fingers, twice, and this time the signal was for Sandoval, who sat across the room and answered with a small nod that meant *understood, after the feast*. They were going to weigh the tribute tonight. They would record it, in a Spanish ledger, with Spanish names for stones the Mexica had carried names for since before the Mexica had a city. They would be polite about it. They would do it after Moctezuma had retired.

Malintzin translated a courtesy about the health of the Triple Alliance. She translated it cleanly. She watched Cortés's hand return to the arm of the chair, and she thought, with the part of her mind that did not require her tongue: he does not know that they see him. He believes he is in his own house. The man on the dais who does not eat is older than this room and has watched men receive gold before.

She drank water. The chocolate she left. Across the courtyard the engine breathed heat into the cold air, and the fires on the temple, on the other side of the precinct, burned at their usual hour, in their usual quantity, for their usual reasons.

The garden behind Axayacatl's palace was rectangular, eighty paces on the long axis, walled in pumice on three sides and open on the fourth to the inner court where two Spanish Reliquaries stood at shoulder-arms in their crating. Malintzin had counted the paces on the second day. She counted everything in the new city. There were twelve flagstones between the bench and the fountain, four cypresses along the north wall, and seven arquebusiers loitering at the gate who pretended not to watch the Mexica women come and go.

Xochitl Tlapala sat on the bench at the eleventh paving stone, knees turned slightly toward Malintzin, hands folded in her lap with the thumbs touching. Even her sitting was a lesson. Mexica noblewomen did not arrange themselves so that a stranger could read their grief or their boredom. Thumbs together meant: I am at ease and not yet finished speaking. Thumbs apart meant: I have said what I came to say.

"You will hold your sleeves," Xochitl said. "Like this. The Cihuacoatl will assume you are a slave if you do not, and slaves are not addressed. He will speak past you to whichever Spanish captain is closest, and your work will be twice as hard."

Malintzin lifted her arms and tried it. The cotton fell wrong. The hem caught at the inside of her wrist and showed the underside of her forearm to the air, a thing a woman in the Mayan compound would have laughed at her for, which was apparently correct here.

"Better," Xochitl said. "Not yet good. We will work on it."

Beyond the south wall a horn sounded, three notes, pause, three notes, and another answered from the sacred precinct half a league off. Engine drill. Malintzin had learned to tell the cadences apart in three weeks. The triple-triple was a Huitzilopochtli flight returning to its housing. The four-and-one was a Tlaloc unit moving water. The long single tone was a pilot dying. She had not asked Xochitl to confirm any of it, because to ask would betray that she was counting horns.

Xochitl heard the triple-triple and her face did something Malintzin, watchful by trade, did not quite catch. A flicker, no more. Then it was gone.

"My husband is at the precinct today," Xochitl said. "He stands in the rotation. The Tlatelolco jaguar houses send eight men a week to watch the staging while the engines are kept warm. He hates it. He says the engines smell like burnt copal and old blood, which is unkind because they do, and one is not supposed to say so."

"What does he do when he is not watching engines."

"He is a merchant. He sells obsidian, mostly, the green-black kind from Pachuca, not the gray stuff from the southern quarries. He has opinions about quarries. I will spare you."

Malintzin smiled. It was the first time she had smiled inside the palace and meant it, which she noted, because she noted everything.

"You have children."

"Two. Tepin is eight. He is going to be very tall. Citlalli is five. She is going to be a problem for whoever marries her, which is a thing I say with pride." Xochitl turned a small folded thing in her fingers, a packet of beaten amate paper, and unfolded it without looking down. "Tepin found a snake yesterday in the third courtyard. He carried it to me on a stick. Citlalli wept because he would not let her hold it. I told her she could hold it when she was eight and could be trusted not to drop it on the herb-mistress. This satisfied no one."

She held out the amate. Inside was a small disc of polished obsidian, no larger than a thumbnail, set in a band of beaten copper. A mirror.

"For you," Xochitl said. "Not yet. In a few weeks, when you have stopped flinching at the sleeves. I am showing it to you now so you know it is coming. The Mexica do not give surprise gifts. Surprise is for ambushes."

Malintzin took it. The obsidian was cool against the pad of her thumb, and she could see in it a sliver of her own eye, which looked tired, and a sliver of cypress branch above her, which moved.

"Why," she said.

"Because I like you," Xochitl said, without performing the saying. "And because the woman who teaches you protocol is the woman who decides whether you survive the second week of January, when the gift-giving cycle begins and every senior wife in the city will inspect you for weaknesses. I would rather you survive."

A second horn from the precinct. Long, single, dying tone. Xochitl did not flinch, but her thumbs drifted apart and then deliberately came back together. Malintzin watched her bring them back. A pilot somewhere had gone over. The Mexica did not weep for pilots in public. They closed the body, sang the body home, and told the apprentices that the deity had taken its servant whole.

"My brother," Xochitl said, after a moment, "is at Texcoco. He would say I am being indiscreet, telling you these things. He is younger than me by five years and treats me like an aunt."

"What is his name."

"Itzcoatl. He will not come here. He does not approve of Spaniards and will not learn their faces."

The fountain ticked. A sparrow landed on the rim, drank, left. Malintzin felt the small hard fact of the pregnancy in her belly, a knot the size of a date pit, four weeks now, certain only since the second missed bleed, and did not move her hand toward it. Xochitl's eyes had already gone there, briefly, and come back. The Mexica court read bodies the way Malintzin read horns. There was no point in trying to hide. There was only the question of who named the seen thing first.

Xochitl did not name it.

"Eat the salt before the maize at the welcome feast," she said instead. "Even if no one tells you to. The Cihuacoatl's house tracks who eats salt first. They write it down. They write everything down."

"I will eat the salt."

"Good." Xochitl folded the amate over the mirror and tucked it back into her sleeve. "Same time on the seventh. We will do greetings. There are forty-two of them and you will hate every one."

She rose. Her thumbs separated as she stood, *I have said what I came to say*, and the rectangular garden, the seven arquebusiers, the two crated Reliquaries in the inner court, all of it tilted slightly as Malintzin stood with her, the mirror's promise still warm in the place under her ribs where the other warm thing was beginning.

She would tell no one. Not yet. Not Xochitl, not Aguilar, not the captain who had been given her name. She would learn the Spanish word for it first. Then she would decide.

The horn from the precinct sounded again. Triple-triple. A flight returning. Somewhere in the staging yard, someone's husband was off rotation and walking home to dinner.

Malintzin watched Xochitl go, counted the paces back to the bench, and sat down again to wait for Aguilar, who would be late, because he was always late, and because in the new city even lateness had its protocol.

Two afternoons a week, Fray Bernardo de Mendoza sat with Malintzin in the palace gardens learning Nahuatl. He carried a notebook. He asked about names and verbs. He asked what *teotl* meant; she gave him the answer (god, sacred, force) and watched him write it down. He asked what it meant when Mexica said "the sun eats hearts." She told him plainly. He did not flinch. He did not ask to be reassured. He asked to understand. Malintzin filed this about him. She had not been asked questions like these by any Spaniard. Bernardo's Franciscan cord rested on his knee; she noticed its hum (third-class, minor potency, tuned to Francis's vow of poverty) and did not comment. Establish: the friendship that will become Bernardo's death-witness in Ch 26.
