He came home by canoe and on foot. *Obsidian Mirror* stayed in Tenochtitlan, chained to its scaffold in the engine yard at Axayacatl's palace, a Tezcatlipoca-caste second-class is not a thing one walks across a causeway with, and a teōmachtiani returning for ritual obligation does not carry his god on his back. He carried instead the obsidian disc the engine wore on its chest in miniature, polished, in a pouch at his hip. Three years since he had last seen the lake from this shore. The water looked wrong.

The family compound stood where it had always stood, on the rise above the eastern shore, the volcanoes hanging over the south horizon, Popocatépetl smoking its slow smoke, Iztaccíhuatl white and quiet. A dog he did not know barked at him. A boy he did not know took his bundle. His mother met him in the second courtyard.

She was a priestess of Tezcatlipoca and looked it: matte gray robes, hair braided tight, the small mirror at her throat that drank the lamplight rather than threw it back. She did not embrace him. She touched his forehead with three fingers, the priestess greeting, not the mother greeting, and led him to the eating-room.

Dinner was modest. Maize, beans, a little turkey, atole. She let him eat before she spoke.

"The seals at Cholula have cracked further," she said. "Since the massacre. The ones at Tula too. Three fingers' width on the western face at Cholula, less at Tula but the stones are warm."

He set down his cup. "How do you know about Tula?"

"The tlamatinime travel. They argue openly now. Half of them say the gods have withdrawn. The other half say the gods are deciding."

"Deciding what?"

"Whether to come back for us or not."

He waited. She did not say which half she belonged to. He had not expected her to. A priestess of the smoking mirror does not give away which face she is wearing.

"And the engines?" he asked. Tezcatlipoca to Tezcatlipoca, mother to son. He could ask her this and only her. "The Tezcatlipoca corps in Tenochtitlan. We are running the same drills we ran in spring. The pilots feel the same. The fuel is the same. But, "

"But the deity is thinner in the mask."

"Yes."

"Here too. Two of the smaller engines at the Texcoco yard would not start last month. The teōmachtiani sat for a full day inside the chest and the god did not arrive. They burned the fuel and re-consecrated the chamber and on the third day the engines stood up. No one knows what to make of it."

He thought of *Obsidian Mirror* on its scaffold, fifty feet of matte obsidian, the smoking-mirror disc on its chest the twin of the disc in his pouch. He thought of the Quetzalcoatl seals at Cholula, sealed for generations, now cracking. The calmecac had taught him the theology: Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl were brothers, and when one rose the other fell.

"Mother. If Quetzalcoatl wakes, "

"Then we will see what we are made of." She refilled his cup. "Tell me about your sister."

"Xochitl is well. Last I saw her. She has been asked by Tecuichpotl to teach a Nahua girl Mexica court protocol. Some interpreter the foreigners brought up from the coast. Xochitl finds it interesting."

"Of course she does." A small smile, the first of the evening. "Check on her when you go back. A house with a Mexica jaguar warrior and two small children and now a foreign girl visiting daily, your sister is too busy to notice she is tired."

"I will."

She let a long pause sit. Then: "Your cousin sent word. He would like to see you while you are home. Before the ritual."

He had been waiting for this and dreading it. "Where is he."

"Three days north. He has people on the road. They will find you if you ride."

He left at dawn, alone. Ixtlilxochitl's people found him on the second day on the trail north, two retainers in plain mantles, polite, unarmed. They led him to a compound smaller than he had expected, set against a hillside where the maize had already been harvested. His cousin met him in the doorway.

Twenty-eight, broad-shouldered, the Nezahualpilli line in the long jaw. Three years of semi-exile had burned the soft off him. He took Itzcoatl's hands in both of his.

"Cousin. You came."

"Mother said you asked."

"She would. Come in."

The room was small, clean, austere as a calmecac cell. Ixtlilxochitl poured cacao into two cups himself, host to honored guest, an unsubtle courtesy. Itzcoatl took the cup and hated the warmth of it in his hand.

"You are corresponding with Cortés," Itzcoatl said.

"Through a Totonac. Yes."

"How long."

"Since summer."

Itzcoatl drank. The cacao was good. He hated that too.

"He has offered nothing yet that I have accepted," Ixtlilxochitl said. "Nor have I offered anything I cannot withdraw. But the road is open. Texcoco can be Texcoco again. Not a vassal. Not a courtesy seat at a Mexica table. Itself. We can choose, before the choice is made for us."

"Cacama is the tlatoani."

"Cacama is Moctezuma's hand wearing our crown."

"He is still our cousin."

"He is. And he is also that." Ixtlilxochitl set his cup down. "I am not asking you to betray him today. I am asking you to know that a door exists, and to know which side of it you are on when it opens."

"No."

Ixtlilxochitl nodded as if he had heard a weather report. "All right."

"I mean it."

"I believe you mean it." He smiled, small, unbitter. "You will come when you come, cousin. Or you will not, and we will meet on the field, and that will also be a kind of answer."

Itzcoatl finished the cacao because to leave it would have been an answer too. They spoke of family for an hour. Of his mother. Of an aunt who had died. Of nothing at all.

He set out south at first light. The seals at Cholula cracking, the engines in the Texcoco yard slow to wake, his cousin already corresponding with the foreigners and his mother letting him go to learn it, none of it fit the shape of a world he had been trained to defend.

In Tenochtitlan he went straight to Xochitl's compound before reporting to the engine yard. She was well. The children were well. The Nahua girl was at the palace that afternoon, not the compound, and Itzcoatl did not ask her name and did not wait. He filed the detail and went to his god.

His loyalties were officially intact. Privately, they were not.
