October 18, 1519. Dawn at Cholula. The courtyard ran east to west between the great pyramid and the lesser shrines, two hundred paces long, paved in pale flagstone that took the light like wet bone. Andrés stood in La Niña's cradle with the faceplate open and the helmet hooked at his belt. The cradle braced him at sternum and hip, eight points of contact, all of them familiar, and held him forty-two feet above the stones. From up here the men coming through the gate looked like a procession of birds.

They were birds. They were noblemen in feathered cloaks: green and red, scarlet and white, the dyes deep enough to drink the dawn. Forty had entered. Sixty more waited at the threshold. None carried weapons. Cortés had asked for them unarmed and they had come unarmed, because parley was parley and a guest was a guest, and these were the rules everyone had agreed to.

Four Reliquaries active in the courtyard. La Niña de Córdoba on the north wall. San Sebastián de Cuba on the south. Two more, Santiago Matamoros and Nuestra Señora del Campeón, at the east end, flanking the gate. Cortés stood inside Nuestra Señora's cockpit. His voice came down through the engine's vox-grille thinner than it should have, as if the saint had taken some breath out of it.

Past the gate, beyond the threshold, Andrés could see the Tlaxcalan rank. Six teōmecahuītlī in second-rank formation, their carved deities catching the sun edgewise, three Huitzilopochtli, two Tezcatlipoca, one squat Tlaloc with its belly of rain-glyphs. They had not been told to engage. They had been told to stand. Which meant they had been told everything.

Andrés had been told nothing. *Be ready,* the captain had said, and crossed himself the way men cross themselves before a thing they will not name. Andrés was twenty-three years old. He had piloted La Niña through Tabasco and Cempoala and the high pass between the volcanoes. He had killed armed men. He had blessed the dying with a fragment of cord. He had prayed *oratio* on the third-day camp on the road to Cempoala and felt the engine answer him in the inclination of her shoulders, the tilt of her head, six to eight inches of motion he had measured against the canyon wall behind her. He had given her name to Malintzin at dawn. Flora of Córdoba, mozárabe, virgin and martyr. Malintzin had said the name back to him and the name had sounded the same in her mouth as in his.

He had not been told he was going to kill men in feathered cloaks at parley.

But he had been told to be ready.

Beyond the lesser shrines, past the eastern colonnade, the precinct opened onto its larger work. The temple of Quetzalcoatl. And in front of it, half-buried in the platform's living architecture, half-emerged from it, as if the platform had grown around the engine instead of the engine settling into the platform, the dormant thing.

No Spanish eye had ever seen it. Andrés's was the first.

He could not measure it. He had measured La Niña against the canyon wall and Nuestra Señora against the watchtower at Cempoala and the Tlaxcalan giants against the trees they had broken. This one would not yield to measurement. It was the size of the temple. It *was* the temple. The feathered serpent's head rested on its forelimbs the way a sleeping dog rests its muzzle, and the head alone, the head alone, was wider than the courtyard he was standing in. Forty Cholulan noblemen had crossed the threshold. The serpent's eye, lidded, would have swallowed all of them and the courtyard with them, and not opened.

Andrés had read, in seminary, of the Leviathan. He had pictured it as a whale. He had been wrong about the picture. He had been wrong about the proportion. He had been wrong about the intention. Leviathan was not a whale. Leviathan was a building that had decided, once, to walk.

He looked away because looking did not stop.

Across the courtyard, Nuestra Señora del Campeón stood in her station at the east gate. Cortés's voice came through her vox-grille, even and slow, the Castilian translated into Mayan by Aguilar and into Nahuatl by Malintzin, a chain of three throats that turned each sentence smaller and more formal by the time it reached the noblemen. *You are welcome. You are honored. We have a thing to discuss.* The noblemen bowed. They had been bowing all morning.

And Andrés saw, saw before he understood, that Nuestra Señora was weeping.

It was not a trick of the dawn. The light came flat from the east and he watched from the north and the streaks ran down the cheek-plates of her faceplate in two clean lines, one from each eye, the way water runs from a roof-tile that has held the rain. The fluid was not water. It caught the sun the way oil catches it, with weight. Cortés was inside her. Cortés was speaking through her vox-grille at this moment, and his engine was crying, and his voice was steady.

The captain of San Sebastián saw it too. Andrés knew because the engine across the courtyard turned its head three degrees, a small motion, a pilot's flinch, and held.

*Mary left no body,* Bernardo had told him on the ship. *So her engines run on what touched what represented her. Tears. Splinters. A statue that wept blood in Medellín in 1497. They are not like the others.* Bernardo had said this with his hand on the Franciscan cord at his waist, and Andrés had understood it as theology and not as a thing he would one day see.

He was seeing it now. The Marian engine was weeping in the second before her pilot gave an order, and her pilot was Cortés, and the order had not yet come.

The threshold was full of feathered cloaks. The last of the noblemen crossed it. The Tlaxcalans beyond the gate had not moved. The Spaniards behind every column had not moved. The dormant serpent had not moved. The world was holding still in the way that worlds hold still when the rule that governs them is being changed somewhere out of sight.

Andrés put his hand on the helmet at his belt. He did not put it on.

Cortés gave the order.

Cortés gave the order.

His vox-grille did not speak. Aguilar's did. Malintzin's did. They said the same thing in different throats and the same thing was *this.*

The Tlaxcalan engines moved. The Spanish engines moved. The four gates slammed shut. The noblemen looked up. They had been looking down at the ground all morning because that was what guests do. Now they looked up and saw the faces of the gods and the faces of the men inside the gods and the gods were moving.

The first volley was arquebus fire. The men on the walls behind La Niña, behind San Sebastián, behind Nuestra Señora, the men behind every engine that stood in the courtyard: they fired. They fired at the noblemen in feathered cloaks, and the noblemen fell, and some of them fell with their backs to the fire, because they had been turning toward the engines when the engines were supposed to be speaking to them and not killing them, and the bullets took them in the spine and the kidneys and the lungs and they were on the ground in a second and their hands were still open, because they had not been given time to make them fists.

Andrés did not know he was going to kill before the order came. He did not know he would have to choose before the bodies fell. The captain had told him to be ready. He had thought this meant: be ready to fight. He had not thought it meant: be ready to kill without asking why.

The captain's vox-grille spoke. The Tlaxcalan vox-grilles spoke. The Cholulan engines spoke. The Cholulan engines were the first to speak and they spoke in a tongue Andrés had not heard and he did not understand it but he knew the shape of it, and the shape of it was *no.*

La Niña moved. He did not tell her to move. He felt the monstrance hum and the hum was not the hum that hummed when the killing was right. It was not the hum that hummed when the killing was wrong. It was the hum that hummed when the killing was *given*, and the hum was at his sternum and it was a pressure and a cleanness and a hum that had no voice in it.

He had killed at Centla. He had killed a man in armor who had been standing on the ground, not in a machine, and the man had been holding a weapon and the weapon had been raised. He had killed a man in armor who had been standing in a machine and the machine had been moving and the man inside had been holding a weapon and the weapon had been raised. He had killed two men. He had killed two men who had been *fighting.*

This was not fighting.

This was a thing he had not named.

He could not tell his hands what to do. He could not tell his mind what to think. La Niña's halberd went down. La Niña's halberd came up. The halberd struck the noblemen in the feathered cloaks and the noblemen fell and the noblemen did not stand up again.

Andrés watched. He did not pilot. He did not decide. La Niña was moving and he was inside her and the hum was at his sternum and the hum was a thing he did not understand.

He killed one. He killed two. He killed three. The noblemen were on the ground and their backs were to the fire and their fronts were to the halberds and the halberds came down and the halberds came up and the noblemen were dead.

The captain of San Sebastián was killing. The men in Nuestra Señora's station were killing. The men in Santiago Matamoros's station were killing. The Tlaxcalans were killing. The Cholulan engines tried to kill back and the Cholulan engines were destroyed.

The slaughter went on for twenty minutes. Andrés did not measure the time. He measured the number of men who fell. He measured the number of men who stood. He measured the number of men who did not fall and the number of men who did not stand and the number of men who were dead.

Thousands died in the courtyard. Andrés killed some of them and the captain of San Sebastián killed some of them and Nuestra Señora killed some of them and Santiago Matamoros killed some of them and the Tlaxcalans killed some of them and the arquebuses killed some of them and the bows killed some of them and the order killed some of them and the silence killed some of them and the hum killed some of them and the hum was at his sternum and the hum was a thing he did not understand.

He did not vomit. He did not vomit and he noticed that he did not vomit and that, too, registered somewhere he did not yet have language for.

The courtyard was full of dead men. Blood was on the stones and on the engines and on the walls and on the cloaks, and the cloaks were feathered and the cloaks were green and red, scarlet and white, and the dyes were deep enough to drink the dawn.

Then the serpent moved.

It was a small movement. It was the smallest movement Andrés had ever watched a thing the size of a temple make. The serpent's head, which had been resting on its forelimbs, lifted perhaps four inches off the stone. The forelimbs took the shift. The plumes along its neck, feathers cast in stone, or stone shaped like feathers, he had never been told which, settled in a new line. The eye, which had been lidded, was not lidded anymore. It did not open. It was open. There is a difference, and Andrés learned the difference in the half-second between the two states.

The serpent did not stand. It did not rise. It did not draw breath in any way he could see. It only turned its head, a degree, perhaps two, and looked at the courtyard.

It looked at the dead. It looked at the killing-engines. It looked, and Andrés felt looked-at, though he was inside La Niña's chest and behind her face plate and the serpent had no clear angle on him.

Every pilot in the courtyard saw it. He knew this because the killing stopped. Not the men on the walls, they were still finding targets. The engines stopped. La Niña stopped. San Sebastián stopped. Santiago Matamoros stopped. Even the Tlaxcalan engines, who had been working through the wounded, stopped. The halberds stopped at the top of their arc and did not come down. The vox-grilles, which had been speaking the order, fell out of speech.

The serpent's head was up. The serpent's eye was open. The serpent did not wake.

Andrés understood, in the cockpit, with the hum still at his sternum, that *not waking* was a choice. The serpent had slept for six generations. It had heard a thing in the courtyard that was loud enough to lift its head four inches and open its eye and turn its face toward the killing, and it had decided not to wake. The decision was visible. The decision was the only thing in the courtyard that was visible.

He had been told, at the seminary in Córdoba, that the saints sleep until they are needed. He had been told that the saint in his monstrance was a girl who died at thirteen. He had been told that what slept in old places was small and human and bound by Rome.

The serpent was the size of the temple. The temple was the size of the courtyard. The courtyard was full of dead men. And the thing that had decided not to wake had decided the way a man decides, for reasons of its own, on a timetable of its own, with a face that turned and an eye that opened and a head that lifted four inches and no more.

He looked away because looking did not stop.

He looked at the captain. The captain was in Nuestra Señora's cockpit. The captain's engine was weeping. Actual fluid was running from the face plates, Andrés could see it from across the courtyard, two thin lines down the cheeks of the Virgin, catching the late-morning light. The captain's vox-grille did not speak. The captain's voice, when at last it came over the corps channel, was steady. The captain said: *cease.* Then he said nothing. Cortés did not speak for the rest of the day. The men around him learned, by midafternoon, that he would not.

Andrés did not speak either. He stood in La Niña's cockpit and the cockpit held him and did not hold him and was a thing he would not leave. The hum was at his sternum. The girl did not say whether the killing was right and did not say whether the killing was wrong. She had not spoken at Centla either. He understood, finally, that she might never speak, that the silence was not waiting to be broken but was the answer she had already given, and the answer was that she would not tell him. The decision was his. It had always been his. The serpent had turned its head to show him whose decision was whose.

He looked back at the serpent.

The head was already lowering. The eye was already lidded. The plumes had already settled. By the time the captain's order came again, *withdraw, form up, count the living*, the serpent looked exactly as it had at dawn, exactly as it had for six generations, and Andrés would have doubted that he had seen it move at all if every other pilot in the courtyard had not seen it too.

He climbed out of La Niña's cockpit on legs he could not feel. He stood on her shoulder and looked at the temple and the temple looked back, asleep, as if nothing had happened. The blood was on the stones.

He counted the living because that was the order, and it was the only order he had been given since *cease*, and he obeyed it because obedience was a shape his body still knew. Eighty-three Spaniards on their feet. Four hundred and some Tlaxcalans. The Cholulan dead were not his to count. He knew this without being told. He looked at the courtyard anyway and tried, and stopped at fourteen, and stopped because fourteen was the smallest number he could hold in his head before the number became a face.

His hands had done some of it. He knew which ones. La Niña's halberd had taken three men who held no weapon, and one priest in a feathered cloak who had been kneeling. The kneeling was the part he could not put down. The seminary had a word for kneeling. The seminary had a word for a man with no weapon. The seminary had words for everything in the courtyard except what he had done with them inside it.

That night he lay in his cot in the Spanish quarters on the western colonnade of the precinct and the hum was at his sternum and would not leave. He had unbuckled. He had washed his hands twice. He had eaten the bread someone pushed at him without remembering taking it. None of these quieted the hum, and he understood, on the cot, in the dark, that the hum was not La Niña. The hum was him. The girl had been silent at Centla. The girl had been silent in the courtyard. The girl was silent now. What he had taken for her voice in his sternum, all these months, was the noise of his own decision returning to him through her.

He thought of the serpent. The serpent had decided not to wake, and the deciding had been visible from across the courtyard, and the serpent had been the size of the temple. He thought of what he had been told a saint was. He thought of what the serpent was. He could not get the two to share a sentence. The seminary had built him a room with a low ceiling, and the room had been adequate for thirteen years, and the ceiling was gone now, and there was no roof at all, and the sky over the courtyard had a face in it that had chosen, on a timetable of its own, not to come down.

He turned on his side. The cot creaked. Somewhere along the colonnade a man was crying, quietly, trying not to. Andrés did not know whose voice it was. He thought, perhaps, the captain's. He thought, perhaps, his own. He could not tell, in the dark, and after a while he stopped trying to tell, and lay with his eyes open until the first grey at the window, and made no decision, and was not absolved of the decisions he had already made.
