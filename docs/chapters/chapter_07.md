The road to Cholula was mud and dust, and the engines walked in single file where the hills began to pinch the trail. Malintzin walked second rank behind the captains. She had been tired for weeks. The market crossing was a clay-smoothed square where four roads met, with stalls of dried chiles and a woman selling water from a gourd, and the column slowed because the engines had to pass between two stone markers set thirty paces apart.

The old woman appeared at her elbow. She had a basket of squash flowers on her hip and the lined face of someone who had walked many roads, and she spoke Nahuatl before Malintzin could raise a hand.

"Daughter," she said, "do not enter the city."

Her voice was low, the voice of a woman who has learned to be quiet in places where quiet is survival. She leaned in. The Cholulans were preparing an ambush. The Mexica priests were coordinating it. They would kill her and her people before they saw them coming. The pits in the side streets were already dug. The roof-stones were already set.

Malintzin kept her face neutral. She did not smile. She did not frown. She nodded, and she said in Nahuatl, *thank you, mother*.

The old woman's eyes were too kind. She thought Malintzin was a fellow Nahua. She thought Malintzin walked as a free woman. She could not see the chains, because the chains were not iron, the chains were translation, and translation was the thing she was learning to break.

The woman touched her sleeve once and was gone, swallowed by the market.

Malintzin walked on. The column resumed its pace. La Niña de Córdoba was four engines ahead, and even at four engines' distance Malintzin could see the small glass box on the chest plate where Saint Flora's bones rode. She had watched the engine kneel on the third-day camp. She had watched the gilding bow six to eight inches and the cradle-timbers groan, and she had suspected that the bones inside the small glass box did not bend when the gilding bent. She still suspected this. She still did not know.

She thought about the old woman's mistake, and the thinking made her cold. The old woman had assumed she was free. The old woman had spoken to her as a Nahua to a Nahua. The old woman would die if the warning reached the wrong ear, and the warning had reached Malintzin's ear, which was a Spanish ear now, or close enough.

She would not tell Andrés. Andrés prayed to a saint named Flora and bowed twice when he gave his name, and Andrés was twenty-three and burned across the shoulders, and Andrés was not a man who carried other people's secrets well. She would tell Cortés. She had decided this before she had words for it. Information was survival, and the only currency a slave woman owned was the thing she heard before anyone else heard it.

She had thirty-one Spanish words now. She had counted them last night in the dark, the four she began with, *agua, fuego, no, aquí*, and twenty-seven more she had gathered from captains and cookfires and from Aguilar's mouth when he did not know she was listening. She needed perhaps four hundred to cut Aguilar out. She had ten weeks, maybe twelve, before Cholula's aftermath would force her hand.

She found Cortés at the head of the column when the column halted to water the pack train. He was off-engine, *Nuestra Señora del Campeón* cradled behind him with her face plates closed, and he was speaking to Sandoval about a foraging detail. Aguilar was at his elbow.

Malintzin spoke to Aguilar in Maya. Aguilar spoke to Cortés in Spanish. The chain felt slow today, slower than it had felt yesterday, and she watched Cortés's face for the words she could already half-read.

"Captain," she said through Aguilar, "an old woman at the market crossing told me the city is preparing an ambush. The Cholulans have dug pits in the side streets. The roof-stones are set. The Mexica priests are coordinating it."

Cortés did not move for the space of three breaths. Then he asked, through Aguilar, though Malintzin caught half of it before Aguilar turned, *was she alone, did anyone hear her, can you find her again*.

Malintzin said, "She was alone. No one heard. I can find her by tomorrow's dawn if you wish her brought."

She watched Cortés's mouth. She caught *bring her* before Aguilar said *bring her*. She caught *quietly* before Aguilar said *quietly*. She caught the small thing in his eyes that was not gratitude, gratitude was a thing free people gave each other, but a recalibration. She had become useful in a different way than she had been useful yesterday. She felt the recalibration in her own ribs.

She returned to her place in the column. The march went on. The hills closed in.

That night the camp pitched in a dry watercourse two leagues short of the city. Cooking fires burned low. The Tlaxcalan auxiliaries slept with their weapons laid against their thighs, and the Spanish slept under the cradled feet of their engines, and Malintzin lay on her mat with her face turned toward the sky and could not close her eyes.

The old woman's face kept arriving. *Daughter*, she had said. *Daughter, do not enter the city.* The woman had used the word *nochpoch*, which meant daughter and meant a thing more than daughter, and Malintzin had not been called *nochpoch* by anyone since the year before she was sold the first time.

She thought: *I have killed her.* She did not know if this was true. She thought it might become true by morning.

She thought: *I have agreed to befriend the boy Rabbit if anything happens to Citlal.* This thought arrived without warning. She had no reason to think of Citlal tonight. She thought of Citlal anyway, because some agreements travel with you whether or not the road is the road they were made on.

She thought: *I will learn enough Spanish to cut the boy out, and then I will be a different kind of slave, and that kind will be worse, because that kind will know what is being done.*

She turned on the mat. She did not sleep. The smoke from the cooking fires lifted and crossed the moon. Somewhere ahead, in the dark, Cholula prepared its pits and set its roof-stones and counted its priests, and somewhere ahead, in the dark, an old woman with a basket of squash flowers walked home along a road she had used all her life.

Malintzin lay awake and counted Spanish words. She had thirty-one. She needed four hundred. She had ten weeks. She closed her eyes and did not sleep, and the column slept around her, and the engines slept on their cradles, and the night before Cholula passed without mercy and without rest.

The old woman's face kept arriving. *Daughter*, she had said. *Daughter, do not enter the city.* The woman had used the word *nochpoch*, which meant daughter and meant a thing more than daughter, and Malintzin had not been called *nochpoch* by anyone since the year before she was sold the first time. The woman had pressed two squash flowers into her hand and folded her fingers over them as if the flowers were the warning and the warning was the flowers.

She thought: *I have killed her.* She did not know if this was true. She thought it might become true by morning. Cortés would send four men. The men would walk *quietly*. The market crossing she had described was a small one, with only two paths in. The old woman lived along one of them. There were not many old women in Cholula with baskets of squash flowers, and a Spaniard moving quietly was not quiet, and a Tlaxcalan moving quietly was a man with a grudge sharpened against the Cholulans for sixty years. She did not know which kind of man Cortés would send. She thought it did not matter.

She thought: *I have agreed to befriend the boy Rabbit if anything happens to Citlal.* The thought arrived without warning. She had no reason to think of Citlal tonight. Citlal had walked third in line at the handover after Centla and Citlal was somewhere west of here, in some other Spanish camp or some other Spanish bed, and the boy Rabbit was in the Mayan compound or had been sold elsewhere and was not findable from a dry watercourse two leagues short of Cholula. She thought of Citlal anyway, because some agreements travel with you whether or not the road is the road they were made on.

She thought: *I will learn enough Spanish to cut the boy out, and then I will be a different kind of slave, and that kind will be worse, because that kind will know what is being done.*

The wind shifted. Smoke from the cooking fires lifted and crossed the moon. Somewhere ahead, in the dark, Cholula prepared its pits and set its roof-stones and counted its priests, and somewhere ahead, in the dark, an old woman with a basket of squash flowers walked home along a road she had used all her life.

Malintzin counted Spanish words. She had thirty-one. *Agua. Fuego. No. Aquí.* Then *capitán*, and *caballo*, and *sí*, and *bring*, and *quietly*, and the rest of them in a list she rehearsed twice through to be sure. Thirty-one. She needed four hundred to cut Aguilar out and stand alone at Cortés's elbow with no Spaniard between her mouth and his ear. She had ten weeks before the thing she had set in motion at the market crossing arrived at whatever it would arrive at. She would not have ten weeks after. She would have hours.

She turned on the mat. She did not sleep.

After the second watch she rose and walked toward the latrine ditch and did not go to it. She walked past the cradles instead. *La Niña de Córdoba* stood in hers, sixty feet of gilded plate dulled by the day's dust, her face plates open in night-aspect, her hands folded at her waist in the receiving posture the Spanish called *en oración*. Andrés de Mora knelt fifteen yards from her cradle on the bare earth, his head uncovered, speaking too quietly for Malintzin to catch the Latin. He did not see her. He was praying for someone, for himself, for a man he had killed, for a man he was going to kill, she did not know.

*La Niña* knelt.

She knelt slowly, with the small hydraulic sigh Malintzin had learned to listen for in the engines at rest, and her right knee touched the dust of the watercourse at the same instant Andrés's voice paused on a syllable. Then she rose again, just as slowly, and folded her hands and stood as she had stood before. The whole motion took the space of four breaths. Andrés did not look up. He had not asked her to kneel, and she had kneeled, and he had not noticed because he was inside the prayer and the prayer had not ended.

Malintzin stood in the dark and watched the engine and the man and the small distance between them. She did not have a word for what she had just seen. She had a word in Nahuatl for it, the word her father had used about the *teōmecahuītlī* at Coatzacoalcos, *ihuintilia*, which meant *to be made drunk by* and meant the way a thing answered a thing it loved, but the Spanish would not call it that. The Spanish would call it grace, or they would call it nothing, or they would not have seen it at all because they had been inside the prayer.

She catalogued it. She did not yet know she was cataloguing. She only knew that *La Niña* had moved without being asked and that no Spaniard fifteen yards away had registered the movement, and that this fact was a thing she could carry, the way she carried *bring her* and *quietly*, the way she would carry every other small fact she gathered between this camp and the next city and the city after that. She filed it under the same shelf in her chest where the old woman's *nochpoch* now lived.

She walked back to her mat past the Tlaxcalan sentries, who saw her and did not stop her, and past Aguilar's tent, where Aguilar slept with his Maya sandals set beside his head as if he might be summoned by water in the night, and past Cortés's tent, where a single lamp still burned and where she could hear no voices, only the small scrape of a man writing.

She lay down. She closed her eyes. She did not sleep.

The smoke from the cooking fires lifted and crossed the moon a second time. The night before Cholula passed without mercy and without rest, and somewhere ahead in the dark, an old woman who had called her *daughter* set her basket down inside her door and bolted the door against a sound she had not yet heard, and somewhere fifteen yards from a kneeling engine a young adorator from Extremadura finished a prayer he did not know had been answered, and Malintzin lay on her mat and counted, and counted, and counted, and the count did not reach four hundred and the night did not end and the column slept around her until the first watch of the dawn.
