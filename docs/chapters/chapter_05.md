The salt-flat below was the color of old bone, and the Tlaxcalan engines on the opposite ridge caught the morning sun. Andrés counted them three times. Six. He had been briefed for two.

Six Huitzilopochtli-class, copper-gilt, hummingbird helms tilted south, perhaps fifty to sixty feet at the shoulder. Knight tier. The kind of engine you walked toward, not around. He had fought one Maya engine at Centla and lived; he had not fought a Nahua engine at all. Six was a number that did not match any account he had heard in the captain's tent.

The captain's tent had spoken of a small frontier polity, surrounded, scarce. The captain's tent had been wrong about scarcity. Tlaxcala had been bleeding warriors into the Flower Wars for fifty years, and what bled out of those wars went somewhere, into teōcalli-chests, into the patient accumulation of war-fuel that nobody downstream had counted. Six engines on a ridge was not a frontier. It was a furnace that had been burning quietly for two generations.

Three weeks they had been in this country. Three weeks of skirmish on broken ground, Tlaxcalan slingers, jaguar-houses on foot, no engines fielded. The Spanish had begun to believe the engines did not exist, or were rumor, or were elsewhere. This morning the rumor had stood up on the opposite ridge and counted to six.

Andrés crossed himself and climbed into La Niña de Córdoba.

The cockpit smelled of olive oil and old beeswax, the polish his father had used on saddles, which an old adorator from Córdoba had insisted was correct for the helm-leather. The monstrance sat at chest-height in its iron cradle, a hand's-breadth of gilded crystal holding three fragments of Saint Flora's femur and a stoppered vial of the breath captured at her death. Andrés rested two fingers on the cradle, said *oratio*, said *Flora*, and felt the engine warm beneath him the way a horse warms when it remembers your weight.

He did not think, not yet, not before combat, about the fact that the engines on the opposite ridge were warming the same way, on a different fuel. He had been trying not to think about it for three weeks. The Tlaxcalan engines did not run on saints. They ran on captives taken at flowered spear-point and given on stone tables, ixiptla dressed as gods and killed as gods, their hearts laid into wooden chests bolted into the ribs of the engine. A Reliquary's heart-chamber was a sealed crystal vessel preserving a death the Church had recognized. A Teōmecahuītlī's was a stained box holding deaths the Church had not. The mechanism was not the same. The mechanism was the same shape from a distance, and that was the part he could not stop circling.

The horn sounded twice from below. Cortés's signal: advance.

La Niña went down the slope at a long stride. The Tlaxcalans came down theirs at a longer one. Five engines split outward to flank the Spanish line; one came on alone, straight at him, the way a man comes at you when he wants the kill to be his.

Andrés saw the pilot's house-mark a hundred yards out, a knot of jaguar-pelt at the engine's collar, and understood, the way you understand weather, that this was not a young teōmachtiani. The engine moved with a settled economy that took years. Knee, hip, shoulder, all paid for in advance.

The first exchange went badly. The Tlaxcalan struck for La Niña's left shoulder with an obsidian-edged macuahuitl twice the length of a horse, and Andrés took the blow on the pauldron because he was a heartbeat slow on the parry. The pauldron split. Saint Flora did not flinch, the monstrance was inboard of the chest plate, untouched, but Andrés felt the shock through his teeth and knew the next blow would not be survivable.

He did the thing his masters had told him never to do. He stepped in.

A knight engine fights at the length of its weapon. A knight engine that closes inside its own reach is, briefly, not a knight engine. It is a man in eight tons of copper with his arms too long. The Tlaxcalan tried to back-step and could not get the angle, and Andrés drove the short reliquary-blade, La Niña carried a saint's-arm-length cinquedea on her right hip for exactly this, up under the engine's gorget and into the cockpit seam.

The seam parted by an inch. An inch was enough.

The engine did not stagger. It went down the way a tree goes down when the last fiber gives, a long, considered fall, no flailing, the hummingbird helm describing a slow arc against the morning. It struck the salt-flat with a sound that was not metal and not wood and that Andrés would hear, on and off, for the next two years.

He climbed out before the dust had settled. He should not have. There were five other engines on the field and his own had a split pauldron. He climbed out anyway, walked to the cockpit-seam, and reached in.

The pilot was already gone. Old man, by the hands, knuckles wide, nails trimmed close, three healed cuts along the left thumb that Andrés would later think were from a knife slipping in old age. The copper identification disc lay against the breastbone where the cord had broken, a thumb's-width of stamped metal, three glyphs Andrés could not read, the edges worn smooth by forty years of being touched in the morning before war.

He took it.

He told himself, climbing back into La Niña with the disc closed in his fist, that he would give it to Malintzin and ask her to read the glyphs. He told himself he would return it to the man's house, when there was a man's house to find. He told himself several things, none of which explained why the disc was already warm against his palm in a way the monstrance, three feet from his chest, had never been.

The horn sounded again. Five engines remaining. He set the disc inside his cuirass, against the skin, and walked La Niña forward.

The duel lasted four minutes by the cradle-clock, and Andrés won by maybe six inches.

Three weeks of combat had narrowed to this. The Tlaxcalan field was a chewed terrace of red earth above a dry watercourse, the line of broken cradles where two third-class Reliquaries had already gone down, pilots named, one buried, one on a litter waiting transport back to Cempoala. Andrés in La Niña de Córdoba walked the line. The Tlaxcalan engine that came forward to meet him was a Camaxtli, hunter's armor in the Tlaxcalan reds and whites, jaguar-pelt motif across the shoulders, atlatl rig at the right hip and a bundle of cane darts taller than a man at the left. The pilot was a kinsman of Xicotencatl the Younger. Andrés had been told the name twice and not retained it. He retained the silhouette.

They met in the open. La Niña was eighteen feet shorter and three hundred years older, a second-class with a thirteenth-century bone-relic in her monstrance, a Mozárabe Virgin from Córdoba who had outlived her cathedral. The Camaxtli engine had been fueled, Domingo had told him on the walk in, with the bones of seven Tlaxcalan warriors taken in the last Flower War. Andrés had not asked how anyone knew that. He understood by now that some things were simply known.

The first exchange was two atlatl-darts La Niña caught on her shield arm, the impact ringing the bronze like a cracked bell. The second was a feint to the right hip, Andrés saw it three breaths before it came and didn't pivot, which was the trick the older adorators had told him to learn. Pivoting was Spanish. Standing was Mozárabe. He stood, and the dart skidded along the monstrance plate without finding the relic-housing. Then La Niña moved.

She moved the way she had moved on the third night out of Cempoala, not fast, but committed, cradle-timbers in her shoulders groaning the way they had groaned when he had knelt and prayed before her. Six inches. He aimed for the hunter's-armor seam below the Camaxtli engine's left arm, where the pelt-motif scaled down to articulation, and La Niña's fist went through the armor and through the cockpit-housing behind it and through the pilot, in that order. The engine came down. The lower cradle-frame was wood, not bone, an older Tlaxcalan convention, and it splintered, and the engine fell forward at the shoulder the way a tree falls when the cut is finished. Forty feet of carved wood and lacquered armor hitting red earth. The sound was less than Andrés had expected. The dust stayed up a long time.

He climbed down. Domingo was shouting something he did not hear. He walked to the broken cockpit. The pilot was already dead. The strike had taken his chest clean, his hands were still on the rein-cords, and at his throat hung a copper disc the size of a Spanish ducado, hammered with a glyph Andrés did not know. Andrés took it. He did not know why. He put it in his coat pocket. Then he walked back to La Niña and stood under her shadow and could not get his breathing right for a quarter of an hour.

That was on the third of September. The Tlaxcalan council folded three weeks after.

He was in the parley tent when the alliance was formalized. The tent smelled of wet reed-matting and Tlaxcalan chocolate, too much cinnamon, the way a house that has tried hard smells. The defeated polity offered alliance against the Mexica, citing fifty years of Flower War predation, tribute that was not tribute because the Mexica called it war, warriors who were not warriors because the Mexica called them captives. The Spanish captains nodded, the way men nod when the arithmetic has been done for them.

Cortés accepted the alliance with the same expression he had worn signing the Veracruz papers. Legalistic, calm, completely opaque. He signed in the margin. He did not say *God is with us.* He did not say *We will win.* He said, *This is legal.* And the Tlaxcalans said, *We agree.*

The Tlaxcalan pilots were seconded to the expedition that afternoon. Six Camaxtli engines, the inventory said, hunter's armor, atlatl rigs, Tlaxcalan red and white still wet on three of them where the council's heralds had retouched the polity-glyphs to mark the new oath. Their cradles rolled into the Spanish yard at dusk. By full dark La Niña de Córdoba and one of the Camaxtli engines stood in adjacent cradles under the same canvas, perhaps eight feet apart, and the yard was very quiet, and Andrés could not sleep.

The two engines hummed against each other. He lay on his back on the pallet between their cradles and listened. The Camaxtli pilot, a man of about forty, scarred at the brow, who had given his name as Tepiltzin and would not look directly at Andrés, was on a pallet three paces away, and Tepiltzin was not sleeping either. The Camaxtli hum sat at a lower pitch, the way a stone hums in a well. La Niña's hum was higher, like a horse's teeth in the dark. The two tones beat against each other in a slow interference, and the canvas above moved a little with each beat, and Andrés did not know if the canvas was actually moving or if he was imagining it.

He had heard older adorators describe this, the way two Reliquaries placed in proximity began, after enough hours, to speak in their humming. They did not call it speech. They called it attendance. He understood now what the word meant, and he did not want to.

The copper disc was in his coat pocket. It had been there twenty-two days. He had not shown it to anyone, not Domingo, not the chaplain Bernardo, not in confession. It felt warm against his thigh. He told himself this was body heat through the cloth, and knew that was not the whole answer.

He thought about the dead pilot. He thought about him without knowing his name. He had asked, once, two days after, whether anyone in the Tlaxcalan column could give him the name of the kinsman of Xicotencatl the Younger he had taken down. The man he had asked had looked at him for a long second and walked away. He had not asked again.

Across the yard Tepiltzin shifted. The Camaxtli engine's hum dropped a half-step and came back. La Niña answered. The interference held. Tepiltzin spoke once, in Nahuatl, three syllables Andrés did not understand and would not ask anyone to translate. Whatever the words were, they were not directed at him. They were directed at the engine.

Andrés put his hand on the disc through the cloth of his coat. He thought: I will give this back.

Then he thought: to whom.

He had no answer for that, and the night was long, and the two engines hummed on, and near dawn he slept.
