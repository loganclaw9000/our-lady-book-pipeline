It rains at midnight. Not a storm, no thunder, nothing that would drive the canoes to the reeds. A cold rain that keeps on. The kind that soaks through and stays.

The Tlacopan causeway is four kilometers long. We have walked perhaps one. The column is long enough to stretch the whole way if the men were packed tight. They are not. Cortés has spread them by order, because the gold each man carries makes him slow, and a slow man on a causeway is a dead man, and a dead man does not carry gold out of this lake.

I am in *La Niña de Córdoba*. The engine walks. The gait is the saint's gait, the one she learned at nine years old, the one she walked playing in the streets of Córdoba before the Umayyad governor took her. Eight steps forward, pause, half-turn, two more. The cycle. Three years to internalize it so I do not have to think. The machine thinks for me. The hum is a tight thing against my spine, transmitted through the harness, through the vestments, through the white tunic and the child's rope belt. The bones of Flora are inside the monstrance. They are dry. They are warm. The world outside is wet.

Ahead of me there are eight other Reliquaries, plus three Tlaxcalan engines Cortés permitted to walk with us. Behind me there are more. I do not know how many. The torches are spaced by the foot soldiers, the ones not in engines, the ones who carry the planks. They flare and gutter. The distance between torches is about thirty yards. The gaps in the causeway are about eighty yards apart, each one a deliberate cut by Mexica engineers, each one crossed by a plank that a detail of men must lay and lift again and carry behind.

The first two gaps are behind us. The third is four hundred yards up. The bridge for it has not yet been laid. The detail is working. The men are soaked to the skin.

On either side of the causeway the lake is black. The torchlight throws a circle on the water, maybe twenty feet wide. In the circles I can see the canoes. Mexica canoes. Hollowed cedar, two or three men in each. They are close. They are moving. The first arrow hit *Santiago del Paso* an hour ago. The pilot is alive. The engine is limping. The second arrow will be worse.

I can hear them. Not from the causeway, from the water. The paddles. The creak of cedar. The hiss of an arrow leaving a bow. They are not shouting. They are professional.

*La Niña de Córdoba* hums. I am in the cockpit. The hatch is closed. The rain is on the outside. I feel the hum through the floor plates, through the soles of my boots. The machine is a girl. The machine is a child. The machine is a saint who died nine years old and will not stop walking because I will not stop walking with her.

I am the eighth engine in the line. The three Tlaxcalans are behind me. The five Spanish ahead are all second-class, all saints, all awake. *Santiago del Paso* is third. *San Cristóbal del Viento* is fifth. *Nuestra Señora del Campeón* is first, the expedition's Marian engine, the one Cortés rides himself. She is the biggest. She is the best. If she goes down, the column breaks.

The third gap comes. The bridge is laid. *Nuestra Señora* crosses first. Then the second engine. Then *Santiago*, limping, the arrow still in the shoulder plate. Then the fourth. Then *San Cristóbal*. Then the sixth. Then the seventh. Then me.

Behind me a Tlaxcalan engine sets a foot on the plank and the wood splits at the join and the engine is in the water before the pilot can speak the death-phrase. The lake takes her. The torches go out where she falls. The men below scream once and do not scream again.

I do not look back. Looking back is what kills you on a causeway.

The fourth gap is six hundred yards on. We move. The canoes move with us. The arrows come now in handfuls, five at a time, ten, aimed at the joints, the knee plates, the elbow seams, the small unarmored slits the saints leave open so the saints can breathe. *Santiago* takes one in the throat-grille and goes down on one knee and stays there. The pilot opens the chest hatch from inside and rolls into the rain. He is on foot now. He carries his monstrance under his arm. He runs.

I keep walking.

The fourth gap is not bridged. The detail that should have laid it is dead, two of them in the water, face down, the planks beside them. The men ahead of me jump. They jump with their gold. The gold pulls them down. I count six who go in and do not come up. *Nuestra Señora* steps across with one long stride because she is sixty feet and the gap is twenty.

The other engines cannot.

*San Cristóbal* tries. He puts a foot on the far edge and the stone gives, Mexica engineers cut these gaps to widen under weight, and he tilts sideways into the lake. The saint is awake when he sinks. I feel the silence inside my own prayer for half a second, and then it closes again.

Three of us left on the wrong side. The seventh, me, and the Tlaxcalan behind. The canoes know it.

The boulder comes from the south.

I do not see the engine that throws it. A Huitzilopochtli, only their casts can heave a stone that size from the water, the gilded shoulder plate breaks the dark for an instant a hundred yards out and is gone. The boulder is the size of a horse. It hits *La Niña* in the chest. It hits the monstrance.

The crack is small. The crack is enough.

I feel Flora go. Not silence this time. A small girl letting go of my hand in the dark. Eight years of her *vita*, daily, in Latin, and the last thing of her I have is the weight of a fingerbone leaving the seal. The hum stops. The engine stops. The engine falls.

I work the chest hatch from inside before the fall completes. The vestments catch on the latch and I rip them. I push off the chest plate as we tilt. I am in the water and she is going down beside me, her gilding catches a torch for one moment and is gone.

The bones of Flora of Córdoba are in the monstrance. The monstrance is cracked. The lake is forty feet deep here. The bones are not recoverable.

I drag. The water is cold past reckoning. I find the causeway stone with my hand and pull. I pull again. There is no boot on my left foot. I do not know when it left.

I get up and walk west.

I pass men I know. I do not know them tonight. I pass the body of a horse. I pass a Spanish boy of perhaps sixteen sitting upright with an arrow through his jaw, still trying to speak. I do not stop.

Near the third gap, the one we crossed cleanly an hour ago, in the time before Flora, I see Bernardo.

He is walking the wrong way. Against the flow. He is in his Franciscan habit, soaked black with rain and lake water, and he is carrying a Tlaxcalan teōmachtiani across his shoulders. The man is twice his weight. Bernardo is stumbling. The man's blood is on Bernardo's neck. Bernardo does not see me. He is looking for the next body, or the next gap, or the next thing he has decided he owes.

I watch him for three seconds.

I do not go to him. I do not call out. I do not turn back into the chaos to help him carry the man. I keep walking west.

I will remember the three seconds for the rest of my life.

At the Tlacopan shore there is a tree. Under the tree there is Cortés. He is sitting. His helmet is off. He is weeping, the noise is small, but it is weeping, and the men around him have arranged themselves so they do not hear it. *Nuestra Señora del Campeón* stands behind him in the grey rain, weeping also, the actual fluid running down her face plates. No one will discuss this in the morning.

I walk past him. Bootless, soaked, without a saint. He raises his head. Our eyes meet. I do not speak.

The rain keeps on. It is July 1, 1520, by an hour. The bones of the child of Córdoba are at the bottom of Lake Texcoco. The paint on her chassis will fade to grey over the next three days. No one has an agreed explanation. I will not be there to see it.

I walk west into Tlacopan.

Tlacopan is not a town tonight. It is a holding pen. The Tlaxcalans have given over three streets to the survivors and posted men at every junction with torches and instructions to shoot any Mexica who steps off the water. None do. The Mexica are not pursuing past the shore. They have what they wanted.

I sit against a wall in the third street.

My back is on the adobe and my legs are out in front of me and I have not moved in some time. The rain keeps on. A lay brother named Tomás comes by with a clay cup and tells me to drink it. I drink it. It is maize gruel with something else in it, salt, or fish, I cannot tell. He looks at my left foot, which is bare and bleeding from the causeway stone, and he says he will come back with cloth. He does not come back. Either he has forgotten me or he has found someone worse off. I assume the second.

The street is full of men I half-recognize. A sergeant from Olid's company, missing two fingers on the right hand. A boy I had thought was Andalusian who turns out to be Extremaduran. Two Tlaxcalan adoraters of an engine I did not know was on the causeway, sitting together, not speaking. One of them is holding a piece of painted wood in his lap. I think it is a fragment of his teōcalli-chest. I do not ask.

No one asks me anything either. I am a pilot without a saint, and word travels fast in a column even when the column has been gutted. Men know what the silence inside a prayer feels like even when they have never prayed. They saw the gilding on *La Niña* go down. They counted.

There is a clock in my head and it is broken. I cannot tell whether I have been sitting against the wall for an hour or for three. The torches do not help. The rain does not help. I am cold in a way that is no longer the cold of being wet. It is the cold of being still.

I think about the eight years.

I think about the morning the prior of Córdoba put the *vita* in my hands and told me to learn it before Lent. I was twenty-two. The pages were vellum and the script was a tight Carolingian minuscule and the prior said *the saint will know you when you know her*. I did not believe him. I learned the *vita* anyway. I learned the part where the governor's men come for her in the third hour and she does not weep, and the part where they offer her bread, and the part where she refuses it, and the part where she is nine years old and tired and she says the words she says before they do what they do. I held those words in my mouth every dawn for eight years. I held them while the engine walked. I held them tonight.

I do not have them anymore. The shape of them is in my mouth. The girl is not.

A man is shouting at the end of the street. The shouting is in Castilian. He is calling a name that is not mine. I do not stand up.

After some time, I will say it is two hours, but I do not know, Tomás comes back. He has cloth. He has also a pair of sandals, Tlaxcalan, woven from maguey fiber, that fit nobody well and me badly. He kneels and ties them on me without asking. He says, *Father Bernardo is on the causeway still. They have seen him. He is bringing them in.*

I close my eyes.

*Bringing them in*, in a sergeant's mouth, would mean retrieving the wounded. In Tomás's mouth it means something larger. Tomás is twenty and was a novice in Sevilla two years ago and he has not learned to be careful with words. I do not correct him.

*How many,* I ask.

*Four so far,* he says. *He goes back each time.*

I open my eyes. I look at the wall opposite. The adobe is the color of wet hide. There is a smear on it where someone has leaned and slid down.

I think about the three seconds.

I have not yet told myself the story of the three seconds. I will tell it many times later, in many rooms, to confessors and to no one, but tonight is the first time, and I find I cannot. The shape of it is too plain. He was walking the wrong way. He was carrying a man twice his weight. I watched. I did not go.

I tell myself that *La Niña* was still walking when I saw him. I tell myself I was eight engines deep into the column and could not have broken file. I tell myself the boulder had not yet come, and the monstrance had not yet cracked, and Flora was still warm in her seal, and a pilot in an awake engine does not leave the line for a friar on foot. All of these are true. None of them helps.

The truth is simpler. I watched for three seconds and I kept walking. The engine was not what kept me walking. I was.

I do not weep. I notice, with a small clinical curiosity, that I do not. I have wept in the sagrario twice in eight years, both times at the Offering, both times for reasons that did not survive the morning. Tonight I am dry. The lake took everything wet out of me on the way to shore.

The rain keeps on.

Near dawn the shouting at the end of the street changes shape. It is not a name now. It is a count. *Three more. Two of them ours.* A captain, I do not turn my head to see which, is making a list against a doorpost in charcoal. The line of marks is uneven because his hand is shaking. I do not blame him. I would not trust my hand with a charcoal stick tonight either.

Tomás comes a third time. He is breathing hard. He says, *Father Bernardo is in.*

I stand up.

Standing hurts in a way that surprises me. The maguey sandals do not hold. I walk to the end of the street with one hand on the wall. The captain at the doorpost does not look at me. The marks on the post number eleven for the Spanish and four for the Tlaxcalans and there is a separate cluster for the engines, which I do not count.

Bernardo is in the street with the wounded. He has set down a man, not the teōmachtiani I saw on the causeway, a different man, a Spanish crossbowman with a leg wrong at the knee, and he is on his knees beside him, working at the leg with both hands. His habit is no longer black with water. It is black with blood. His hair is matted to his scalp. He looks up. He sees me. He does not stop working.

I do not go to him. I stand at the edge of the torchlight and I watch.

After a while he ties the leg and stands and crosses to me. He is shorter than I am by a hand. He has always been shorter than I am by a hand. It does not feel like that tonight.

*Andrés,* he says.

*Bernardo.*

He looks at my hands. My hands are empty. He understands. He does not ask. He says, *the man I was carrying.*

*I saw you.*

*He is alive,* Bernardo says. *In the second house. He will not be alive by sunrise.*

I nod. I do not know what I am nodding to. I have not told him I saw him. I have told him I saw him. Both are true and the second is the one he will hold.

He looks at my feet. He looks at the sandals. He says, *come inside.*

I do not move. He waits. He has waited for me at doorways before, in cloisters, in libraries, with a question in his hand he was about to ask and would not ask until I was ready to hear it. He waits now in the same way. He does not know yet that I will not enter another sagrario. I do not know it either, in the words I will use for it later. But the shape of the decision is already in me, sitting under the wet tunic where Flora used to hum, and Bernardo is the first person to stand close enough to feel it without being told.

The rain keeps on. The captain at the doorpost makes another mark.

I go inside with him.

<!-- scene: ch17_sc03 -->
The rain has not stopped. Six hours of it, and the cold is in my bones the way old water is in old wood, not on me but through me, lodged somewhere it will not leave for two days.

I am sitting twenty yards past the tree where Cortés stopped. My left boot is gone. The skin of that foot is red and the grass is full of small roots that I can feel one by one. Behind me, the column is forming. Men wake the way the half-drowned wake, slowly and angry. Somewhere a captain is counting torches that have already gone out and getting the wrong number.

We lost four Reliquaries on the causeway.

La Niña de Córdoba. San Cristóbal del Viento. Two third-class without names, knight engines, fifteen-foot infantry frames carried by lay riders. San Ildefonso del Mar was already gone before any of this, lost in the storm off Cozumel in February of last year, and its relic was being reconsecrated in Tlaxcala when we marched. They will rebind it before winter. They will call it San Esteban del Río. That will be the fifth. I have been told it will be mine. I have not answered.

The ones left, Nuestra Señora del Campeón, the Marian Cortés rides. Santiago del Paso, eleven stories, Sandoval's, the only first-class we have ever fielded. San Sebastián de Cuba, Ordaz's, dependable as a hammer. Santiago del Alba, Alvarado's, which Alvarado polished himself even at the gallop. Two third-class with no names of their own. Two Tlaxcalan engines, Teōmecahuītlī, that came across with us. Eight standing. Four down. Three knights from the third-class line dragged themselves out of the lake without their engines and are still alive, which the chaplains will count as a small mercy and which the men in question will count as the worst thing that has ever happened to them.

Two-thirds of the expedition is dead. The count will come later. I know the number because I counted the torches going out.

Bernardo is alive. He is somewhere in the column behind me. I saw him on the causeway during the last hour of it, walking the wrong way against the flow, shouldering a Tlaxcalan teōmachtiani who was twice his weight and bleeding from somewhere under the arm. I watched him for three seconds and kept moving. I will remember those three seconds. He will not speak of them. He has never raised his hand to anyone and he has never been inside a cockpit and he was carrying a man on his back across a causeway that the Mexica had cut into pieces, and I walked past him.

Malintzin is alive. She is somewhere in the column too. She will be wet and she will be tired and she will be speaking to three men at once in three languages none of them understand fully, and none of them will be weeping in front of her, because she is not.

I am not weeping either. I am not anything yet.

The monstrance cracked at the third hour of the retreat, when the boulder came over the parapet from a Huitzilopochtli engine on the lake side. I felt it. The bones of Flora of Córdoba were bound in a casing of silver and beaten gold the size of a man's chest, and when the silver split I felt her let go the way a hand lets go of another hand. Not violence. Permission. She had been with me for nine years. The childhood cycle, the catechism, the refusal, the death, the ascension, I knew her the way you know the rooms of your mother's house. I had her saint's life memorized before I had my own.

She is at the bottom of Lake Texcoco now. Her chassis is on its side in the silt with the gilding starting to lift. The paint will go to grey within a year. The bones will outlast the metal. They will outlast me. They will not be recovered.

I have not prayed since the boulder hit. I do not know what I am without the cycle. A Reliquary pilot is not a man with a prayer; he is a prayer that happens to have a man inside it. Take the prayer out and what sits past Cortés's tree at dawn is something I do not have a word for yet.

The column is forming. The dead are being stacked under oilcloth. The wounded are being walked or carried. Somebody, Olid, by the voice, is shouting at the porters about the gold, because we still have some of it, and the men who carried it across are the ones who came out alive, which Olid is taking as evidence of providence and which the men taking it on their backs are taking as evidence of nothing.

Cortés has stood up from under his tree. He is walking toward the column. He will speak in a moment. He will say what is true, because he is good at that, and the truth he picks will be the one that gets us moving north toward Tlaxcala by noon. He will not mention the Reliquaries by name. He never does. He will say "the saints stayed with us" and a hundred and seventy-some surviving men will believe him and the four of us still able to pilot will know what he meant and what he did not mean.

He passes me. Six feet away. He looks at me for one second and does not stop. The look is not unkind. The look is a man counting a horse and noting it is still a horse. I do not stand. I do not salute. He keeps walking. That, too, is a kind of mercy.

The rain slacks. Not stops, slacks. The drops are smaller and farther between, and there is a paleness on the eastern edge of the trees that is not yet light but is the rumor of it. I can hear, from somewhere down the line, a horse being shot because it cannot walk. I can hear the chaplain Olmedo praying in Latin over a man who is past Latin. I can hear Malintzin, very faint, in Nahuatl, and a Tlaxcalan voice answering her, and then both of them in Spanish, agreeing on the road north.

Somewhere behind me a Tlaxcalan engine, one of the two left to us, takes a step. The ground moves under my unbooted foot in a way I know by feel, the way a sailor knows a deck. Teōmecahuītlī walks differently than a Reliquary; the pulse of it under the soil is faster and more even, no procession-tempo, no martyr-cycle. I have learned to feel the difference. I am still feeling it. That is something.

I sit. The cold is still in me. It will be in me through tomorrow and most of the day after. I am bootless on the left foot and the toes are not all the right colors. None of that is going to kill me. None of that is the thing.

The thing is that there is nothing on the inside of my chest where the cycle was. Not absence in the sentimental sense. Absence the way a room is absent of furniture after the fire. The walls are still there. The walls are charred. Somebody will put new furniture in eventually. It will not be the same furniture and the room will not be the same room and the person sitting in it will not be the same person, and I do not know yet whether that person will be a man I would have wanted to be.

San Esteban del Río. The name is already in my mouth and I have not agreed to it. The relic is the same relic, a fragment of arrow shaft, a child's tooth, a knuckle, I have not been told which, pulled from the wreck off Cozumel and carried in oilcloth across the water and over the mountains to Tlaxcala. They will bind it to a new chassis. They will call it a new saint. They will hand me the cockpit key and look at me and wait.

I have not decided what my face will do.

The first true light is in the trees now. Pale, eastern, watery. Cortés has reached the head of the column. He is speaking. I cannot hear the words from where I sit and I do not need to. I know his cadences the way I knew Flora's. Childhood, faith, refusal, death, ascension. He will hit four of the five. He always skips the fourth.

I get up.

It surprises me. My right boot is full of water and my left foot will not take the weight cleanly and I get up anyway, because the column is moving and because Bernardo is in it and because Malintzin is in it and because I have not yet found the word for what I am, and a man without a word for himself ought at least to be on his feet while he looks for one.

I walk toward the column. The grass under my bare foot is cold and full of roots. The rain has nearly stopped. The lake at my back is grey and very still and is keeping its dead.

I do not look back.
