Dawn broke over Tenochtitlan in thin gold, the light of a high-altitude morning, the light of a city that had burned for seventy-five days. The Templo Mayor's lower platforms were rubble, their stepped geometry shelled into ramps of broken stone, but the pyramid still rose. The Great Engine of the sun-god sat in her cradle at the base, hum at half-state, dark-eyed, immense. Sixty meters of stepped basalt, taller than most cathedrals in Castile, and at her foot a stone block forty paces wide where the linen cloth had been laid.

*San Esteban del Río* stood at the far end of the plaza, hatch open. Andrés was in her cockpit, face visible to the men below, eyes on the figure walking toward them. Bernardo came alone, in his ordinary brown habit, the Franciscan cord knotted at his waist. He carried nothing. He had already given his last confession to the senior Franciscan, who waited now beside the monstrance bracket, the bracket open, the linen cloth laid out on the stone, the bone-chisels arranged in their nine-piece order on a strip of unbleached linen.

Andrés watched him. He had killed a stranger in his first chapter. He was about to kill a friend in this one.

The witnesses had formed a circle. Not concealed. Cortés and his captains. Ixtlilxochitl and four Texcocan nobles in their feather-cloaks. A knot of Tlaxcalan war chiefs. Itzcoatl in *Obsidian Mirror's* cradle to one side, hatch open, watching. Malintzin standing where Bernardo could see her and where she could see Bernardo. Three Franciscan brothers in their habits. The *San Esteban* adorators with their tools laid bare.

Three engine-hums interfered in the air over the plaza. Andrés felt the pattern against his breastbone, a slow beat under the higher harmonic, the way thunder is felt before it is heard. Across the circle a Tlaxcalan chief shifted his stance and laid a hand flat on his sternum. The Franciscan brothers held still and did not look at one another. Whatever they were feeling, they did not name it.

Bernardo stopped ten paces from the stone block. He looked at the senior Franciscan. He looked at Andrés. He looked at Malintzin. He said nothing.

The senior Franciscan spoke the prayer for the martyr, in Latin, in the form the friars had carried out of Córdoba seven centuries before. Bernardo knelt. The prayer finished. The Franciscan raised his hand.

Bernardo stood. He walked forward. He knelt before the stone block and placed his hands on the linen cloth. He spoke aloud the words he had said the night before to Malintzin and to Andrés, in private, in the chapel of the siege camp.

*I know what I have to do. I know what you have to do.*

Then, louder, so the circle could hear: *Her name was Flora. She was nine. She refused to recant in Córdoba in the year of our Lord 851, and they took her head for it. Her bones powered* La Niña *in this man's hands for two years. This is what she was, and I choose it.*

He held out his wrists. Andrés stepped from the cockpit and crossed the plaza. He untied the Franciscan cord at Bernardo's waist and bound the wrists with it himself, the knot the friars taught novices, three turns and a single hitch. The saint of radical poverty and nonviolence, made the ritual instrument of a war engine's fuel. Bernardo watched the knot tighten. He said it aloud, for the record: *San Francisco. Forgive me. Forgive him.*

Andrés drew the Toledo short-sword from his hip. His own blade, blessed in Havana before the crossing, carried through Cempoala and Cholula and the Noche Triste causeway, never used for an execution before this dawn. The senior adorator stepped back from the chisels and opened a hand toward the stone block, ceding the ground. The kill was the pilot's. The bracket-rite came after, the chisels were the tooling that fused warm bone to monstrance, not the instrument that ended the man. The two were separate operations; the rite required they stay separate, by a margin of seconds the senior adorator was paid to keep narrow.

Andrés could feel *San Esteban's* hum in the steel of the sword's tang, in the soles of his boots, in the bones of his own jaw. He could feel the saint's arm in the monstrance three paces behind him. He had used up whatever inside him could weep. There was nothing left to spend on the moment, and he was glad of it, because the cut had to be quick and the bones had to be warm.

The senior Franciscan spoke the name aloud, so every pilot and soldier within earshot could hear it. *Flora of Córdoba.* Bernardo's eyes were open. He nodded once.

Andrés made the cut. A single transverse stroke at the base of the skull. Bernardo went forward onto the linen cloth and did not move again.

The senior adorator was already at the stone block. He worked with the speed and economy of a man who had done this nineteen times in his life and had been told twice the bracket-rite must not exceed three minutes if the bones were to enter warm. Right humerus. Lower jaw. Two cervical vertebrae. Each piece lifted, set on the linen, wiped, carried the four paces to the monstrance bracket and laid alongside the arm of San Esteban de Granada. The senior Franciscan said the sealing rite over the bracket in older, harder Mozarabic Latin, rapid-fire, the words the Reconquista friars had used to fuse the bones of their own dead into the engines that took Granada in 1491. The bracket closed. The seal was struck. The monstrance whined up through three octaves in under a breath.

*San Esteban del Río* rose.

What happened in the next hour was witnessed by thousands and recorded in three chronicles that disagree on every detail except the central one. The dual-saint configuration drove the engine to apex-equivalent output. It crossed the plaza. The Great Engine of the sun-god, half-awake in her cradle, lifted to meet it. They struck once at the foot of the pyramid. The shock split paving-stones from the calmecac wall to the ball court. They struck a second time on the lower terrace. *San Esteban* opened the Great Engine's chest seam with the cut Bernardo's bones had bought, and the sun-god went down across her own steps, and *San Esteban* went down beside her, and the upper shrines of Huitzilopochtli and Tlaloc folded inward in a slow column of dust that took the shape, briefly, of a man.

Cuauhtémoc, escaping by canoe across the southeast canal toward Iztapalapa, was overtaken by Sandoval's brigantine and brought back. By the second hour of afternoon the city was taken.

Andrés was pulled from the wreckage with two cracked ribs and a burn across his left forearm. He did not weep then either. He had used up whatever inside him could, and what was left was something colder, more useful, that he would carry for the rest of his life and never name.

They sat him down on a fallen step at the base of the calmecac wall. The plaza was loud, then it was not. He kept his hands on his knees. The cord at Bernardo's wrists was the one knot in the entire morning he had tied with his own fingers, and he could still feel the friction of it in his palms. He did not look toward the body. That hour belonged to someone else.

The plaza was no longer quiet. The first hour after the duel sounded like an assembly line of small noises, collapsing masonry working its way down through itself, men coughing dust, a Tlaxcalan officer somewhere calling out grid numbers for the pyramid's debris field. Andrés heard it from the wreckage of *San Esteban del Río*, where he had been pinned for thirty-seven minutes between the cockpit door and the warped knee-hinge before two soldiers cut the door free and pulled him out by the armpits.

He came down the broken ladder on his own. Two cracked ribs, a burn the length of his left forearm, a sprained ankle he chose not to limp on. The hour was over. The fused bones in the bracket had cooled. *San Esteban* would not move again today, perhaps not ever. He nodded to the soldiers and walked.

Bernardo's body lay where it had been left, on the linen, hands still bound in his own Franciscan cord. The senior friar of the expedition knelt beside him, prayer-book open, the flat afternoon light coming over the templo wall and falling across the page. Andrés did not approach. The friar had begun, and the form was already chosen.

It was the office for confessors. Not the office for martyrs. Andrés caught the form in the second clause and stayed where he was. The friar was declining to rule on what Bernardo had been at the moment of his death. He was passing the case up to Rome. Rome, Andrés thought, would not answer; and from the cadence of the Latin he was hearing, the friar was not waiting for one. The Church would receive the file and shelve it in a quiet place for a hundred years.

Across the plaza, the cradle of *Obsidian Mirror* sat low against the calmecac wall, where Itzcoatl had walked her after the second strike and dismounted. Andrés had not seen him climb out. The hatch was closed now and unmoving, and it would stay that way for the rest of the afternoon and most of the next day. Andrés did not begrudge him.

Sandoval came up the south ramp at the head of a small column. Behind him, two Spanish soldiers walked a young man between them with his wrists tied in front, water still beading down the ankles of his cotton kilt. Andrés had been a soldier for nine years and recognized the gait of someone who had not yet been struck. Cuauhtémoc had been overtaken in his canoe along the southeast canal toward Iztapalapa and brought back without injury. Sandoval had been clear, as he was always clear, that the prisoner was to be delivered intact.

Cortés stood waiting at the base of the broken steps. Malintzin stood at his shoulder, half a pace back, where she always stood when this kind of conversation was about to begin. She did not look at Andrés. She looked at the prisoner. The prisoner looked at her.

Andrés did not stay for the exchange. He had heard enough exchanges of that shape across two years to know what the first ten minutes of one sounded like, and what they were for. He turned and walked the long way back across the plaza, past the linen, past the friar still reading, past the cooling foot of his own engine, and stopped at the base of the cradle ladder.

He climbed it. The rungs were crimped where the duel had wrenched the frame. He used his good hand. The cockpit smelled of his own blood and of *San Esteban*'s broken coolant, a sweet metallic smell the manuals warned about and never described. He sat in the seat. The seat was cold. The hum was gone. The bracket at the chest, behind the crystal, held a small dark shape that had been Bernardo's lower jaw an hour ago and was now the heart of a machine that would not move again.

He prayed the *vita* of San Esteban del Río. He had prayed it every morning since his investiture, and the Latin was so worn into the inside of his mouth that the words came without his consent. He went through the whole *vita* and found nothing in it that opened. Then the *vita* of Flora of Córdoba, which had been the morning before that one for eight years, and which he had not been able to pray since June. The Latin came. The girl did not. Then he tried to pray the *vita* of Bernardo, and it was not written, and he assembled it himself out of the eleven months he had known the man, line by line, in bad seminary Latin, in the cooling cockpit of an engine that had killed a god.

When he was done he sat for a long time. The sun moved across the cracked windscreen. He did not weep. He had used up whatever inside him could weep. What was left was something colder and more useful, and he understood already that he would carry it the rest of his life and never name it.

He climbed down.

Malintzin was waiting at the foot of the ladder. She had taken a corner of the linen, not the part that had Bernardo's blood on it but the part that had only the dust of the plaza and the morning's incense, and folded it into a square the size of a child's hand and put it inside her sash. She had walked here from where Cortés and the prisoner were still talking. No one had stopped her. No one had asked her what the linen was for.

Andrés said it in Spanish, because Spanish was the cleanest language for the thing he had to say: *Her name was Flora. The man on the linen was my friend. I will not tell you which of those two facts will keep me awake.*

She listened to the whole sentence before she answered. When she answered she answered in Nahuatl, and the choice of Nahuatl was the answer's first half. *You did not ask me which,* she said. *That is the part of the answer I am willing to give you today.*

She turned and walked back across the plaza toward Cortés and the prisoner.

Andrés watched her go. He did not follow.

The city was taken. The afternoon would be long. The friar's voice across the plaza had moved to the closing antiphon, no louder now than the wind through the templo's broken upper screen. Andrés stood at the foot of his fallen engine and waited for someone to come and tell him what to do next, because today, for the first time in eight years, he could not think of it himself.

The second hour of afternoon. The city taken.

Andrés sat on the broken shoulder of *San Esteban del Río* and counted the things he could still feel. The bench under him, hot from the sun and from what had happened in the engine three hours earlier. The cord at his belt where he had cut a length for Bernardo's hands and not yet replaced. The salt at the corner of his mouth, which was sweat, not weeping. He had checked.

Below him the plaza was being inventoried. Tlaxcalans worked the southern edge with notched poles, separating Mexica dead by caste, eagles to one row, jaguars to another, calmecac priests to a third, for the engine corps officers to walk later and identify. Cortés had ordered the dead counted before they were burned. He wanted a number to write to the Emperor. Cortés always wanted a number.

The Great Engine of the Templo Mayor lay across her own cradle in two hundred tons of basalt and iron. The upper shrine of Huitzilopochtli had folded forward when she went down; the shrine of Tlaloc had folded backward. The two halves of the temple lay on opposite sides of the broken pyramid like the two leaves of a book that had been closed too hard. A Franciscan brother walked the rim of the ruin with a censer and a phrasebook, doing what could be done.

Sandoval's brigantine had brought Cuauhtémoc to the foot of the south causeway. Andrés had not been there for it. The runner who climbed the steps to tell him said the *tlatoani* had not resisted, had stepped from the canoe onto the flagstones with both hands open at his sides, and had said, in Nahuatl that Malintzin had translated on the spot: *I have done what I was given to do. Take what is left of the city. Do not torture me for the gold; there is none.* Cortés had embraced him. The runner reported the embrace as if it had been the strangest part of the morning, which in his counting it perhaps was.

They had taken Cuauhtémoc to Coyoacán under guard. Andrés would not see him. Andrés would never see him. There were ranks at which the war stopped being a thing pilots watched, and his rank was below that line.

He climbed down from the engine. His knees did not want to do it. He let them not want to and made them do it anyway.

Bernardo's body had been moved, in the hour he had been sitting in the cockpit, from the stone block to a litter inside the calmecac. He went to find it. The senior Franciscan was already there, kneeling at the head of the litter, reading from the breviary in a voice gone thin from morning use. Andrés recognized the form before the friar reached the third antiphon: the office for confessors. Not the office for martyrs. The friar was not going to rule. He was leaving the ruling to Rome.

Andrés stood at the foot of the litter for the length of the office. He did not kneel. He did not know whether kneeling would have been a lie or the only true thing left to him, and he was tired of choosing wrong, so he stood.

The cord was still on Bernardo's wrists. Someone had washed the linen at the throat, and the blood there had gone the colour of old wine. The hands that had bound themselves were folded now in the way the friars folded the hands of any dead brother, right over left, thumbs crossed. The friar had not removed the cord to do it. He had folded the hands around the cord, which Andrés understood to be a small private theology of the friar's own, and not for him to question.

When the office ended the friar closed the breviary, set his palm briefly on the crown of Bernardo's head, and stood. He looked at Andrés across the litter. He did not speak. After a moment he walked out of the calmecac and into the smoke of the plaza and was gone.

Itzcoatl came down from *Obsidian Mirror* an hour after that. Andrés watched him cross the plaza on foot, alone, the engine left open behind him in its cradle in a posture Andrés had never seen a Texcocan engine left in, hatch up, hands at rest on the knees, head bowed three degrees. Itzcoatl came as far as the threshold of the calmecac, looked once at the litter, did not enter, and walked back to his cradle. He climbed in. He closed the hatch. The cradle did not move again that day.

Malintzin he found at dusk, on the lower terrace of the broken pyramid, where she had stood through the fight. She had a folded square of linen in her sash. He had seen her take it from the corner of the cloth that lay under Bernardo's head, before the body was moved, when she thought no one was watching. He had been watching. He did not know why she had taken it and he was not going to ask.

He stopped at three paces. He did not know what he had walked over to her to say. He had thought it would come to him on the way and it had not.

She looked at him. She waited the way she waited for Cortés to finish a sentence, which was the way she waited for any man who was not yet sure what he was about to say.

He said, after a moment, in Spanish: *I do not know what I am.*

She said, in Nahuatl, which he understood now after two years of her voice in his ear at every parley: *No one does, on this day. Tomorrow some of us will choose. Some of us will not.*

She did not say which she would be. She did not say which she expected him to be. She turned, after a count of three, and walked down the terrace toward the lake, where the brigantines were taking on the wounded.

Andrés stood on the terrace until the sun cleared the western shore. Then he walked down to the lake himself, because there was nothing left at the top of the pyramid that wanted him there, and because the wounded would need a man who could read Latin over them, and that, at least, was a thing he was still permitted to do.
