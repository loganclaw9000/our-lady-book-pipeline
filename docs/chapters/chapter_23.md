The road down the pass was a switchback cut into the side of the volcano, and *Obsidian Mirror* did not hurry. The engine's hum was a constant, a note that lived in Itzcoatl's bones and had lived there for twenty years. He could feel the weight of her through the pilot-chair, the deliberate placement of each foot, the scrape of obsidian pad against volcanic rock. The Spanish had made a cradle for her at the bottom of the road. It was wrong. The geometry of it was wrong. A Tezcatlipoca engine does not rest on flat stone. It rests on shadow.

The pikemen stared. He saw them through the engine's mirror-face, reflected back at him distorted. Thirty men in steel, halberds crossed, eyes fixed on the fifty feet of basalt that walked past them under its own power. An enemy god. A Mexica engine. Itzcoatl's engine. The hum dropped a quarter-tone as he passed the first line of pikemen.

The adorator in the white tunic crossed himself. The gesture was mechanical, the fingers moving over chest and shoulders in the Latin sequence. Itzcoatl did not cross himself. He had never been baptized. He would not be baptized. He had come over. He had walked down the road. That was enough.

The cradle was prepared by the lake, a depression in the ground lined with rough-hewn stone. When *Obsidian Mirror* stepped into it, the obsidian pads scraped on the wrong surface, and Itzcoatl felt the wrongness through his whole body. The Spanish did not know engine geometry. They had never built a Teōmecahuītlī. They had never housed a god. They had built a pit and hoped the engine would fit.

He climbed down through the chest hatch. The air in the cockpit was always warm, the smell of burnt offering and old blood. He stepped out onto the Spanish ground and stood in the shadow of his own machine, a man in obsidian-scale armor, a man who had walked for twenty years as a god and now stood among men who had only just begun to understand what that meant.

Cortés came out from the tents. Malintzin was half a step behind him, as always. Itzcoatl recognized her from the causeway in Tlaxcala, from the day the alliance had been sealed and the engines had first walked side by side. Her face did not change. She looked at him the way you look at a thing you already know will happen.

The exchange was short. Cortés spoke. Malintzin translated. Itzcoatl pledged his engines, his men, his Texcocan retinue to the Spanish command. Cortés accepted. The gesture of the crucifix was small, a silver cross laid briefly against the flank of *Obsidian Mirror*, and Itzcoatl permitted it without comment, though the engine's hum dropped another quarter-tone for a beat and then returned.

He did not speak of his sister. Malintzin did not speak of his sister. Both of them knew the other knew. The compact held.

The Spanish had built a stage for him, a platform of rough planks where he would make his formal declaration. He stood on it. He looked out over the staging ground. Thousands of indigenous porters were moving the brigantine pieces down the pass, the great ships of Lake Texcoco disassembled into sections and loaded onto human backs. The Spanish would reassemble them on the lake, and the ring would close around Tenochtitlan.

Ixtlilxochitl stood to the left of the stage, in the Texcocan contingent. He had already converted. He had already paid the price. He wore a Spanish cloak over his Mexica tunic, and his engine *Sun-That-Answers* stood behind him, a Huitzilopochtli-caste giant in the employ of the man who would conquer his own people. The engine was painted with Spanish heraldry over the Mexica glyphs. Itzcoatl looked at it for a long moment. He did not look away.

He had felt the Tlaxcalan defection, two years gone, as a betrayal. He had watched the alliance form on the high plain and felt the old house cracking. Now the house was his own. He had walked down the road. He had stood on the stage. He had pledged his engines to the men who would conquer the city he had been born to serve.

The hum of *Obsidian Mirror* behind him was the only thing that was constant. He had housed Tezcatlipoca for twenty years. He knew what the god wanted. The god wanted shadow. The god wanted edges. The god wanted the old house to break.

He had come to help it break.

The Spanish would reassemble the brigantines. The ring would close. The siege would begin. And he would walk with them into the plaza of the Templo Mayor, a Mexica engine in a Christian war, a god on the wrong side, and he would not speak of his sister, and he would not speak of what he was doing, and he would not speak at all.

The hum held. He stood on the stage. He said nothing. He had said enough.

The staging ground at the foot of the pass was a place of overlays. Spanish tents on Mexica ground. Christian crosses over Tlaloc glyphs. Brigantine pieces hauled by Tlaxcalan porters down a road cut by Mexica engineers. Itzcoatl had walked into it and felt every overlay, every wrongness, every deliberate misalignment of the two cosmologies now pressed together at a point of force.

He had come over. The defection was complete. He would not go back. He would not go forward. He would stand on the stage, in the shadow of *Obsidian Mirror*, and he would do what he had always done: house the god, keep the hum, walk the road.

The hum held. It was the only thing that held.

He looked at Ixtlilxochitl, who was looking back at him, and he saw what he had to see: the mirror-cousin who had chosen early and won, the mirror-cousin who had chosen late and survived, the mirror-cousin who would be, after the siege, the man who would fund the Spanish church and the man who would watch it being built, and the man who would never speak of his sister.

The hum held.

He stepped off the stage. He walked back to *Obsidian Mirror*. He climbed into the cockpit. He sat in the pilot-chair. The hum returned to him.

They would move the brigantines. They would reassemble the ships. They would close the ring. He would walk with them. He would not speak.

The hum held.

It held.

The Spanish breastplate sat wrong on his shoulders. An armorer in Tlaxcala had hammered it down to his frame over two afternoons last week and pronounced it good. It was not good. The plate met his collarbone where Mexica cotton would have met his clavicle three fingers lower, and the steel rim cut a thin line of pressure across the soft place above the bone, and he had been riding into that pressure since dawn, a small wrongness, one among many, that his body refused to absorb and his mind refused to stop noting.

Behind him, *Obsidian Mirror*'s hum carried at the pitch it had carried for twenty years. Half a quarter-step off it, walking behind and to the left, the dissonance of Ixtlilxochitl's giant. Huitzilopochtli-caste. The two engines could not harmonize and would not, and the air between them carried a pressure he could feel against the side of his face like a stranger standing too close.

His horse's hooves struck the causeway wrong. The stone had been laid by Mexica engineers across the shallow north of Lake Texcoco, generations of work, set for human feet and engine pads, never for shod animals. Each hoof clicked off the joints between blocks at the wrong tempo. He was riding a Spanish horse in Spanish steel under a Spanish banner, and the road beneath him answered each step with a sound that meant: *not this*.

Ixtlilxochitl rode at his right. Spanish cloak pinned at the shoulder. Texcocan undertunic showing at the wrist, a small concession to the men who would line both shores of the city when they reached it. He was speaking, and had been speaking since the column had stepped onto the northern causeway an hour past dawn.

He spoke of reconstruction.

He spoke of a Texcoco rebuilt around a Spanish stone church on the foundation of the old precinct, the same masons, he said, would lay the new stones; nothing of the craft itself would be lost. He spoke of a Latin school. Texcocan boys reading Cicero. The library Nezahualpilli had kept would not be burned, he had a written promise on that, signed.

He spoke as a man who had finished a thing.

He had been baptized last autumn, in some chapel in Tlaxcala that Itzcoatl had not seen, on a schedule Itzcoatl had not been invited to keep. He had grieved in private. By the time the cousins met again that winter in the Tlaxcalan compound, the choice had already been a closed thing inside him. Don Fernando, since then. Gracious, since then. Untroubled, by every visible measure, since then.

His calm was not cruelty. That was the worst of it. The calm was its own sealed thing, complete in itself, and to stand beside it while not yet finished was to be measured by a yardstick that did not see you.

Itzcoatl could not speak.

The lake lay on both sides. To the south, on the island, the geometry of the city still held: the twin shrines of the Templo Mayor visible above the rooflines, the long lines of the chinampas reading green even from this distance, the canals catching morning light in straight cuts. To the north of the island, smoke. The Tlaxcalan vanguard had been at the suburbs since yesterday, and the wind off the lake brought the smell of burnt thatch and burnt copal and, faintly, something animal he chose not to name.

Behind him, *Obsidian Mirror* walked on her own legs.

He could feel her through the pilot-chair even from outside it, the deliberate placement of each foot, the scrape of obsidian pad against laid stone, the half-pause she made at every joint between blocks because the deity preferred edges to flats. Her hum sat in his marrow at the pitch it had sat for two decades, the pitch he had learned in his fifteenth year in the engine-yard at Texcoco, the pitch he had carried unbroken through Toxcatl and the Noche Triste and the long winter in Tlaxcala. It had not changed. The world around it had changed, and her hum had not, and that was what made the dissonance with the Huitzilopochtli engine so difficult to bear: the wrongness was not in his engine. The wrongness was in the field she now walked through.

Behind her, the giant.

Ixtlilxochitl's deliberate provocation. The patron god of the Mexica, piloted in a Texcocan engine cast for him a generation ago by a smith long since dead, walking now toward the Mexica capital on the Spanish side of the line. Two engines beside her of lesser scale, both Texcocan. Behind those, fifty thousand warriors in column, mostly Texcocan and Tlaxcalan, the number Itzcoatl had himself counted twice because he had not believed it the first time. Behind the column, somewhere out on the water already, the brigantines: thirteen of them, hauled in sections over the Paso de Cortés on the backs of porters whose names no one would write down, and reassembled at Texcoco's shore in a yard that smelled of pitch and resined wood. The ring was closing.

", and the canal grid, of course, will need re-cutting in the southern wards," Ixtlilxochitl was saying. "Sandoval's engineers have already drawn it. They asked me to mark which of the older lines to keep. I marked the ones our grandfather had widened."

He said *our grandfather* lightly. The way a man says a thing he has thought through and decided he is allowed to say.

Itzcoatl looked at him.

Looked at the Spanish cloak, at the small enameled cross at the throat, at the face he had grown up across a courtyard from and had wrestled with at eight and had read codices with at twelve and had drunk pulque with at sixteen on the night before the cousin had left for the north and the long succession quarrel that had ended with another half-brother on the Texcocan throne. The face had not changed. The face had simply finished a journey.

"The grandfather's lines were good," Itzcoatl said.

Three words. Four. In Nahuatl, dragged up out of him by the small concrete fact of canals and his cousin's hand having marked them.

Ixtlilxochitl turned his head. He did not smile, which was a kindness; a smile would have been unbearable. He nodded, once.

"Yes," he said. "They were."

He went on, after a pause, about the masons.

Itzcoatl let the words pass over him. He let his eyes go forward, to the line of the causeway running in stone toward the island, to the smoke, to the temples he had been raised to believe were the axis of the world and that he was now riding toward as a part of the army that would unmake them. He let *Mirror*'s hum sit in his bones at the pitch it had always sat. He let the dissonance with the Huitzilopochtli engine be what it was, a pressure against the cheek, a faint nausea at the jaw, a thing he was now required to live inside.

He had pledged his engines. Last month, in the great courtyard at Tlaxcala, in front of Cortés and Olid and Alvarado and the Tlaxcalan lords, with Malintzin two paces back of Cortés translating what did not need translating because everyone understood the shape of the thing already. The pledge was formal. It was on paper. It was witnessed.

It was not finished inside him.

He suspected, riding now with his cousin's clean voice at his right and the city he had been born to defend at the southern end of the stone road, that it would never be finished inside him. He would do the work. He would walk *Mirror* into the breach when the order came. He would point her obsidian hand at men he had trained beside and bring it down. He would survive, probably. He would live in the new Texcoco his cousin was building, probably. The small unfinished thing in him would walk beside the work for the rest of his life, half a quarter-step off the note, never harmonizing.

That was, he realized, exactly what Ixtlilxochitl had not said.

The cousin who had finished the choice early, and who had said so all morning in many different gracious phrasings, had not once claimed that the grief had ended. Only that he was past it. There was a distance between those claims. Itzcoatl, not yet past anything, could see it now from the wrong side.

He turned his face toward the city. The smoke from the northern wards had thickened to a band. *Mirror* set another foot down behind him; the obsidian pad scraped the joint and held.

He decided, without speaking, that he would learn to live half a quarter-step off the note. He would not pretend to harmonize. He would walk the engine he had piloted for twenty years into the work that was coming, and he would not lie to her about what they were doing.

It was not enough. It was the only honest thing he had left.

Ixtlilxochitl was speaking of the new bells.

The hum from across the water reached him as a thread, not a presence. Two miles of open lake between Mirror's cradle and the Templo Mayor, and the Great Engine's idle pitch arrived at his hatch so attenuated it could be mistaken for wind in the canvas. He had been listening to it since before first light. Half-awake. The way a sleeping animal breathes, audible if you knew to listen for it, inaudible to anyone who did not.

Fifty thousand warriors slept behind him in the rows of twelve the Spanish quartermasters had laid out, canvas and ash anchored in mud, cooking fires at thirty-pace intervals already going cold. Beyond the picket the brigantines floated at their moorings, thirteen of them, masts black against the paling sky, the lake holding them with the indifference of water that had been holding boats and corpses and floating gardens for as long as the city had existed.

Mirror sat in her cradle on a platform of laid logs at the camp's western edge, hatch up, hands at rest on her knees. He had taken the dawn watch himself. The adorators slept; the apprentice who had polished the chest-disc the previous evening had been sent to the Franciscan tent for a poultice on his hand. Itzcoatl sat in the open cockpit with his ankles crossed against the rim and listened.

Mirror's own hum sat under him at the pitch he had learned at fifteen in the engine-yard at Texcoco, the pitch that had carried him through Toxcatl and the Noche Triste and the long winter at Tlaxcala. The Great Engine's hum, two miles off, sat a sixth above. Not dissonant. Not consonant. The interval was old. Tezcatlipoca and Huitzilopochtli were brothers in the order of the world, which was not the same as harmony, and the engines remembered what the men who had built them had known.

The wind shifted and the smell of copal came across the water for the third time that hour. The Mexica priests were at their dawn rites. Whoever was left of them. He had not let himself count.

Footsteps on the log platform.

He did not turn his head. He had heard the rhythm of his cousin's stride on stone and on clay since both of them had been small enough to fit under the same blanket during a court reception in Texcoco at which neither had been wanted on the floor.

"You're up early," Ixtlilxochitl said. Castilian. He used it in camp now, even in private. The discipline of the choice.

"I have been up."

"For how long."

"A while."

His cousin came up the side of the cradle in two unhurried steps and rested his forearms on the platform rail. He had not put on the Spanish cloak yet. He was in a plain undertunic, Texcocan cut, with the small enameled cross at his throat that he never took off. In this light, before the cloak, he looked very nearly like his old self, and Itzcoatl had to look away for a moment before that registered as the trick it was.

"Sandoval wants the Texcocan engines on the western causeway by midday," Ixtlilxochitl said. "I told him you would walk Mirror across the camp at the third hour and stage by the brigantine yard. He asked me to confirm with you."

"Confirmed."

"Good."

The cousin did not leave. He stood at the rail and listened, also, for a while, to the sound across the water. Something passed across his face that Itzcoatl could not read because he had not yet learned to read his cousin's face after a winter of baptism.

"That's the Great Engine," Itzcoatl said.

"Yes."

"It's been half-awake all night."

"It will stay half-awake," Ixtlilxochitl said. "The pilot is dead. Smallpox, the courier said. The new pilot will not climb in while there is any other choice. They will wait until the western wall comes down, and then he will climb in, and then we will know."

The Spanish word for it was *engagement*. The Nahuatl word was older. Itzcoatl did not say either.

"I'll be ready at the third hour."

"I know."

Ixtlilxochitl rested a hand briefly on the cradle rail, the gesture of a man touching his horse's flank before turning away, and went back down the logs and along the line of tents toward the command tent, where Cortés was already up and Malintzin would already be at the small folding desk inside her walled annex with the morning's intercepts laid out. He did not look back. The discipline of the choice, again. Itzcoatl watched him go until the canvas swallowed him.

Then he reached up and pulled the hatch down.

Inside the cockpit the world contracted to the divination port, the four-hand-wide circle of polished obsidian that was the working face of Mirror's chest-disc seen from the inside, and the mirror-stack, three smaller obsidian discs angled around the seat that gave him the field at four removes during combat, plus the corner-mirror set behind his head that no other Tezcatlipoca pilot he knew had asked their smiths to install. He had asked for it the year his mother first fell ill. He had wanted to be able to see his own back. Older pilots had told him the request was unwise. They had been right; the corner-mirror showed him things he had spent the next twelve years declining to look at.

He looked at it now.

In the corner-mirror, behind his own seated shoulders, he saw the line of his back in a Texcocan undertunic with a Spanish leather belt cinched over it, the belt's small iron buckle gleaming where the pre-dawn light came down through the divination port and reflected once, twice, across the obsidian stack, and arrived at the corner-mirror with most of its color drained out. The iron buckle was the only foreign thing in the image. It was small. It was unambiguous. It had been issued to him, with the rest of his tack, on the morning after the pledge, a month ago in the great courtyard at Tlaxcala, witnessed by Cortés and Olid and Alvarado and the Tlaxcalan lords, with Malintzin two paces back of Cortés translating what did not need translating because everyone present understood the shape of the thing already.

He looked at the iron for some time.

Then he looked at the chest-disc straight ahead. The polished obsidian held his face. It held the cockpit lamp behind his face. It held, beyond that, in the way Tezcatlipoca's mirrors always held more than the room they were in, a gray distorted version of a face he had stopped recognizing on the field at Otumba and had not yet learned again.

Mirror's hum did not change. It had not changed when his mother died and would not change when he died. The hum was the hum of an engine, not a mourner. Tezcatlipoca passed through houses, not individuals. She had been working thirty years and would work thirty more if the bones in her chest were kept fresh. The deity at her core had noticed his drift on the field at Otumba and had stayed anyway. The catch in her hum that the older pilots described as the deity coughing in sleep was still there, very faint, and she had not departed.

He set his palms on his knees in the formal posture and bowed his head three degrees. He had been doing this every dawn for a month and had not told anyone. It was not a Spanish gesture and it was not, properly, a Texcocan one. It was something he had begun on his own. It said, to her, more or less: *I am still here, half a quarter-step off the note, and I am not going to lie to you about what we are doing.*

The Great Engine's distant hum lay over Mirror's like an unfinished chord that no living musician would be asked to resolve. He let it stand.

Mirror's hum sat under him at the pitch he had learned at fifteen.

He kept his head bowed until the third hour bell sounded from the Franciscan tent. Then he reached up and unsealed the hatch and went out into the cold light to walk her down the line to the brigantine yard, where Sandoval and his cousin would be waiting, and the day this campaign had been built around would begin.
