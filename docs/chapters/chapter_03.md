The fence opened at first light. Twenty of them walked across.

She counted because counting kept her hands from shaking. Eleven from her own compound. Six from the one beyond the salt-pan. Three she did not know by name, only by the look of women who had not slept. Citlal walked third in line. Citlal had said, at dusk, *if it is me, tell Rabbit it was me.* It was both of them. The boy was not among the twenty. Boys were not part of this trade. He would be kept by whoever was keeping the compound now, and he would learn a different word for the woman who had been his aunt.

Malintzin walked seventh. She was small for seventeen, the men measuring them at the gate had paused on her, then moved on. Small was useful. Small had kept her alive at the last handover and the one before. This was the fifth.

The Spanish camp smelled, first, of horses. She had not known horses smelled. The animals stood in a long picket-line tethered to a rope strung between two palms, and the smell of them was sweet and rank at once, sweat under leather, a kind of grass-piss that was nothing like dog or deer. Forty paces in, the smell changed. Gunpowder. She did not have a word for it yet, but she had a memory of it from yesterday's noise across the salt-flat, the noise that had done what spears could not. Then men. Men who had not bathed in a month, perhaps two. The smell was a fact. She did not flinch from it. Citlal, ahead of her, did not flinch either.

They were walked in a line down the center of the camp. To either side, men stopped what they were doing to look. Some looked the way men always looked. Some looked the way you look at a horse you have not yet decided whether to buy. One, older, gray at the temples, a wooden cross on a thong against his shirt, did not look at all. He turned his face away and kept walking.

At the edge of the camp she saw the demons.

That was the only word she had for them. They stood in cradles of cut timber, four of them, the smallest two stories tall and the tallest more than three. Iron, she thought first, because of the color where the sun had not yet touched them, a cold gray-blue at the joints, at the seams down the chest. Then she saw the gilding. Gold leaf laid across the breastplate of the largest. A face on the face, not a face, a mask, the face of a woman in a posture of weeping. The mask had been polished that morning. She could see the rag-marks.

Steam came from the cradles. Not smoke, steam, the kind that rises from a stone after rain. The demons were cooling. They had been hot from yesterday's work, and now, in the gray hour, they were giving the heat back to the air. A man with a leather apron and a brush was washing the foot of the largest one. The water in his bucket was the color of old blood. He worked without looking up.

Malintzin understood, without being told, that she was supposed to be afraid. She was. But fear was a room she had lived in for a long time, and there was furniture in it she knew how to sit on. She walked past the demons. She did not slow. She did not look twice. Looking twice was a thing the small did not do.

They were stopped in a cleared square at the back of the camp. A man came out of the largest tent. He was not tall. His hair was thin on top and he had not shaved in three days. He looked at the twenty of them the way a steward looks at a delivery of cloth, counting, assessing, pricing. He spoke. The words went past her ear. She caught one she had heard yesterday from a runner, *capitán*, and another that might have been a number. She filed them. She kept her face still.

A second man came forward. He was thin and burned by sun in the way of someone who had not been born under it. His robe was Spanish-cut but his sandals were Maya. He spoke, and he spoke in the coast tongue, slowly, like a man feeling for a step in the dark, but in it. He asked them, by the women's word for it, whether any of them had been a slave to a household that worshipped at a coastal shrine.

Three women raised their faces. The coast-tongue speakers by birth. He went to them first.

Malintzin kept her face down. Her father had taught her Nahuatl before he died. The compound where she had been a girl had spoken Nahuatl. The compound where she had been sold had spoken Mayan. She had picked up the coast tongue the way a cook picks up the taste of a new spice, by needing to. She had picked up four words of Spanish in the last two days, listening at the kitchen-hut wall: *agua*, *fuego*, *no*, *aquí*. She did not raise her face. She did not yet know what the man wanted, and she had learned, four handovers ago, that the first thing offered is the thing taken.

Then he turned to the rest of them and said it again in Mayan. He had a soft Mayan, mission-trained, the consonants too clean. *Has any of you,* he said, *come from the high country.*

It was the question she had been waiting for someone to ask, in some form, all her life.

She did not raise her face yet.

She looked instead at the demons.

The largest of them, the gilded one with the weeping mask, was not entirely cold. Where the steam thinned, she could see the woman-face again, and she saw, she was sure she saw, a single bright drop run from the corner of the mask's eye down along the cheek, and stop. Water. Or oil. Or the thing she did not have a word for. The man with the bucket saw it too. He reached up with a clean cloth and wiped it away without changing his expression, the way you wipe a baby's nose.

Behind her, footsteps. A different man. He did not stop in the line of officers. He came to the second demon, the one with the standing collar of feathers, no, not feathers, gilded iron worked to look like feathers, and he stopped in front of it, and he knelt.

He knelt on both knees. He bowed his head. His lips moved. He was perhaps her age, perhaps a little older, and he had taken his helmet off to do this, and his hair was light brown and damp at the neck.

The demon knelt with him.

Not all the way, it could not, in the cradle, but the great gilded shoulders inclined forward six inches, eight, and the head tilted as if listening, and the cradle-timbers groaned, and the man with the bucket stepped back and crossed himself.

The young man finished his prayer. He stood. He put his helmet back on. He did not look pleased or surprised or proud. He looked like a man who had said good morning to his horse.

He walked past the line of women on his way to the captain's tent. As he passed her, he glanced down. He saw her looking at the demon. He stopped.

He said, in slow careful Mayan with a bad accent, *what is your name.*

She looked up at him, finally, and gave him the name she was willing to give.

*Malintzin,* she said.

He repeated it once, getting the *tz* almost right, and went on.

Behind her, the thin sun-burned man in the Spanish robe had heard her say it. He turned. He had heard the shape of the name. He had heard the language of the name. He came toward her across the cleared square at a pace that was not quite running, and Malintzin understood, with the part of her that had been counting since dawn, that the fifth handover was over and the sixth had begun.

Twenty women, she counted them on the first morning and again on the second, in case the number had changed in the night. It had not. They walked behind the horses and ahead of the engines, twenty women given as part of the settlement after Centla, and Malintzin was the fourth from the left in the line.

The track to Cempoala was not a road. The Spaniards called it one because they had no other word. Sand, then crushed shell, then sand again, and a column of three hundred men plus four iron giants pressing it deeper with every step. By midday the dust came up to the women's knees. By evening it was in their teeth.

She had been given to a captain named Portocarrero. The giving had taken less than a minute. A priest spoke water over her head and a name onto her tongue, Marina, and then a hand on her elbow turned her toward the captain and the matter was finished. She had been a slave in a Mayan compound for about a year before the Spaniards came. She understood the shape of being given.

What the Spaniards did not yet understand was that she also spoke Nahuatl.

She had spoken it before she spoke anything else. Her father had taught her the words for sky and salt and the names of the months. That had been before, before the second husband, before the night journey, before the compound at Potonchán where she had learned Maya by the simple method of being beaten when she did not. She kept the Nahuatl in her mouth like a stone under the tongue. For a year nobody had asked her to spit it out.

On the third day of the march the Spaniards met a Mexica trade party on the track. The party had come down from the highlands to look at the strangers and decide whether the strangers were gods. They spoke Nahuatl. Aguilar, the Spaniard who had lived eight years among the Maya, stepped forward to speak with them and discovered that his Maya was useless. The trade party stood in the road, polite, uncomprehending, and the column halted around them in the dust.

Malintzin watched. She was not yet supposed to watch. A woman given to a captain was supposed to keep her eyes on the sand. She watched anyway. She watched Aguilar try three sentences in Maya and get three blank faces. She watched the Mexica leader's hand begin to settle on the obsidian club at his belt, not as a threat, only as a man's hand seeking a familiar weight while he decided whether to be afraid. She watched Cortés's face decide that the parley had failed and that the column would have to walk around them.

She stepped out of the line.

It was three steps. She took them quickly so that no one had time to forbid them. She said in Nahuatl to the Mexica leader: *They are asking whether you serve a great lord, and whether his city lies near.* She had guessed the question from the cadence of Cortés's voice and the angle of Aguilar's gestures. The guess was good enough.

The Mexica leader answered. She listened, turned her head, and said in Maya to Aguilar: *He says his lord is the speaker of Tenochtitlan and that the city lies twenty days inland by foot.*

Aguilar opened his mouth and shut it. Then he said it in Spanish to Cortés.

The column held its breath for the length of one such sentence, and then another, and then a third, and after the fourth Malintzin felt the captain Portocarrero's hand release her elbow as if she had become, in that minute, an object he was no longer entitled to hold so casually.

She did not look at him. She looked at Aguilar, because Aguilar was the other half of what she had just become.

She had become a link in a chain. Nahuatl in at one end, Spanish out at the other, and in the middle a woman who spoke Maya and a man who spoke Maya, passing the meaning between them like a clay cup that must not be dropped. She understood at once that the chain was fragile. Aguilar's Maya was a sailor's Maya, eight years old and learned among fishermen. Hers was a slave's Maya, a year old and learned under a stick. Between them, for some weeks, they would be the only road by which the Spanish lord could speak to the country he intended to take.

She also understood, standing in the dust at the head of the column with the Mexica leader still waiting for an answer, that she would not remain a link. A link was a thing that two stronger hands held between them. She did not intend to be held. She intended, by the time the column reached Cempoala or whatever city came after it, to have learned enough Spanish to cut Aguilar out and become the chain entire.

She did not say this. She translated the next sentence and the next, and when the parley ended and the trade party went on its way, she walked back to her place in the line of women without being told.

That night the priest came to her.

He was small, not young, a Franciscan in a brown habit too heavy for the coast, and he spoke Latin to her first, which she did not understand, and then Spanish, which she did not understand, and then he stopped and waited, which she did. He had not come to give her orders. He had come to look at her. She let him look. His name was Bernardo. She learned this later. That night he only nodded once, twice, and went away.

The other Spaniard came at dawn.

He was younger than she had expected, twenty-three, she would later be told, though she did not yet have the Spanish word for the number, and he walked with the particular stiffness of a man whose shoulders had been burning all the day before. He was the pilot of the largest of the four iron giants, the one whose plate was gilded yellow and whose chest carried a small glass box. He did not speak Nahuatl or Maya. He had, she gathered, asked Aguilar where the new translator-woman was, and Aguilar had pointed.

He bowed. She had not seen a Spaniard bow to a woman of the line before. He said his name, Andrés de Mora, and a city she could not place, Córdoba, and then a saint's name, Flora, and then he ran out of words she could be expected to know and simply stood there, polite, embarrassed, looking at the sand between her feet.

She looked at the engine behind him.

It was fifty feet of gilded plate, kneeling. Not standing, kneeling. The engines of the other three Spanish pilots stood at parade rest in the dawn light, iron feet planted, waiting for their men to finish breakfast. His was on one knee, the other knee folded, the great gilded head bent forward as if listening to something said into the sand. She had thought, when she first saw it the previous afternoon, that it had been damaged in the parley. Now she saw that it was intact and that the posture was a chosen one.

Andrés saw her looking. He said something quick in Spanish that she did not catch and then, more slowly, the Latin word *oratio*. He pressed his palms together in front of his chest. He pointed at the engine and then at himself and then at the sand under the engine's bent knee.

The engine was praying. Or, she corrected herself, because the distinction would matter, the man was praying, and the engine was kneeling because the man was. The gilding bent. The bones inside the small glass box, she suspected, did not.

She nodded once. She did not smile. She had learned, in a year of being a slave in a Mayan compound, that a woman who smiled at a polite man was understood to be agreeing to something she had not been asked about. She intended to be asked.

Andrés bowed again, the same small awkward bow, and walked back toward his kneeling engine. The engine, when he reached it, did not yet rise. He stepped into a cradle at its waist that took him a full minute to enter. Then the gilded knee lifted out of the sand and the great head came up and the giant stood, and the column began to move.

Malintzin walked. She counted the women again. Twenty. She counted the Spanish words she had learned in a day and a night: four. She counted the weeks she thought it would take her to learn enough to no longer need Aguilar: ten, perhaps twelve.

She walked north with the column, and she kept the count in her mouth, under the tongue, beside the stone of her first language.
