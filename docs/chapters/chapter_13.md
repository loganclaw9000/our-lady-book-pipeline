The cradle bay smelled of beeswax and oil. Andrés had been awake since the third hour of the night, kneeling on the boards beside La Niña de Córdoba's monstrance with a soft cloth and a phial of olive oil from the chapel stores. The phial had come from Medellín with a different expedition; the oil itself was sour. He used it anyway. There was no other.

Flora's voice in his sleep had grown clearer in the week since they had returned from Texcoco. Last night she had asked him, in Latin he had not taught her, what he expected the morning to bring. He had not answered. He had woken and gone to her cradle and begun the cleaning at the joint of the right gauntlet, where the gilding had thinned to the brass beneath after the third-day prayer on the road to Cempoala, the prayer the engine had answered by tilting six inches forward, the cradle-timbers groaning under fifty feet of standing weight.

He worked in candlelight. The other Reliquaries stood in their stalls along the eastern wall of the courtyard: *San Cristóbal del Viento* under a canvas, *San Sebastián de Cuba* unhooded but dark, *Santiago del Paso* at the head of the row in his minor-first-class livery. Across the open ground, behind a separate rope, *Nuestra Señora del Campeón* rested with her face plates closed. Cortés's Marian engine had wept at Cholula and again on the way down out of the mountains. She had not wept since they had entered the city. Andrés did not know whether that meant she was content or only that the tears had run out.

The runner came in through the south arcade at a pace that meant news. A boy from the coast, not the priest, not Sandoval's man, but a Totonac boy with a leather satchel and a red ribbon sewn to his cuff that meant Cortés and only Cortés. He passed through the chapel court without breaking stride. Andrés stood up. The cloth was still in his hand.

By the second hour after sunrise the council was in session in the long room that had been Axayacatl's audience hall. Andrés was not on the council. He stood with the other pilots in the antechamber and watched Malintzin pass through to translate, twice, before the doors closed. The third time the doors opened, Cortés came out alone.

Nine hundred men. Pánfilo de Narváez. The name moved through the antechamber like cold water. Velázquez had sent him from Cuba to arrest Cortés and dismantle the expedition. Narváez had landed at Veracruz with eighteen ships, sixty horses, and, the part that hollowed Andrés's chest, at least four Reliquaries, two of them rumored second-class, and a minor first-class on loan from the Bishop of Santiago de Cuba. More armored saints than the Spanish currently held standing in this palace.

Cortés was already moving. He spoke without stopping. Alvarado would hold Tenochtitlan with eighty men and three engines: *Santiago del Paso*, *San Cristóbal del Viento*, and, he named it without looking at Andrés, *La Niña de Córdoba*. The rest would ride to the coast at first light tomorrow.

Andrés said, *Captain.* Cortés stopped., I need her with me, Captain. She does not stand for another pilot.

Cortés looked at him for one count, two. Then: *She stands for Alvarado's defense, not his charge. He will not move her unless the palace is breached. You will ride with me on foot if you must.*, On foot., On foot. We need every armored thing we have, Mora. I am taking *Nuestra Señora*.

That was the line that stopped him. The Marian engine had not weathered the descent from the mountains well. Her gilding had cracked in three places at the shoulder, and Marian potency fell with the piety of the locals around her, and the locals around the coast road had not been catechized. Cortés was taking his own protection, his only protection, the engine that did not strike but shielded, out of the city and onto a hundred-and-twenty-league road to fight a force larger than his own. He was leaving Alvarado eighty men and Andrés's saint in a city of two hundred thousand who had not yet decided whether they were hosts or jailers.

Andrés went back to the cradle bay and finished the gauntlet joint. He did not speak to Flora. He did not pray aloud. He laid the cloth over her face plates the way a wife lays a sheet over a sleeping husband, walked out into the courtyard, and reported to the column at the southern gate before the light had fully cleared the volcanoes.

They marched out by the Iztapalapa causeway. Itzcoatl rode in the Texcocan train at the rear, his face the same face he had worn since Texcoco, which was the face of a man waiting. Malintzin rode forward near Cortés. *Nuestra Señora del Campeón* walked under her own pilot at the head of the column, fifty-five feet of cracked gilt and crystal, the small lead-sealed phial at her heart catching the early sun. Past the last chinampa Andrés looked back once at the Templo Mayor's twin shrines on their sixty-meter platform, and at the engine corps staging plaza beneath them where a dozen Mexica machines stood in shrine-stable, watching the Spanish leave with most of their strength.

By the second day, on the climb up out of the basin, he understood what he had not understood in the antechamber.

Cortés was going to gamble the expedition. Not hedge it. Not preserve it. He was going to ride three hundred kilometers with most of his armored strength, fight a force larger than his own, and leave the capital, the prize, the cage, the trap, to eighty men and a captain who was a knife in search of an excuse. If he won, he would come back with double the men and the engines. If he lost, there would be no expedition to come back to. There was no middle case. Cortés had not built one.

And Andrés, walking in the dust behind *Nuestra Señora*'s heels with his cuirass too heavy on his sunburned shoulders, found that he did not object. He found, when he probed the place inside him where objection should have been, that he had already given Cortés a kind of trust he had previously reserved for the saints. He had been giving it for months. He had not noticed the giving.

He thought: this is faith. He thought: this is the same shape. He thought: Flora.

He did not finish the thought. The column moved.

The march out of Tenochtitlan was long and slow, and the engines were not used to walk save when necessary. So for the days that it took the column to cross the lake and then the pass at Río Frío, descending toward Cholula and Tlaxcala, and on, Andrés rode.

The Tlaxcalan teōmecahuītlī walked with the column. Malintzin rode forward with the captains. But Andrés was in La Niña's cradle for the long marches, and when they reached rougher ground he walked her on her own legs. It used fuel. The adorators all winced whenever it happened.

The engines were spaced out along the road so as not to overload it. Each stride of the fifty feet of *La Niña de Córdoba* kicked up enough dust to blind a horse. The column of Reliquaries cast one moving shadow across the morning hills.

Andrés realized as he rode on, clear as a stone in his mouth, what Cortés was doing. He was going to gamble the expedition. The whole thing. And Cortés was not going to lose, because Cortés could not bear the thought of Velázquez's men taking his prize.

His faith in Cortés's judgment, which he realized now he had always possessed, felt in that moment like a piety. Like the faith he put in the bones of the child of Córdoba, whose relics were enshrined in the small glass box on his cuirass.

Andrés rode the rest of the day in silence. Bernardo, next to him on his mule, watched him watch the column and said nothing.

The rate of fuel consumption on long marches was a fact that would matter later, at Otumba, and on the return. The felt weight of the gamble was another fact. Andrés was busy, as always, re-cataloging his own beliefs in the most mechanical way possible. It was a slow process. He had not yet reached the end.

That night they camped on the western shoulder of the pass, two thousand feet above the lake. The air was thin and the fires burned blue at their edges. Andrés set La Niña down on her cradle-frame and climbed out with his shoulders aching from a day of held posture. Bernardo had a fire going by the time he reached the ground. The friar was eating a cold tortilla and a piece of dried fish and saying the *Magnificat* under his breath in the same rhythm he chewed.

Andrés sat. He took the offered tortilla. He ate., You are quiet, Bernardo said., I am thinking., I know.

Bernardo did not ask further. That was his particular cruelty as a friend: he would not collect the confession until the penitent offered it. Andrés had been on the road with him for nine months and had learned that silence from Bernardo was the silence of a man with a hand out, palm up.

He gave it. Quietly, looking at the fire and not at the friar., He is going to gamble all of us. To keep his command., Yes., You knew this., I have suspected since the second day. He is not a man who lets a prize be taken. Bernardo wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist. He weighs the value of the prize against the cost of the gamble. The cost of the gamble is us., And you ride out anyway., I am a friar, Andrés. I am not asked.

The fire cracked. Somewhere down the line a horse complained. Andrés thought about how to say what he wanted to say next and could not find the shape of it., I have been examining my faith in him, he said at last. I did not know I had it. I find it has the same shape as my faith in Flora.

Bernardo watched him for a long moment., That is not a small thing to find., No., What will you do with it., I do not know.

They were silent for a while. The Tlaxcalan teōmecahuītlī down the line was being stowed for the night by its handlers, and the deity-mask glowed faintly in the dark, a turquoise that was not quite turquoise, the color of a god in low rest. Sandoval's *Saint James of the Pass* stood twenty paces off, the apostle's finger riding in the monstrance at the throat. Sandoval was already asleep in his cradle. He always was. Andrés envied that more than he envied any other thing about the man., I will tell you a thing, Bernardo said, and his voice had gone careful. The Order taught you that the relic powers the engine. Yes?, Yes., It is not wrong. But it is not all of it. The relic is the lever. The pilot is the hand. What you put your weight against, that is a third thing. If a man puts his weight against a captain because the captain has promised him a prize, and he calls that piety, the engine still walks.

Andrés did not answer., The engine does not know the difference, Bernardo said. The pilot is the one who must.

Flora was a pressure at his sternum. Not a voice. A pressure and a cleanness, the way a held breath is clean. She had been quiet since Centla and quieter still in the gilded cage at Tenochtitlan, where there had been no killing to test her against. He wondered if she would recognize the gamble for what it was. He wondered if she had already.

He did not finish the thought., Eat, Bernardo said. We march at dawn.

He ate.

In the cradle that night he did not sleep well. The march had four hundred kilometers in it, three to four weeks if they pushed, and he had been counting strides since Tenochtitlan in the way an adorator counted, which was the way a rosary counted, one bead and then the next and then the next. Each stride of La Niña was thirty feet. The first day she had walked four miles on her own legs in the rough section and burned through a quarter of her sealed reservoir; the rest of the day she had ridden the cradle on the wagon team, and he had ridden a horse alongside her, watching her sway with the road. The reservoir would not refill until they reached Cempoala. The rate of consumption was a fact. He kept catching himself counting and stopping and starting again.

Cortés rode forward with the captains and the standards. From where Andrés lay he could see the moving silhouette of the line against the sky to the east, fifteen Reliquaries, three Tlaxcalan engines, four hundred men, the wagons, the horses. The largest column the expedition had ever fielded for one purpose, and the purpose was to arrest a Spaniard. He thought about Alvarado, left behind in the city with eighty men and three engines, and the eight thousand Mexica who could empty the plaza in a quarter hour if Alvarado mishandled them. He thought about the festival the priests had told them would come in two weeks. He thought about Moctezuma, who had not attacked when the column entered and would not attack now, because Moctezuma was a man who waited too long for reasons the Spanish would never understand.

It was a long thought. It had teeth in it. He set it down and picked up the simpler one again.

This is faith, he thought. This is the same shape. This is Flora.

And then, because he had said it to Bernardo aloud and now had to live with it, he made himself finish the thought.

If the shape was the same, then either the faith in Cortés was holy, or the faith in Flora was not. There was no third reading. He could not yet say which way the equality cut. He suspected he would not be allowed to choose; he suspected the road itself would choose for him, somewhere between here and the coast, or on the way back, or in the city after, in the dark, with his hands on the cradle controls and a decision he had not rehearsed in front of him.

He closed his eyes. Flora's pressure stayed at his sternum, neither rising nor falling, the held breath of a child who had learned at thirteen to hold her breath and then to give it up cleanly. He listened for her until he slept.

Bernardo was already up when he woke. The fire was cold. The column was forming. Andrés climbed into the cradle, ran the morning consecration in the abbreviated field form, and gave the engine her standing prayer.

La Niña rose. Her shoulders inclined six inches, the way they always did at the close of the prayer, as if she were listening for something that had not yet been said.

The column moved.
