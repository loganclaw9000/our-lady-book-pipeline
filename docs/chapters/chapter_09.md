The causeway was a causeway. Not a miracle, not a bridge from the gods. A road built of stone and will and the backs of ten thousand men, stretching north from Iztapalapa through the mirror of the lake toward the twin cities. Lake Texcoco at mid-morning was flat and glassy, the sky above it the pale blue of new cotton, and on the far shore Tenochtitlan rose the way it had always risen, temples, causeways, canals, chinampas, the geometry that taught a child what a city was.

I stood at the edge of it, behind my engine, with forty other engines flanking the causeway in drill-rank, and watched the strangers file in.

*Obsidian Mirror* was behind me, fifty-five feet of obsidian-gilt and matte-black plating, the smoking-mirror chest-disc polished to a dull sheen. My hands were on the yoke-straps. I felt the engine's hum through the leather, through my palms, through the bones of my arms. It had been waiting. It always waited. Tezcatlipoca engines waited.

The Spanish column came up the causeway in file. I counted them as they passed, because that is what drill-rank teaches. Twelve small engines first, the third-class things that looked to me like crude dolls dressed in iron. Then the four second-class Reliquaries our scouts had named: *La Niña de Córdoba*, *San Esteban de la Cruz*, *San Sebastián*, and behind them the one our Totonac informants had warned about most, the captain's own. Saint-names in a language I had never spoken. Their fuel was a rumor. Our spies had never gotten close enough to the Spanish shrines to see.

Then the first-class.

It was *Santiago del Paso*, nearly as tall as my own engine, and it walked with a deliberate, measured step that tightened my stomach in a way I could not name. The chest-plate was gilded, and the gilding caught the morning light and flung it back in a glare that made me look down at the stone. Its sword hung at the hip. Its pilot's voice carried across the water when he called an order back to the Tlaxcalans behind.

And the Tlaxcalans.

They came behind the Spanish in their own engines. *Teōmecahuītlī*. Kin. I knew the shape of them, the way they walked, the way their pilot-voices carried. I had trained beside Tlaxcalan pilots once, a decade ago, before the last cycle of Flower Wars, before my cousin's cousin had been chosen for the Texcocan succession and the family had cracked along a fault line that would never close. The Tlaxcalan engines were our kind of engine, built the way our kind were built, fueled the way our kind were fueled. And now they walked behind the strangers.

The taste of the betrayal was copper at the back of my tongue. I knew that taste. I had tasted it at Centla when the Maya engines turned, and I had tasted it again at Cholula when the dormant Quetzalcoatl shrine had not so much as stirred while the strangers killed the noble youth in the plaza. Now it was in my mouth a third time, and a third time meant a pattern.

The column was halfway up the causeway. I could see the Tlaxcalan pilots in their cockpits, faces pressed to the viewing-glass. I could see the Spanish pilots too, the way they stood at ease behind their Reliquaries, hands resting on swords. Their internal mechanics were unknown to me. Their liturgy was unknown to me. Their saint-relic theology was rumor, a thing the priests at the *calmecac* had argued and never pinned down. They had engines, they had power, they had a tradition we did not understand.

We had forty engines.

I felt the Great Engine of the Templo Mayor through the water, through the causeway, through the stone beneath my feet. Its hum was a low thing, a cathedral-hum, and as the Spanish column passed beneath the lake's mirror the hum shifted register half a step. The Great Engine was awake. It had been awake for hours, perhaps for days. A hundred and fifty feet of Huitzilopochtli-caste, first-class, the largest engine the Mexica had ever built, standing in the center of Tenochtitlan waiting for an order that was not going to come.

I knew the mathematics. I had run the mathematics a hundred times in my head between Iztapalapa and here. Forty engines to twelve, plus four second-class, plus one first-class. Fifty to one in our favor on bodies, ten to one in tonnage, and that was before the Great Engine moved. A single charge of the Mexica line could finish the expedition inside ten minutes. I knew the kill-rates. I knew the closing speeds. I knew how *Obsidian Mirror* could stalk and blur and strike a Spanish engine's cockpit from the blind side; how a Huitzilopochtli engine could charge and cleave through second-class gilding the way an obsidian blade goes through agave; how the Great Engine could walk the causeway and level the column in a single sweep, the way a man brushes ants from a stone.

The mathematics were clean. The mathematics were right.

And Moctezuma would not give the order.

I felt that too. The tlatoani's choice was in the air, in the water, in the half-step shift of the Great Engine's hum. He had chosen to let them in. The reason was hidden from me, and the hiddenness of the reason was, in some private way, more frightening than the strangers themselves.

The column reached the end of the causeway. The Tlaxcalans halted. The Spanish halted. The Mexica engines stood at attention, my hands still on the yoke-straps, my breath shallow in my throat, and the strangers filed past us into the sacred precinct. *Santiago del Paso* passed within a stone's throw of *Obsidian Mirror*. Its pilot turned his head, a small motion, polite, almost curious, and looked at me. I looked back. He nodded once. I did not return the nod, because no one had told me whether to, and because the muscles of my neck had locked.

Then he was past, and the column moved on, and the city swallowed them.

Behind me *Obsidian Mirror* hummed at the pitch it always hummed, but the hum had become a question. Not the old question, the one a Tezcatlipoca engine asks its pilot before a fight. A new one. A question for which our liturgy had no answer-phrase.

I stood in drill-rank and watched them go.

The Tenochtitlan on the far shore was the Tenochtitlan I had grown up looking at from Texcoco. The same temples. The same causeways. The same chinampas. And it was already a different city, the change had happened in the half-step shift of the Great Engine's hum, in the moment Moctezuma chose silence over the order he was owed, in the moment a stranger's first-class crossed the threshold of the precinct and was not struck down.

The mathematics were right, and the mathematics had not been followed, and the gap between those two facts was the shape of what was coming. I was, so far as I could tell, the only pilot in the formation who had let himself look at the gap.

I held the yoke-straps. *Obsidian Mirror* hummed its new question. The column disappeared between the temple walls.

I stood in drill-rank, and I began to count what we had just spent.

The litter was already in place when I reached the midpoint of the causeway. Moctezuma's sedan, four-poled, lacquered, the goldwork catching the sun the way it always caught the sun, the bearers in full dress, the litter canopy open. He stood inside, and I could see him from the causeway's edge: the crown, the cloak, the face. His face was tired. I have known the tlatoani since I was a boy, and his fatigue was a new thing on him, a thing that did not sit well with the regalia. He had not slept. I knew he had not slept.

I took my place at the rear of the sedan's honor guard, two paces behind the litter, *Obsidian Mirror* directly behind me. The engine's hum was a low thing, a steady thing, the hum of a god waiting for an order that might not come. My hands were on the yoke-straps, the leather warm from the morning's walk.

The Spanish came up the causeway.

Cortés led them. He rode a horse, a thing I had seen before at Centla, a thing I still did not trust, a thing that made the ground vibrate in a way I could not name. His armor was steel, the steel was dented, the dents from Centla, from the road, from Cholula, I did not know which. He carried a sword. He carried a banner I could not read. His face was the face of a man who had made a calculation and was walking into the result.

Behind him the engines. I counted them as they passed, the way drill-rank teaches. Twelve third-class, crude iron dolls, four second-class Reliquaries, one first-class, *Santiago del Paso*. Behind the engines, the men. Spanish, Tlaxcalan, Totonac. The Tlaxcalans were the problem, the problem I had tasted on the road, the problem that sat in my mouth like copper.

And then the girl.

She walked beside Cortés's horse, on foot, in a Spanish dress that had been altered for her, the hem shortened for the causeway, the sleeves cut narrow. She was not a warrior. She was not an engine-pilot. She was not, by any Mexica measure, anyone. And yet she walked beside the captain. And yet she carried herself the way a person carries herself when she knows, without being told, that she is the hinge on which the meeting turns.

And I knew her.

I knew her by the way she held her head. I knew her by the way she looked at Moctezuma's sedan, not with the fear of a slave, not with the curiosity of a stranger, but with the flat, appraising look of a person who has been taught, carefully, what a tlatoani is, and what a tlatoani is not.

She was the girl Xochitl was teaching.

I had heard my sister mention her, once, in the garden, weeks ago. A strange Nahua girl, given to the strangers, who spoke three tongues, who learned court protocol the way a fish learns to swim. I had not met her. I had not thought of her again. And now she was here, on the causeway, at the midpoint of the city's throat, and she was the hinge.

I filed the fact away. I did not know why I filed it away. I knew, only, that it would be useful.

The Spanish column halted. The Tlaxcalan engines halted. The Mexica engines stood at attention, my hands on the yoke-straps, my breath shallow, the hum of *Obsidian Mirror* a question I did not know how to answer.

Moctezuma stepped down from the sedan.

He walked forward, the bearers following, the four poles of the litter trailing behind him, and he met Cortés on the causeway. They stood ten paces apart. The girl walked forward to stand between them.

She spoke.

She spoke Nahuatl to Moctezuma, and the words were the words of court protocol, the words of welcome, the words of a tlatoani receiving a guest. She spoke Spanish to Cortés, and the words were the words of the captain's greeting, the words of a man presenting himself to a ruler. She spoke again, Nahuatl, Spanish, Nahuatl, Spanish, and between her and the man beside her, the priest Aguilar, the chain of translation held. I could hear the Nahuatl clearly, I could hear the Spanish in fragments, I could hear the way the girl's face changed with each language, the way her eyes held Moctezuma's and held Cortés's, the way she was, in that moment, the only person on the causeway who understood both sides.

I knew the way a pilot understands an engine. I knew the way a translator understands a word. The girl understood both.

Moctezuma spoke. He spoke in Nahuatl, the voice tired, the words formal. The girl translated. Aguilar translated. Cortés answered in Spanish, the words lawyerly, ceremonial, the words of a man who had read the *Requerimiento* and had chosen, for this moment, to set it aside. The girl translated.

They spoke for a long time. They spoke about nothing and everything. They spoke about the journey, about the gifts, about the road, about the city. They did not speak about Cholula. I noticed the silence. I noticed the way Moctezuma's eyes did not drift to the south when the girl's voice faltered on the word *Cholula*. I noticed the way Cortés's face remained, for a moment, a fraction of a moment, too still.

The exchange was formal. The exchange was polite. The exchange was, in the way such exchanges always are, a mask for a decision that had already been made.

Moctezuma had decided. He had decided to let them in.

I felt the decision in the air, in the water, in the half-step shift of the Great Engine's hum behind us. I felt it in the way the girl's eyes met Moctezuma's and held, and in the way Moctezuma's eyes met hers and held back.

I stood at the rear of the honor guard. I held the yoke-straps. *Obsidian Mirror* hummed its new question. The Spanish engines hummed their old ones, the saint-frequencies, the frequencies of the Reconquista, the frequencies our pilots had never heard, the frequencies that interleaved with the god-frequencies of the Mexica in a soundscape neither tradition had ever heard before.

The soundscape was wrong.

The soundscape was, in the way that a soundscape can be, a symptom.

I stood in drill-rank and listened to it.

The girl finished the last translation. She stepped back. Moctezuma nodded. Cortés nodded. The girl looked up at me, for a moment, and I looked back, and she knew, without being told, that I knew her. She did not smile. She did not frown. She nodded once, and the nod was not a greeting, it was an acknowledgment, the acknowledgment of one person who understood the hinge to another.

The litter was raised. Moctezuma stepped inside. The bearers lifted the poles. The column moved on, the Spanish, the Tlaxcalans, the engines, the girl, the captain, the priests, the men, the horses, the engines, the engines, the engines.

*Obsidian Mirror* hummed. The Great Engine hummed. The Reliquaries hummed.

I stood in drill-rank and watched them go.

The city swallowed them. The causeway was empty behind. The lake was mirror-flat. The sky was pale blue.

I began to count what we had just spent.

The city took them. It always took them, the way a body takes a meal, the way a stomach knows a thing is coming and prepares for it in secret. The causeway emptied behind the column. The lake went flat again. The mirror restored.

*Obsidian Mirror* hummed. The hum was different. I could not have said how, not with words, only with the part of me that knew the engine the way a pilot knows a god. The hum was a question.

I had no answer to give it.

---

The palace of Axayacatl was not my palace. It was not any Texcocan's palace. It was the Mexica palace, the Mexica court, the Mexica center of power, and I had never been inside it before.

The honor guard filed in behind the sedan. We stood at attention in the courtyard, engines flanking us, guards in lacquered armor, priests in black. The courtyard was stone. A pool. An old avocado at the center, branches heavy with fruit. The tree was the only thing in the courtyard that had no purpose, and that was perhaps why my eye kept returning to it.

Moctezuma stepped down from the sedan. The bearers set the poles aside. The captain, Cortés, I made myself name him, stepped down from the horse. The girl, Malintzin, moved forward and spoke, and her two languages opened in her mouth like halves of a folded cloth. The exchange was formal. The exchange was polite. The exchange was, in the way such exchanges always are, a mask laid over a decision already made.

I stood at attention. My hands were at my sides. My breath was shallow. The engine was behind me, eight tons of obsidian and lacquered cedar, and the engine hummed.

The courtyard was, in that long minute, only a courtyard.

Then we were dismissed, honor guard, engines, priests, guards, and Moctezuma and Cortés and Malintzin and the captains went into the palace, and the palace took them as the city had taken the column. The doors did not close so much as fold around them.

I held attention for one more breath. Then I turned and walked toward the engine cradle.

---

The cradle was a long stone shed open to the south, with cedar beams blackened by twenty generations of incense. It had its own courtyard. Its own pool. Its own tree, a smaller avocado, scarred where pilots over the years had cut staves from a low branch. Forty engines stood in their stalls in two facing rows, painted motifs vivid as the day they were laid down. Quetzal feathers. Obsidian discs. Hummingbird heraldry on the Huitzilopochtli ranks at the far end. The smell was copal and old grease and the particular dry metallic taste of consecrated bone-stores.

The teōmachtiani on duty did not look up as I passed. None of them spoke to a Texcocan in the corridors of the cradle if they could help it. That had not changed today. That, at least, had not changed.

I climbed the ladder of *Obsidian Mirror*. Forty rungs. I stepped through the chest-hatch and into the cockpit and pulled the hatch shut behind me, and for the first time in nine days I was alone with my engine in a room that was not the open road.

The cockpit was dark. The cockpit was always dark. A single seam of light came in through the eye-slits, and a second through the throat-vent above me, and the smoking-mirror disc set in the chest-plate caught both and threw them back at the pilot's seat.

I sat. I put my hands on the yoke-straps. I did not yet wake the deity. There was a thing to do first, and the thing was to look.

I looked at myself in the mirror.

The face was my face. The face was not my face. It was a face I had worn for a lifetime and had not known, until that moment, that I wore it. A Texcocan face, the long jaw of my mother's line, the small scar at the temple from a childhood fall, the hair pulled back in the warrior's knot the calmecac had taught me when I was eleven. A son of the Triple Alliance. An honor guard at the meeting that had not become a battle.

The mirror was honest in the way Tezcatlipoca was honest, which is to say, it showed what the viewer had not yet permitted himself to see.

I closed my eyes. I breathed. I opened them. I made myself say it inside the cockpit, where only the deity could overhear.

The mathematics were right. The mathematics had not been followed.

Forty Mexica engines in formation on the causeway. Thirteen Spanish, by my count, battered, salt-pitted, three of them limping. Their heaviest was a second-class Reliquary half the size of a Huitzilopochtli line unit. We were not outmatched. We were not even matched. We were arranged around them the way a hand is arranged around a moth.

In ten minutes, twelve at the outside, I could have walked *Obsidian Mirror* through their column and folded it. I had run the geometry behind my eye-slit all the way down the causeway, the way a teōmachtiani is taught to run geometry, distances, scales, the throat-paths to each Spanish heart-chamber. I had the geometry. The pilot two stalls down had the geometry. Every teōmachtiani on the causeway had the geometry. We had been trained from boys to have the geometry.

And Moctezuma, who had also been trained, who had piloted, before he had been tlatoani, who knew exactly what a Huitzilopochtli engine could do to a Reliquary the size of a market stall, had given the order to lower the standards and walk the strangers in.

There was an answer for that. The answer the priests would give. The answer the court would give. The answer my cousin Ixtlilxochitl, off in his exile in the north, would give in a different language and call by a different name. Each answer a different shape over the same gap.

I did not believe any of them. Not yet. Not in the cockpit, with the mirror in my face.

The engine hummed under my palms. The hum sharpened, that was the only word, and I understood that the deity had been listening to my silence and had decided to speak first. Tezcatlipoca, the smoking mirror, the god whose business it was to show people what they would rather not see. Of course he had a vote. Of course the question I had carried up the ladder was one he had been waiting to ask.

*What did you see, teōmachtiani?*

I let my hands stay loose on the yoke-straps. I did not pretend not to have heard.

I saw a tlatoani who could have ended a war in twelve minutes and chose to host a feast instead. I saw forty engines in drill-rank with full bone-stores and cold motifs. I saw the strangers walk through a hand that did not close. I saw, somewhere behind Moctezuma's eye, a calculation I did not have the language to read, not fear, not exactly, and not piety, and not cunning, but some braided thing of all three that was its own grammar.

I saw the gap.

The hum eased. Not satisfaction. Acknowledgment. The deity does not reward; the deity records.

I sat with my hands on the straps and let the cockpit be dark around me and let the mirror be a mirror. The strangers were in the palace. The strangers were in the city. The strangers were in the world, now, in a way they had not been before, not as a rumor on the coast, not as a column on a road, but as a fact that breathed under the same roof as the tlatoani.

And I, so far as I could tell, was the only pilot in the formation who had let himself walk up his own ladder and look at the gap with the lights on.

That was not a comforting thought. It was not meant to be. Tezcatlipoca was not a comforting god.

I unclipped the yoke-straps. I let my hands fall.

The decision, when it came, if it came, would have to be mine, because the men I would have asked to make it for me had already, by their silence on the causeway, declined the office.

I climbed back down the forty rungs. The teōmachtiani on duty did not look up. The avocado in the cradle courtyard had dropped a fruit in my absence; it lay split on the flagstones, pale flesh open to the air. I stepped around it.

The hum followed me out of the shed and faded only at the gate.
