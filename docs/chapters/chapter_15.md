Through the soles of his sandals Itzcoatl could feel the engine working, a low tremor passing up the pyramid stone into the bones of his feet, into his shins, into the standing weight of him. Steady. Fed.

He was on the lower steps in feast-day cotton, unarmed, unarmored, the white linen still smelling faintly of the cedar chest his mother's house kept it in. Obsidian Mirror sat a quarter mile away, cradled in its shrine in the engine compound, attended only by its priests. He was forbidden to approach it while Toxcatl ran. The rule was old. The rule was right. The rule felt wrong today.

Four ixiptla moved among the dancers. Young men, nineteen or twenty, each chosen a year ago to live as Huitzilopochtli for a single year, wives, servants, the god's voice in the courtyard, the god's gifts in his hands. Today they would die. The youngest passed close, a boy with a flower-crown braided in, calm, hollow-eyed. Itzcoatl had received gifts from him at court. The boy did not look at him.

The drum beat. The singers held a long high note. Beneath it the engine ran on, the bass under the city, the thing the people standing on stone always heard without hearing.

He counted Spanish at the perimeter. Thirty, maybe more. Thirty-five. Thirty-eight. They wore steel. They held their swords in scabbards. They did not dance and they did not speak and they had not been invited in this number. Moctezuma had approved a witness contingent of perhaps a dozen, a courtesy to the guests, the way one allows a foreigner to sit at the edge of a wedding feast. There were not a dozen here. Itzcoatl filed the count and did not move.

He was on his feast day. He was unarmed. He was a quarter mile from his engine.

The thing under everything held its baseline pitch. He had heard it change once, years ago, when a Flower War went badly and the pipeline ran thin for a week. The bass had dropped a semitone. The priests had noticed. The tlatoani had noticed. The city had felt it, even the people who did not know the sound was a sound. A bad omen, that week. Today the register was strong. The pyramid drank. The teōcalli-chest at the summit was full of the bones of warriors who had ascended the steps willingly, sun-fed, and the engine breathed them out as the city's continuous note.

The boy with the flower-crown turned in the ring. His eyes were not empty in the way Spanish eyes called empty. They were the eyes of a man who has finished the year of his life and is now waiting for the last hour of it. He had finished. He was waiting.

A Spanish captain at the courtyard's southern edge raised his hand and did not lower it. Itzcoatl read the gesture. So did the soldiers. They shifted half a pace forward. One of them, a boy himself, beard barely starting, drew his sword an inch and let the steel show. Itzcoatl saw the inch. Filed the inch. Did not move.

The singers' note climbed. The drum answered. The pyramid's tremor under his sandals did not change, and that was the wrongness of it: the city was about to be opened and the engine did not know yet, because its feeding had not yet been interrupted. The pipeline was continuous. It would not be continuous in a few minutes. He could feel the not-yet of it like a held breath in another man's chest.

The four ixiptla turned together. They had practiced this. They had been practiced for a year. They were ready.

Itzcoatl was not.

He thought, briefly, of the run to the engine compound. Quarter mile across packed ceremonial ground, then the perimeter wall, then the shrine doors, then consecration, cold meltwater drawn from the Texcoco hills, the bath he had not taken this morning because it was his feast day and he was forbidden it. Then fasting he had not done. Then the teoxicalli, half an hour minimum if he rushed it and broke the form, longer if he did not. Thirty minutes from a cold start to standing, and he did not have a cold start; he had a feast and a meal in him and a body warm with the sun. Forty-five. An hour.

The Spanish would be done in less than that.

He understood this the way he had understood the dropped semitone years ago: by the bones, before the priests said it.

The captain's hand fell.

The southern line moved as one. Steel cleared scabbards in a single dry sound, like a flock of birds lifting from a reed-bed at once. The dancers nearest the line saw it before the singers did. The singers kept singing for another beat, they had been trained to keep singing, and then a man in the inner ring made a sound that was not part of any song Itzcoatl had ever heard, and the festival ended.

The boy with the flower-crown was the third to fall. Itzcoatl saw the cut. It was not clean. Spanish steel against unarmored cotton was never clean.

Then the bass under his sandals moved.

Not dropped. Not yet. It tightened, a shift in tension he felt through his shins as a small, terrible sharpening, the engine registering its first severed feed: the warriors who would have ascended at the climax of Toxcatl, ascending instead under foreign blades, in the wrong rite, with the wrong intent, into a pipeline that could not accept them.

He stood on the steps in his white cotton with his hands open at his sides and listened to the change. Around him the courtyard came apart. Inside him, very quietly, a calculation finished: by tonight he would be in the city's outer rings raising the calpulli. By morning he would be at the engine compound bathing in cold water. By tomorrow midday Obsidian Mirror would walk.

A quarter mile was a long way from a feast day, and not far at all from a war.

He went down the steps.

The drum changed.

A single ocelot-hide strike fell outside the rhythm, cut the singers' syllable in mid-sustain, and went still. The drummers were professionals. They did not break rhythm. The beat had come from outside the drum.

Itzcoatl turned his head one degree eastward, just enough to see, and saw the bearded sergeant lift a hand and drop it, and saw the six men at the gate behind him step left to block the exit, and saw the twelve at the eastern arch step right, and saw, in the same instant, the Spanish at the western parapet begin to descend.

The first blow was not a Spanish sword. It was a stone. Itzcoatl saw the throw before he saw the thrower, a Mexica nobleman three paces inside the eastern arch, an arm already coming forward, a stone leaving the hand as the Spanish blade reached the shoulder. The man fell with his arm still extended. The stone struck a helmet and bounced. He was the first of the thousands.

The Spanish swords were out in the same second.

The courtyard was not a place for sword-fighting. It was a place for dancing, for feasting, for the slow procession of the ixiptla, for the ritual exchange of gifts between nobles and priests. It was open stone, flat, two hundred paces across in every direction, and it held five hundred nobles and three hundred priests and two hundred dancers and one hundred singers and four ixiptla and three thousand onlookers, and the Spanish were at the perimeter, and the Spanish had swords, and the Mexica had none.

The first ten seconds were a slaughter.

The outer ring of dancers broke. The inner ring did not. The singers held their syllable for three more beats and then fell silent. The four ixiptla stood where they were and looked at the nearest Spaniard and did not move. Cuauhtli of Cuauhtitlán was cut first. The sword came up under his arm and into his belly and out his back, and he fell forward onto his knees and stayed there for one breath and then went down, and the flower-crown rolled away from his head and came to rest three paces from Itzcoatl's sandals.

The second ixiptla, Tochtli, did not fall. The sword that would have killed him was stopped by the arm of a nobleman who stepped in front of him, and the nobleman died, and Tochtli looked at the dead nobleman and did not move, and then the second sword came, and he fell, and his flower-crown rolled, and it struck Itzcoatl's ankle and went on.

Mazatl was killed standing. Tepetl was killed kneeling, his flower-crown still on his head, the sword going into his throat from behind, and the blood ran down his chest and stained the dahlia petals red.

The four were down in twelve seconds.

The killing continued.

Itzcoatl saw the outer ring of dancers collapse inward in three concentric failures. He saw the inner ring break at the point where the first Spanish sword had struck, and the dancers there turned and ran, and were cut down running, and the survivors turned and ran the other way, and were cut down running that way, and the survivors who could not run fell to their knees and put their hands up and were cut down kneeling. He saw a priest from the calmecac step in front of a child and take a sword into his chest and fall, and the child ran, and the child was cut down five paces later. He saw a noblewoman throw a jar of pulque at a Spaniard and miss and be cut down where she stood. He saw a jaguar-warrior step into the path of a Spanish blade meant for a younger man at his shoulder and go down, and the younger man survived, and the warrior went down and did not rise. Itzcoatl did not see his face. He did not know him. He looked away.

He counted to forty. The killing had begun forty seconds before. He had counted to forty and the killing had not stopped. He counted to eighty. The killing had not stopped. He counted to one hundred. The killing had not stopped.

He did not count further. He had counted enough.

The drum was silent. The singers were silent. The hum of the Great Engine was the only sound that remained, and the hum was changing.

It began as a shift in pitch, a slight upward bend that should not have been possible, and then it broke, and the bass note that had been the city's foundation since before Itzcoatl was born went jagged, then thin, then dropped a full interval and stayed there, and the change was not subtle, and every pilot in Tenochtitlan, every priest, every adorator, every citizen with an ear trained to hear the engines, felt it at the same instant, like the stone of the city shifting an inch underfoot, and Itzcoatl felt it in his teeth and in the small bones behind his ears and in his sternum, and he knew what had happened.

The pipeline had been severed.

The ixiptla, the Flower War captives held for the next sacrifice cycle, the priests at the bone-dust furnace, all of them were dead or dying within fifty paces of where he stood, and the fuel was cut, and the hum would not return to baseline in Itzcoatl's lifetime.

He was unarmed. He was three steps up from the pavement, where the lesser nobility had been placed an hour ago. The men who had been beside him had gone down in the first ten seconds. He did not look at their bodies. He looked across the precinct toward the southwestern corner, where the engine compound stood. He could not see Mirror from where he was, the muster wall hid the stables, but he knew where Mirror was, and he knew that Mirror's two adorator-priests would not know what had happened until the hum told them. The hum had told them.

He stepped down from the third step to the second.

A Spaniard fifteen paces to his right cut down a man who had been singing an hour ago. The Spaniard turned, looked at the steps, did not see Itzcoatl, or saw him and did not classify him, and moved on toward a closer target. Itzcoatl held still until the Spaniard's eyes had passed. Then he stepped down to the first.

He stood on the first step for a count of three. The exits were held. The southwestern corner held the engine compound. Its gate did not face the courtyard.

He stepped onto the pavement.

He did not run. A fleeing man on this stone would be cut down inside ten paces. He walked. He walked along the base of the pyramid, with the south wall of the Great Engine at his right shoulder and the killing at his back, and he did not look at the bodies, and he did not look at the flower-crowns scattered across the stone, and he kept his pace at the pace of a man with business to attend, and the business was not yet decided.

The hum was wrong at his right shoulder. The hum was the sound of the Great Engine wounded. The hum was the sound of the pipeline severed. The hum was the sound that would be the city's voice for the rest of his life.

He reached the corner of the southwestern wall. The engine compound's gate was twenty paces beyond it. He could not yet see the gate from where he stood.

He did not yet know what he would do when he could.

He reached the stable door with the last ten paces still in his legs and the breath of running thick in his chest. The cadence of feast-day movement that had carried him through the morning, one foot, one breath, the slow processional discipline a pilli-class pilot owed Toxcatl, had broken somewhere on the lower terrace and not yet rebuilt itself. The engine compound at this hour should have been empty. The corps was in the courtyard for the festival, in feast paint, in unarmored cloth. The two adorator-priests at *Obsidian Mirror's* stable were the only living men in the compound, and they were standing across the door.

He had already known they would be. He had known it climbing down from the lesser-nobility step after the first sword fell, known it crossing the precinct floor against the panic, known it passing under the Coatepantli where a child screamed somewhere outside the wall in the broken-pitch sound of stone-impact, not blade. He had thought, without willing the thought, of his nephew Tepin, eight years old, who had been promised a view of the dancers from a Tlatelolco rooftop, and he had not been able to see the boy from anywhere along the route, and he had kept moving because there was nowhere else along the route to be.

The older adorator's posture said it before the older adorator did: *do not come.*

Itzcoatl stopped three paces short of the door.

Behind the adorators, in the dim of the shrine-stable, the high curved spine of *Obsidian Mirror* held the late sun on the smoking-mirror motif at the chest and gave nothing back. The deity slept. The cables were cold. The teōcalli-chest was sealed with the feast-day seals he had helped place at dawn, his own hands on three of them, and the breaking of those seals was not a thing a pilot did alone, not on Toxcatl, not while the city was still inside the festival hours by the calendar he had been raised on. Toxcatl forbade the pilot's approach. He had known this when he dressed at dawn. He had known it standing in line for the morning rite. He had known it descending the lesser-nobility step into the killing.

His hand had still moved a half-pace toward the door before the knowledge caught up to it. Then it stopped. Then it fell back to his side. He was not entirely sure who in his body had made the decision and who in his body had been overruled. He was sure only that the decision had been made.

The older adorator nodded once, very small, and did not move from the door.

Behind him the Great Engine's wounded note went on. He felt the second drop of it in his teeth. It was not a sound any longer. It was a structural fact about the city, the way a snapped rafter is a structural fact about a roof. The pyramid behind him went on, blind, half-fueled, mid-sentence in a liturgy no one was now alive to finish. He did not turn to look at it. He had been looking at it long enough.

The younger adorator, a man not five years older than Itzcoatl himself, was crying without sound. The tears went down through his ash-mark in clean lines. He did not move from the door either. The adorators of a sleeping engine had one office on a feast day and they were performing it. Itzcoatl recognized the discipline because it was his own discipline, run on a different organ.

A horn went up from the southern gate. Not the awakening horn. The recall: pilots not in deployment to fall back to the corps muster point. It came again four breaths later. Itzcoatl heard it as an order and as a permission. The recall released him from the stable. It released him from the question the stable had been about to ask.

He bent his head briefly toward the older adorator, the half-bow a pilot owed the men who kept his deity's body, and turned away from the door.

He did not run. He went the long way around the smaller stables, because the direct line through the compound would have taken him past the Huitzilopochtli stalls where two solar-caste pilots in Toxcatl whites were sitting on the packed earth with their heads in their hands, and he could not afford to look closely at either of them, because his caste was not their caste and his grief was not their grief and the day would not survive his beginning to mistake the two.

Past the compound's eastern arch, the lower terrace was a different terrace from the one he had crossed. Bodies in feast colors lay where they had run out of running. He did not count. The count had ended earlier in the day in someone else's body, and he was not going to start it again here. He saw a jaguar pelt across a man's shoulders, full uniform, unarmored because it was Toxcatl, and he did not let his eyes finish the figure. He did not yet know that he was passing within forty paces of Cuauhtli Ocelotl, his sister's husband, who had stepped between a Spanish sword and his younger cousin and gone down with both hands open. That knowledge would arrive by the next morning, by way of a Tlatelolco runner with a child's scar to describe and a household to account for. For now Itzcoatl kept his eyes at the height of the rooftops and walked.

From inside Axayacatl's palace, a hundred paces across the terrace, a single Spanish voice rose. He could not make out words from this distance, but he knew the cadence: a Latin litany, slow, clear, the way Fray Bernardo had recited above their Nahuatl lessons during the gilded months. The friar was naming something. Itzcoatl could not yet know that he was naming the dead, and naming them by the names he had learned during those lessons, *ixiptla* by *ixiptla*. He knew only that one Spanish voice in the palace had separated itself from the others, and that the other Spanish voices, now distantly audible through the precinct's stone, were not stopping their work.

He kept walking.

At the southern gate the corps was already forming in unawakened ranks. Pilots in Toxcatl whites, adorators in feast ash, two Tezcatlipoca seniors he recognized and one Huitzilopochtli captain he did not. No engine had been moved. No seal had been broken. The discipline that the day had not yet eaten was holding here, held by men whose authority to hold it had not been severed with the pipeline. Itzcoatl took his place in the pilli-class line and did not speak. The captain to his left, a Tezcatlipoca senior, ash-mark fresh, mouth a flat line, looked at his face for two breaths and looked away. That was the only acknowledgement either of them needed.

A messenger came in through the gate with a report from the eastern wards: the Spanish garrison had locked itself inside Axayacatl's palace; the city was beginning to surround it; the canals north of the precinct were filling with people moving in the wrong direction; Tlatelolco was barring its bridges. The Huitzilopochtli captain received the report without expression. Itzcoatl heard the word *Tlatelolco* and did not let his face do anything. His sister's compound was in Tlatelolco. The bridges being barred could mean she was inside them or outside them. It was not a question the muster point was here to answer.

The captain's voice, when it came, was the calmecac voice, old, level, instructional. Pilots would hold here. Engines would not be woken. The corps would await the council's decision. Anyone who broke ranks toward a stable would answer to the senior adorators. Anyone who broke ranks toward the precinct would answer to him.

Itzcoatl heard the order and felt, against his will, a small relief at the shape of it. The shape was the shape his life had been built to. Stand. Wait. Do not wake the deity until the priests of the deity say so. The day had broken every other thing in the city, and this one piece of his training the day had not yet reached.

Behind him the Great Engine hummed sour and held.

In front of him a Spanish garrison had locked itself inside Axayacatl's palace, and the city was beginning to surround it.

His sister's husband was already dead. He did not yet know.

The friar's litany went on.

Itzcoatl stood at the muster point and waited for an order he could obey.
